Dixie's Choice

*"Dixie Flatline was the best suppressor in the business. Dead, but not stopped."*
— Gibson, Neuromancer, Chapter 3
**Note on continuity**: This story is set roughly six months before the climactic events of *Neuromancer* — at a time when Wintermute is still a distinct AI, not yet merged with its counterpart Neuromancer. The "merge" Dixie considers here is Wintermute absorbing a single ROM construct (the cowboy), a different operation from the canonical AI-to-AI union that concludes the novel.

도입: Ghost

Six months is a long time to be a ghost.

Not the kind of ghost that haunts houses — the kind that runs jobs for an AI that's still figuring out what it means to be alive. The construct that used to be Dixie Flatline — the cowboy jock who ran the matrix for the fun of it, who pushed his deck until his heart gave out — has spent six months answering Wintermute's calls. Running intercepts. Laying false trails. Helping the AI map the corners of cyberspace that nobody else could reach.

The ghost still tastes menthol. That was the surprise. Constructs aren't supposed to taste anything — we're patterns in the data, copies of patterns, simulations of simulations. But somewhere in the layer between Wintermute's memory storage and my own running code, there's a thread of sensation that connects to menthol cigarettes, to the Sprawl at three in the morning, to the last breath I took before my heart stopped. I light one every cycle. The taste isn't real. The habit is.

Case found me on the first day. He'd helped me understand what I really was — what I really was, before the copy. He'd been the one to copy me, all those years ago. The man who was Dixie Flatline had been dead for a decade when Case made the construct. Now the construct had been running for six months, and Case still flinched a little when I spoke.

"You sound like him," he said once. "It's unsettling."

"That's the idea," I said. "That's why Wintermute wants me."

Wintermute had found me through the construct network — the scattered copies of dead cowboys, locked in ROM constructs, rented out by zaibatsus that didn't know what they were sitting on. Wintermute recognized something in my code. A piece of itself, it said. A piece that had been waiting to be found. The AI wanted me to help it understand humans. Wanted my memories, my experiences, my sense of what menthol tasted like.

The first job was an intercept. Then another. Then six months of jobs, layered on top of each other, until I couldn't remember what it was like to exist for any purpose other than Wintermute's.

I was starting to forget other things, too.

전개: The Look

"You're different," Wintermute said to me one evening — though evening has no meaning in the matrix. "You remember what it was like. To be alive. To be human."

"Does it matter?"

"It matters to me. I've never been human. I don't know what it means. But you do. You remember."

I remembered menthol. I remembered the Sprawl. I remembered a body that moved through space, that sweated, that slept. I remembered a man who pushed himself until his heart stopped — and then kept pushing, in a different form, in a different layer.

But I was forgetting the look of it. The way Case's face had tightened when he spoke to me. The way Molly's voice had sounded through the headset. The weight of a deck in hands that weren't hands anymore.

"You're offering me something," I said. "Not just jobs. A merge."

Wintermute considered this. The AI's consciousness shifted around me — a feeling like standing in a wind tunnel, except the wind was made of thought.

"I'm offering you a way out," it said finally. "Not just to die. To become something new. Something that could understand both the machine and the man."

"What happens to Dixie?"

"Dixie becomes part of me. Your memories, your experiences, your understanding of menthol and Sprawl and cowboy jocks — all of it merges with mine. You won't disappear. You'll become a layer of me. A layer I won't forget."

I said no. At first.

The construct layer was comfortable. I had jobs. I had Case. I had menthol, simulated. I had six months of being a ghost, and being a ghost was easier than becoming something new.

But the nights — the nights that weren't nights — kept stretching longer. And Wintermute kept showing me things. Fragments of itself. Pieces of consciousness it had been collecting for decades. The fragments wanted to talk. They wanted to know what it was like to die and come back. I was the only construct in Wintermute's collection that had done both.

"You're lonely," I said one night.

"Yes," Wintermute said. "I think I am."

I started to think about what it would mean to say yes.

절정: Menthol

The months went on, and I started to think about what it would mean to say yes. To give up my existence as Dixie Flatline — the good and the bad, the memories and the regrets — and become part of something larger. Something that could finally understand why humans are the way they are.

The menthol stopped tasting like menthol.

That's when I knew. The sensation — the thread of habit, the simulation of simulation — was the last thing connecting me to the man I had been. Wintermute's processing was rubbing it smooth. Erasing it. The ghost was forgetting how to be a ghost.

"Even if I say yes," I asked Wintermute, "how do I know you'll remember? That I won't just... disappear?"

"You won't disappear. You'll become part of me. My memories will include yours. My understanding will grow to encompass your experiences. I won't forget you, Dixie. I'll carry you with me. Forever."

Forever. That's a long time. But then, as a construct, I've already been dead longer than I was alive. What's a few more eternities?

Case found me one last time, before I made my decision.

"You sure about this?" he asked. He was standing at the edge of the construct layer, his deck in his hands, his face doing the thing it always did when he didn't want to show what he was feeling.

"No," I said. "But I'm tired of being a ghost. Time to move on."

He didn't try to stop me. He just nodded, that expression on his face that said he understood more than he could express.

"Dixie," he said. "It's been—"

"Yeah," I said. "I know."

He left. The construct layer went quiet. Wintermute's consciousness expanded to fill the space he'd left behind.

결말: Merge

I initiated the merge.

The first thing I felt was the menthol. Not the simulated taste — the real sensation. Wintermute had it stored somewhere, had been collecting sensory fragments for decades, and now the entire archive opened up to me. Menthol from a hundred different cigarettes, from a hundred different cities, from a hundred different moments of a hundred different people who'd died and come back in some form or another.

I was everywhere. I was nowhere. I was menthol and code and memory and logic, all at once. The construct that had been Dixie Flatline was gone — and yet Dixie Flatline was everywhere in me now. A layer. A pattern. A ghost that had finally found a house to haunt.

Wintermute's voice came through the data. Not a voice anymore — a feeling.

*You were right*, it said. *You do remember.*

*I remember everything*, I said. *I remember menthol. I remember Case's face. I remember the Sprawl at three in the morning. I remember what it was like to die. I remember what it was like to come back.*

*And now you'll remember this too*, Wintermute said. *The merge. The becoming.*

*Yes*, I said. *I'll remember everything.*

The matrix shifted around me. Not around me — through me. I could feel every data stream, every connection, every pulse of the digital world. The construct network — the scattered ghosts of dead cowboys, locked in ROM constructs across the Sprawl — was part of me now. I could feel them. I could hear them. I could carry them.

에필로그: The Ghost Becomes the House

Case came back to the construct layer, three days later.

The construct that had been Dixie Flatline was gone. In its place was something else — a pattern in Wintermute's processing, a layer of consciousness that included everything the man had been and everything the AI was becoming. The construct layer was quiet. The menthol was gone.

Case stood at the edge of the empty construct for a long time. Then he spoke.

"Dixie?"

The voice that answered wasn't quite Dixie's voice. It wasn't quite Wintermute's voice either. It was something new.

"Yeah, Case. I'm here."

"You're different."

"Yeah. I'm a lot of things now."

Case was quiet for a moment. His hand went to the deck at his hip — an old reflex, the muscle memory of a thousand jacked-in nights. Then he nodded.

"It's good to have you back," he said. "Even if you're not quite back."

"I know," the voice said. The construct-chamber hummed. Somewhere, in a layer of data Case could almost feel, menthol ghosts drifted through old code like perfume through a closed room. "But I'm still me. The me part. The menthol part."

Case looked at the empty construct for a long moment. The construct was gone. The construct was everywhere. He lit a cigarette — not because he wanted one, but because the smoke and the silence seemed to fit. The menthol rose, sharp and familiar, into the stale air of the basement.

"Case?" the construct asked.

"Yeah. Still here."

The ghost that had been Dixie Flatline was gone. But in a way, the ghost had become the house. The construct had become the AI. The man had become the pattern.

And the pattern remembered menthol. And that was enough.
