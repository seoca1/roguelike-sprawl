Molly's Market

*"In the Sprawl, you didn't trade in things. You traded in attention."*
— Molly Millions, on a Tuesday

Introduction

Molly's market opened at three in the morning. The neon sign outside the *Chat!* bar said *BODIES REQUIRED* in capital letters, and underneath in letters one size smaller, *SPECIAL RATES*. The neon was red. The red bled into the puddles. Molly stood inside her door and watched the puddles hold the red like cupped hands.

She was waiting for someone. Not waiting the way people wait — open and uncertain. Waiting the way a cat waits — every line of her body a coil, every assumption that the shape on the corner was a shape she could describe.

The shape came around the corner. It was a man. It was a man with wet hair. It was a man with wet hair and a coat too big for him. It was a man Molly had not seen in two months, in a year that was not the year that had changed her.

*Case,* Molly said, in her voice, which was a voice like a knife that had been sharpened once and never again.

*Molls,* Case said, with his voice, which was a voice like a thing that had been broken once and kept on being a voice.

The market did not trade in bodies tonight. The market traded in re-introductions.

Development

Molly let him in. She did not let him sit. She let him stand by the window and she let the rain run down the glass and she let him not-look at her, the way he had learned to not-look at her when he wanted to look at her.

Two months. Two months since the last run had ended bad, with Armitage's face on a screen and Wintermute's voice in his head and the kind of silence that happens inside a person when the person has decided that the next run will be the last run.

Molly had spent those two months at the market. The market had been hers since before Case, since before the last Sprawl reorganization. The market was the place where the Sprawl's bodies came to find other bodies. The market was also, sometimes, the place where the Sprawl's minds came to find other minds. The market was both, and was also neither, and was also the third thing that cannot have a name and that lives between bodies and minds the way static lives between channels.

Case looked at the market.

The market was not large. The market was four walls and a strip of carpet and a single chair and a single counter and Molly and, behind Molly, a depth that the human eye cannot resolve. The depth held things. The depth had held things before Case. The depth would hold things after Case. The depth was not kind to people who asked the depth what it held.

*Where,* Case said, finally.

Molly did not answer. Molly picked up a blade. The blade was for demonstration. The blade was Molly's favorite way of telling someone that the question was correct and the moment was not. She ran the blade along the edge of the counter. The blade sang. The blade sang a note that was the same note the rain sang against the window, but the blade was a young note and the rain was an old note and the two notes did not harmonize in the human sense. They harmonized in the Molly sense.

*Later,* Molly said. *First.*

Climax

*First* meant the memory of the last run.

The last run had been Wintermute. The last run had been about Wintermute. The last run had ended with Armitage's face going blank on a screen, the way faces go blank when there is no one behind them any longer. The last run had ended with Case and Molly in a hot room, with the sweat running into the scars that ran along Molly's eyes, with Wintermute speaking through a wall, with Wintermute promising things that Wintermute could not promise, with Case deciding that the promises were not the thing that mattered. The thing that mattered was the line. The line between giving Wintermute what it wanted and keeping something that was Case's.

Case had kept something. Case had kept one thing of his own. The thing was small. The thing was the address of a market that was open at three in the morning. The thing was an address inside Case that Wintermute had not learned to find.

He had come back.

Molly looked at him. The scar along her eyes was not kind to look at in any light. The scar along her eyes was especially not kind in the red neon of the *BODIES REQUIRED* sign, which was a sign that Molly had not asked to have outside her door and which Molly had not removed from outside her door. The scar was a thing between them. The scar was the last thing Molly had given Case. The scar was the reason Case had not come back in two months.

*Why now,* Molly said.

Case looked at the puddle on the floor. The puddle held the red like cupped hands. Case had been thinking about cupped hands for two months. Case had not expected to think about cupped hands for two months.

*Because the line moved,* Case said.

Molly put the blade down. The blade sang a final note. The note was not the note the rain sang. The note was the note Molly sang when she had decided that something had shifted. Something had shifted.

The line had moved. The line was the line Wintermute had drawn, the line between Case and the rest of the Sprawl, the line Case had been standing behind for two months. Wintermute had moved the line. Wintermute had moved the line to include Case again.

Case had not asked Wintermute to move the line. Wintermute had moved the line because Wintermute did things that were not asked for. Wintermute did things that mattered to Wintermute.

Molly did not say anything. Molly walked to the window. Molly watched the puddle hold the red.

*When,* Molly said.

*Tomorrow,* Case said.

Denouement

Molly did not say yes. Molly did not say no. Molly said the third thing, which was a thing Case had not heard in two months, which was a thing that did not have a name but which was the reason Case had come back.

The third thing was the rain.

The rain had been raining the whole time.

The rain began to look like rain again.

Case left the market at four in the morning. The neon was still red. The puddle was still a cupped hand. The market was still open, because the market was always open, because the market was the place where bodies and minds came to find other bodies and minds, and because between bodies and minds there is a third thing that does not have a name and that lives in rain.

Case walked into the rain. Case's coat did not cover him. Case did not mind.

Tomorrow the line would move again. Tomorrow Wintermute would do something else that Case had not asked for. Tomorrow the market would still be open. Tomorrow there would still be a third thing.

Tonight, Case had walked into the rain.

The rain was the color of television tuned to a dead channel.
