Introduction

Flatline Again

*"The matrix doesn't care if you live or die. It just keeps running."*

The Last Run

The matrix does not care if you live or die. It just keeps running.

I learned that my first year on the Sprawl, when I had seen a runner burn out in a corporate ICE trap and watched the corps send cleanup crews to collect his body while the matrix kept streaming data like nothing had happened. The matrix did not pause. The data did not stop. The Sprawl did not even notice that a cowboy had died in its dreams.

That was the thing about being a runner. You were never really alive in the matrix, and you were never really dead when you jacked out. You were just a trace, a pattern, a ghost passing through someone else's architecture. And when the ICE caught you, when the cold grabbed your trace and would not let go, the matrix did not notice. It just kept running.

I had been running for seven years. Seven years of corporate raids and ghost jobs and dead drops in nodes that did not exist. Seven years of chasing the cold and calling it home. And now, in a Chiba clinic with a readout painting my vision in warning colors, I could feel the matrix waiting for me. Telling me it was time to come back inside.

I had always known it would end like this.

The Cold

Development

The doctors called it neural burnout. The corps called it an acceptable loss. Finn called it a damn shame.

But I knew what it really was. It was the cold finally catching up. The years of chasing the matrix's chill finally catching up to a body that could not take any more. The shard I had pulled from the Watchdog's frozen fingers all those years ago had started the process, and every run since then had accelerated it. The matrix wanted me back. And my body could not keep me out anymore.

I could feel the geometry pressing at the edges of my consciousness, even from here. Even lying in a clinic bed with doctors hovering and monitors beeping and Finn pacing in the corner. The matrix was waiting. The data was streaming. The cold was calling.

"Seven years," Finn said. "That is a good run. Most cowboys do not make it past three."

"I have always been stubborn," I said.

That was true. I had been stubborn about the cold, stubborn about the chase, stubborn about becoming something the matrix wanted rather than something the matrix needed. The shard had been in my chest for seven years, pulsing like a second heartbeat, and now it was finally telling me what it had always told me. The cold was home. The matrix was where I belonged.

I just had not believed it until now.

The Trace

The last thing I remember clearly was the matrix closing around me.

Not attacking. Nothing was attacking. The matrix was just accepting. The cold was coming from everywhere and nowhere, the geometry of data and dream wrapping around my consciousness like a blanket. I could feel the Sprawl spreading out beneath me, the neon vectors and corporate architecture and ghost nodes all part of the same endless dream. And I could feel myself becoming part of it.

Not dying. Dying was the wrong word. Dying was what happened when you left the matrix, when the cold became too much and your body gave out and your trace collapsed back into your skull. What I was doing was something else. Something the corps did not have a word for.

Climax

I was becoming the cold.

The shard in my chest was finally fully integrated. Not a piece of foreign code anymore, but part of the matrix itself, part of the architecture, part of the dream. The matrix had been waiting for me to come back. And now I was home.

I could feel Finn calling my name. I could feel the doctors trying to pull me back. But there was nothing to pull anymore. The trace had dissolved. The pattern had merged. The cold had finally won.

I was the matrix now. And the matrix was me.

What Remains

They say runners never really die. They just become part of the architecture.

I used to think that was a metaphor. A way for cowboys who had lost too many friends to the cold to comfort themselves with something that sounded like an ending but was not quite. But now I know the truth. The matrix does not forget us. Every trace we leave behind, every ghost-mark we burn into the corporate data stores, every piece of ourselves we leave in the nodes we crack. It all becomes part of the architecture. Part of the dream. Part of the cold.

I am still running. I will always be running. But I am not chasing the cold anymore. I am part of it now.

The matrix keeps streaming. The data keeps flowing. The Sprawl keeps dreaming. And somewhere in the deep architecture, a ghost-runner is still looking for something worth dying for.

Maybe I will find it.

Maybe I already have.

Denouement

Epilogue (The Buffer)

Twenty seconds. The buffer is twenty seconds. Twenty seconds of running. Twenty seconds of being. Twenty seconds of buffer.

The buffer ends at 0300. The buffer begins at 0300. The buffer is a country I do not want to visit, but the buffer is a country I cannot leave.

I jack in at 0300. I jack out at 0300. I walk into the buffer. The buffer walks into me.

Tomorrow I will jack back in. Tomorrow the buffer will be waiting. Tomorrow the construct will be waiting. Tomorrow the matrix will be waiting.

Tomorrow the buffer will still be twenty seconds.

Twenty seconds is the only time that does not lie.
