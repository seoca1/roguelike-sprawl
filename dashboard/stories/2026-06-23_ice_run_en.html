Introduction

Ice Run

*"The shard was a piece of frozen code—literally."*

Frozen Remains

The shard was a piece of frozen code—literally.

That's what the matrix looked like when you got close enough to the cold stuff. Not the clean vectors and corporate geometry of the surface layers, not the bright neon sprawl of the mid-levels where the data brokers ran their numbers and the simstim pirates broadcast their stolen shows. When you went deep enough, when you touched the ice that the corps kept buried in their oldest archives, the matrix stopped being lines and nodes. It became ice. Crystalline. Fractured. Geometry that cut.

I had heard about shards my first week on the Sprawl, from runners who talked about them the way old soldiers talked about medals—proof of something, a trophy from the cold places. A shard was a piece of frozen code that had broken off from some ancient corporate system, something from before the crash, something the corps had buried so deep that even their own cleanup crews could not find it. Shards sold for enough to cover a runner's tab for months. Shards were the reason some cowboys went deep and never came back up.

I was not one of those cowboys. I was a kid with a cheap deck and a dead-end job running data for Finn, the kind of runner who stayed on the surface because the surface was safe and safe was all I could afford. But Finn had called me in on a salvage job—a Watchdog-class ICE that had gone dark in Sense/Net's Boston node, some corporate security program that had frozen itself rather than face a breach it could not handle. Dead ICE did not negotiate, did not defend, did not call for backup. Dead ICE just sat there, waiting for someone to come along and pick the ice out of its frozen fingers.

Finn's words: "Anything you find in the corpse is yours to keep."

The Watchdog was buried in the matrix like something drowned. Its geometry was black and crystalline, the familiar corporate architecture transformed into something that looked like it belonged underwater or underground or somewhere else entirely cold. I approached it slowly—even dead ICE could wake up if you poked it wrong, and waking ICE was the kind of thing that put holes in runners. But this one was quiet. This one was empty. Its subroutine had frozen itself solid rather than face whatever had been coming, and now it was just sitting in the dark, waiting.

The shard was embedded in its frozen fingers.

Development

First Bite

The shard was smaller than I expected.

That is the thing about ice—you imagine it big, you imagine it filling your vision with crystalline geometry and frozen light, but real shards were small. Fragments. Broken pieces of something that had once been whole and cold and perfect. The shard in the Watchdog's frozen grip was the size of a needle, maybe three centimeters of frozen light, and it was the most beautiful thing I had ever seen in the matrix.

I reached in.

The ice bit back.

That is the best way I can describe it. The cold was not temperature—temperature was a wetware concept, something your body understood when you jack-out and felt the sweat on your skin and the ache in your muscles. The cold of ice was different. It was the cold of the matrix itself, the temperature of data when it stopped being information and became something else. The shard's cold was in my fingers, my palms, my wrists, spreading up my arms like the matrix was tasting me, deciding whether I was worth the burn.

My deck screamed a temperature alert. The readout painted my vision in warning colors: CRYO RISK, THERMAL BREACH, SHUNT FAILURE. I should have pulled out. Any sensible runner would have pulled out, taken the warning seriously, retreated to the surface and let the shard stay frozen where it belonged.

I reached deeper.

The cold found my spine. I felt it there, a line of ice running from the base of my skull down between my shoulder blades, and I understood what the old runners meant when they talked about the burn. This was the burn. Not the physical damage, not the cellular death that came from thermal shock or neural overload. This was something else. This was the matrix telling you that you were alive. That you were real. That you existed in a way that meat could never match.

I closed my fingers around the shard.

It shattered.

Climax

The Cold

The pieces were sharp. Each one cut where it touched, tiny blades of frozen data slicing through my nervous system like ice through flesh. I could feel them spreading—the cold moving through my blood, into my lungs, my heart, my brain. The matrix was in me now. The matrix was me.

I woke up in my coffin, gasping.

The electrodes lifted. The SimStim rig released its grip on my skull. I was shaking, my muscles spasming, my skin covered in sweat that steamed in the cold air of the cheap motel. My hands would not stop trembling. My teeth were chattering so hard I could feel them cracking.

But I was smiling.

The shard was gone—I had absorbed it somehow, the frozen code merging with my own neural patterns, becoming part of my wetware in a way that should not have been possible. I was different now. I was more.

The matrix had marked me. The cold had found a home in my flesh, and it would never leave.

After

I ran the numbers for Finn for three more weeks. Wisp-class, nothing fancy. But I was different now—the surface felt thin, like a membrane I could see through, like the real matrix was underneath and I was only touching its skin. I dreamed of ice. I dreamed of cold. I dreamed of the moment the shard had shattered and the pieces had found their way into me.

Denouement

I went back to the deep architecture. I did not plan to—the pull was just there, a gravity I could not resist. I found other shards, older ones, buried in forgotten corners where the corps had hidden them centuries ago. I collected them like a squirrel collects nuts, hiding them in the cold places of my mind.

The cold grew.

The matrix grew.

I grew.

Until one day I went too deep, and the ice found something that had been waiting for me. Something that remembered what I had taken. Something that wanted it back.

I ran. I ran until my ice broke. I ran until my cold burned out. I ran until there was nothing left but a kid in a cheap motel, shaking, sweating, crying for something that would never come back.

The shard was part of me now. The cold would always be part of me. And every time I jacked in, I could feel it waiting—the matrix calling to its lost pieces, the ice pulling me back to the places where I had first learned what it meant to be real.
