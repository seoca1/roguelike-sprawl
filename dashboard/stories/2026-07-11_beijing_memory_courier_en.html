Beijing Memory Courier

Sprawl #3 (test) · "2026-07-11" · EN

Introduction

The rain tastes like burnt copper when I step out of the Chiba Street Market, the Ono-Sendai export seal heavy in my jacket's inner pocket, wrapped twice in static-proof mylar that crackles against my ribs with each breath. Four hundred and eighty thousand yuan for a twenty-four-hour run to Tessier-Ashpool orbit—more than I've made in six months of courier work combined. The fixer who hired me had chrome teeth and didn't smile. "Do-not-open-until-delivery," he said, sliding the package across rain-slick carbon fiber. "Window closes at 03:00 tomorrow, Beijing time. Miss it, and the construct degrades."

I'm twenty-two and I've never left atmosphere.

The seal hums against my chest as I navigate the night market's neon sprawl, past vendors selling bootleg Hosaka decks and gray-market simstim chips that smell like ozone and burnt plastic. My fingers keep touching the package through the jacket, feeling its warmth—the Ono-Sendai signature, that particular heat that means active storage, live data cycling in its quantum loops.

I should have taken it straight to the shuttle port.

Instead, I'm in my capsule hotel room in Kowloon, the seal open on the fold-down desk, my hands shaking. The export wrapper peeled back clean, no alarms, no corporate screaming—because I needed to know what was worth half a million yuan and an orbital delivery with a twenty-four-hour window. The fragment sits there, smaller than my thumb, its surface catching the room's LED strip in rainbow oil-slick patterns.

I jack it into my pocket deck, a secondhand Hosaka I bought in Chiba three years ago when I started running.

The fragment loads.

It's not corporate data.

It's a memory construct—personal, not commercial. The metadata scrolls across my deck's cracked screen: SUBJECT_IDENTITY: Lin Mei-Hua. DATE_CAPTURE: 2056-03-14. LOCATION: Freeside_Straylight_Medical_Cycle_7.

Development

My mother's name.

My mother, who died fourteen years ago in a Straylight medical cycle, one of those corporate test programs that promised regeneration and delivered dissolution. I was eight. They sent me a form letter and a box of her things that smelled like antiseptic and something else, something sweet and wrong that I later learned was the smell of memory extraction gone bad.

The construct begins to play without my permission, flooding my deck's buffer, and then I'm not in the capsule hotel anymore.

I'm standing in a white room that doesn't exist, watching my mother sit on a bed that was real once, in Freeside, in the Tessier-Ashpool medical wing where they took her apart looking for whatever made her valuable to them. She's younger than I am now—twenty, maybe twenty-one. Her hair is short, black, wet from a shower. The room smells like recycled air and the particular plastic scent of orbital habitats, that off-gassing that never quite stops.

She's talking to someone I can't see.

No—she's talking to a camera. To me.

"I don't know if you'll ever see this," she says. Her voice is exactly as I remember it, and I realize I'd started to forget, that the edges had been going soft. "They're starting the cycle tomorrow. Eighty percent success rate, they say. Twenty percent chance I don't come back." She touches her stomach, and I understand—I'm there, inside her, a month old, maybe two. "I keep thinking about what kind of world I'm giving you. What kind of world gives itself to Tessier-Ashpool and asks to be remembered by machines."

The construct flickers. Corruption at the edges, data rot setting in. When it stabilizes, she's crying.

"They offered me two million yuan for the cycle. For my memories, my personality, everything that makes me me. They said they're building something, a new kind of AI that needs human templates. Real humans, not constructs. They said I'd be helping the world remember itself." She laughs, and it's bitter. "But you—you're the only thing I want to remember. And after tomorrow, if the twenty percent takes me, you'll have nothing. No mother. Just data somewhere in Straylight's banks, teaching machines how to feel."

The construct cuts to black.

Climax

New text scrolls: EMBEDDED_FILE_DETECTED. CORPORATE_TRIAL_RECORDS. TESSIER-ASHPOOL_ILLEGAL_EXTRACTION_PROGRAM_2056-2058. EVIDENCE_PACKAGE_COMPLETE.

So that's what this is. Someone inside Tessier-Ashpool is burning the company. My mother's memory is the proof—evidence of illegal extraction, of buying bodies and minds from desperate people in the Sprawl and the orbital slums, feeding them into some vast project that the corps never disclosed. The memory is the scandal. The memory is the weapon.

And I'm the courier.

At 02:47 Beijing time, I'm in a Hostel terminal three blocks from my capsule, jacked into the matrix with the Ono-Sendai seal in my left hand and a corporate uplink address burning in my vision. The matrix unfolds around me in familiar geometries—ice towers and data streams, the vast neon architecture of global information rendered in colors that don't exist in meatspace. I can see the Tessier-Ashpool orbital node from here, a black pyramid wrapped in red security lattice, waiting for my delivery.

Fifteen minutes until the shuttle lifts. Thirteen minutes until the construct's window closes and the data degrades into nothing.

I could deliver it. Plug the fragment into the uplink, take my payment, watch as the scandal tears through Tessier-Ashpool's corporate structure like a virus through wetware. My mother's memory would become evidence, exhibit A in some massive trial that might actually change something, might actually make the corps think twice before they buy and sell human consciousness like it's just another commodity.

Or I could burn it. Wipe the fragment, erase the seal, keep my mother's last words for myself. Let Tessier-Ashpool keep its secrets. Let the world keep forgetting.

The construct flickers in my hand, warming. Active data, degrading with each passing second.

Denouement

I think about what she said—about giving a child to a world that has begun to remember itself. But the world doesn't remember. The world is corporate ice and stolen memories and people like me running packages we don't understand for money we desperately need. The world is my mother dying in a Straylight cycle so I could eat, so I could survive, so I could grow up to deliver the proof of her murder to the people who bought her.

Just then, I understand: she knew. She knew they'd take everything. She knew I'd be here someday, holding her last words, deciding what they mean.

The uplink pulses. Five minutes.

I jack the fragment into the matrix.

The data streams toward the Tessier-Ashpool node, carrying my mother's voice into the architecture of corporate memory. I watch it go, a bright thread in the neon dark, and I think maybe this is what she wanted—not to be remembered by machines, but to be the memory that makes them forget how to lie.

The payment hits my account at 03:01. Four hundred and eighty thousand yuan, plus a bonus I didn't expect. The fragment's window closes at 03:00 exactly, the data locked into Tessier-Ashpool's evidence servers, irretrievable.

I jack out. The Hostel terminal smells like sweat and burnt coffee. My hands are steady.

Six months later, the trial starts. I'm in Chiba by then, running bigger packages, better money. Sometimes I see her face on the newsfeeds—younger than I remember, talking to a camera about the world and what we give it. They call her a victim. They call her a hero.

I call her Mom, and finally, I remember.
