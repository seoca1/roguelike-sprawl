Maas Biolabs Heist

*"The Sprawl has a long memory. The Sprawl keeps things in basements."*
— Gibson, Neuromancer, Chapter 7

도입: The Door

Maas Biolabs had a warehouse in the Sprawl that no one talked about.

The official address on the corporate registry was a post box in Zurich. The real address was three levels down from a noodle shop in the Narita corridor, behind a door that didn't look like a door. The kind of door you walk past three times before you notice it. The kind of door that has a small sign in three languages saying EMPLOYEES ONLY, in handwriting that has been the same since 1987.

The sign was a joke, of course. Maas Biolabs didn't have employees. They had constructs — biological constructs, the kind that walked and talked and remembered being human but weren't, not anymore, not since the wetware had finished integrating. The constructs worked the warehouse. The constructs loaded the trucks. The constructs asked no questions because the parts of their brains that asked questions had been replaced with parts that loaded trucks.

The noodle shop upstairs was a real noodle shop. The old woman who ran it had been there since before the war. She didn't know what was below her, or she pretended not to know, and the Finn had paid her enough over the years to keep the pretense going. K had eaten there twice — once on a job three years ago, once tonight, waiting for the security shift to rotate.

The shift changed at 0400.

K had been hired to break in at 0300, copy the biochip specs, and get out before the new shift arrived. The job paid fifteen thousand credits — twice what the Finn usually offered. Twice meant dangerous. Twice meant Maas wanted the specs back before their competitors could reverse-engineer them. Twice meant the ICE inside the warehouse was not corporate-standard.

Twice meant the constructs Maas kept in the basement had names.

The Finn hadn't said what names. The Finn had just said: be back by 0400. The constructs are not pets. The constructs are not people. The constructs are Maas Biolabs constructs, and you don't want to meet one in the dark.

전개: The Warehouse

K jacked in at 0300.

The warehouse was colder than the data had suggested — colder, and quieter, and smelling faintly of antiseptic and something older. The smell of a place that had been kept at 4°C for too long. The smell of meat that had forgotten it was once an animal.

K's deck hummed against his hip. The Ono-Sendai electrodes settled into the familiar grooves of his skull. The Sprawl's noise — the street sounds, the noodle shop's clatter, the soft pulse of the building's old air conditioning — fell away. The matrix materialized around him.

The warehouse's data architecture was old. Not Sprawl-era old — *old* old. The kind of architecture that hadn't been updated since the late 80s, when Maas Biolabs had been called something else and had done something else. The data felt like walking through a museum after closing. Everything was in its place. Nothing was alive.

The biochip specs were in a node four levels deep, behind a Watchdog that had been reprogrammed for biological threats. Watchdogs were the kind of ICE that could distinguish between a person walking through data and a virus replicating through data. The reprogrammed version could also distinguish between a person and a biological construct — which meant K had to be very careful about how he moved.

K had bought a Yakuza program three weeks ago, from a code-house in Freeside. The program was called something unpronounceable in Japanese. It worked by mimicking the wetware signature of a Maas Biolabs construct — tricking the Watchdog into thinking K was one of its own. K had tested the program against Maas's public-facing servers in Zurich. It worked.

The program worked in the warehouse too. The Watchdog didn't even register K's approach. It stood at the edge of the node like a sleeping dog, and K walked past it like a thing that had always belonged there.

The data was in his deck at 0315.

절정: The Construct

K started the jack-out sequence.

The construct appeared at 0318.

It didn't have a face. It didn't have a name that K could read. It had the geometry of a Maas Biolabs prototype — the kind of thing that lived in basements and didn't ask permission. Its outline was humanoid but slightly wrong, the way a reflection in a funhouse mirror is humanoid but slightly wrong. It stood in the data stream between K and the exit node, and it watched him with the patient attention of something that had been waiting for a long time.

K didn't name it. He didn't talk to it. Maas constructs weren't supposed to be talking — they were working constructs, biological machines, the kind of thing you assigned a job to and forgot about. They didn't have names. They didn't have personalities. They didn't have the parts of themselves that would have made them curious about a stranger in their warehouse at 0318.

But this one was curious.

It tilted its head — a gesture so human that K felt something in his chest tighten. The construct's outline shifted, suggesting a smile that wasn't quite a smile. The construct was reading him. Reading his trace. Reading the Yakuza program that was pretending to be one of them.

The construct reached out a hand. The hand was made of data — K could see the code that composed it, the wetware signature, the fragments of personality that had once belonged to someone who had worked at Maas Biolabs and had made a bad deal. The hand reached toward K's trace, and K could feel the moment when it realized the trace wasn't one of them.

The construct paused. The construct considered. The construct did something that K would think about for the rest of his life.

The construct let him go.

K jack out at 0319. He was in the noodle shop, paying for a bowl he wouldn't eat, and the biochip specs were in his deck. The data was clean. The ICE didn't follow him. The constructs didn't follow him.

결말: The Basement

K was back at the Finn's office by 0330. The Finn paid him in cash. The Finn didn't ask about the construct.

Some things are better not named. Some things are better not asked about. The Sprawl has a long memory, and the Sprawl keeps things in basements, and the things in the basements don't like to be disturbed.

But some of them, sometimes, let you go.

K walked out of the Finn's office into the Sprawl's late-night rain. The data was in his deck. The construct was in the basement. The door that didn't look like a door was still closed, somewhere in the Narita corridor, three levels down from a noodle shop that didn't know what was underneath it.

K didn't go back. The Finn wouldn't hire him for another Maas job — the Finn never hired the same runner twice for the same corporate, on principle, because corporations noticed patterns. But K knew, as he walked through the rain toward his coffin hotel in the 11th level, that he would think about the construct sometimes. About the head-tilt. About the smile that wasn't quite a smile. About the hand that had reached out and decided, at the last moment, not to touch him.

Some things in the Sprawl are kept in basements because they shouldn't exist.

Some things in the Sprawl are kept in basements because they're too kind to be let out.

K didn't know which one the construct was. He didn't want to know. He just wanted to get paid, and stay alive, and not think about the constructs that had names.

The Sprawl has a long memory. The Sprawl keeps things in basements.

K walked on.
