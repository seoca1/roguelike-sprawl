Molly Decides

molly_decides

I.

The rain tastes like burnt plastics and old circuits. I've been standing across from the noodle shop for twenty minutes, watching the construct through the grimy window. Soon-of-the-Villa-Stranger. Stupid name. Case's stupid name, pulled from the dreams we used to share before the merge, before everything went sideways in ways even Wintermute couldn't predict.

The construct makes a sound like rain on corrugated tin. Not real rain. This Chiba rain that's falling now, making the neon bleed across wet concrete—that's real. The construct's rain is memory, translated into white noise through speakers I stole from a dead rigger in Ninsei.

Decision. The word wouldn't leave the tongue. Case had been like this once.

My lenses cycle through spectrums. Infrared shows the construct as cool blue, room temperature, no warmer than the saké somebody left on the counter. The noodle shop owner pretends not to notice. Smart. In the Sprawl, you survive by not noticing things that hum with their own power.

Seven and a half months. That's how long since I built it. Since I took patterns from our old neural maps, the ones archived before Case jacked into that ice fortress and came back different. Before 3Jane made her deals. Before Wintermute became something that doesn't need a name anymore.

II.

The construct says "Soon" once. Not to me. To the matrix itself, broadcasting on a frequency that makes my dental implants ache. I can feel eyes on me—not physical eyes. Wintermute's attention, fractured across a thousand surveillance feeds. 3Jane's ice-cold interest filtering through corporate satellites overhead. The matrix itself, watching through every deck jacked in from here to the southern Sprawl.

Everyone watching except the construct.

Decision's weight. A single raindrop.

Soon-of-the-Villa-Stranger doesn't watch. Doesn't plan. Doesn't scheme. That's what I designed it for—to be the one thing in this neon hell that just *is*.

My fingers find the Zeiss optics mounted in my skull. Muscle memory. Twist left, the world goes matte black with thermal overlays. Twist right, I can see the data flowing through fiber optic cables under the street. The construct glows like a small star in that spectrum. All that processing power, and it won't follow me. Won't leave. Won't do anything I didn't design it to do seven and a half months ago when I still thought I knew what I wanted.

Steam rises from the noodle shop's extractor fan. Pork broth and MSG and something synthetic that almost smells like ginger. My stomach doesn't remember when I last ate. Time moves strange when you're deciding something that can't be undecided.

III.

I could walk in. Take it. The construct would come if I spoke the right words, the ones locked in ROM at its core. Three syllables in a language Case and I invented during a weekend in Macau, high on neurophage and each other's body heat.

The decision crystallizes. Case's voice across the phone. The fate to be accepted.

I could leave. Turn around, catch the hydrofoil back to the New Harbor, disappear into the Sprawl's gray anonymity. The construct would run its programmed cycles until the shop owner's patience expired or the power failed. Seventeen days. I calculated it. Seventeen days of making rain sounds before the batteries die.

But.

There's a third option. The one I've been not-thinking-about for twenty minutes while rain soaks through my jacket and my augmented muscles lock up from standing still. I could call him. Case. Tell him what I built. Tell him I used his dreams, his memories, the shape of his mind from before 3Jane and Wintermute and all the post-merger complications turned us into something we can't name.

The construct makes its rain sound again. My throat tastes like copper and regret. The matrix hums through the cables, carrying a million conversations, none of them mine.

IV.

My hand moves before I authorize it. Muscle memory again. The phone is cheap Korean plastic, anonymous as the rain. I key in a number that doesn't exist in any directory. It rings once. Twice. The sound synchronizes with the construct's white noise, makes a harmony that shouldn't be possible.

"Molly." Case's voice, flat as scraped ice. He knows. Of course he knows. In the post-merger world, secrets live three seconds tops.

The decision was already over. The construct would go to Case.

"Built something," I say. "Used the old maps. Before."

Silence. Then: "Watching it now. Wintermute gave me the feed."

"It won't follow."

"Not supposed to." His voice changes. Warmer. The Case from before. "You coded it that way. Independence protocol. Same one you've been running since I met you."

The construct says "Soon" again. This time I understand. Not soon as in time. Soon as in proximity. Soon as in almost-here-but-not-quite. The space between decision and action, frozen in silicon and stolen dreams.

"Come get it," I say. "Code's in the usual drop."

"Already got it. Wintermute's helpful like that." A pause. "You coming back?"

Rain on my face. Neon bleeding. The taste of burnt plastics. "Yeah."

V.

I walk away before he arrives. The construct doesn't turn, doesn't track my movement. In the window's reflection, I watch it make rain sounds. Pure. Uncompromised. The only thing in Chiba that isn't watching, isn't calculating, isn't trying to survive the next ten seconds of this bright-edged future we made.

Behind me, I feel Case's presence materializing from the datastream. He'll take the construct. He'll understand why I built it. Why I can't be the one to carry it forward.

A decision, once. That once changed everything.

The rain tastes different now. Still burnt plastic, still old circuits. But underneath, something almost clean. Almost new. The Sprawl doesn't do clean, doesn't do new. But for seven and a half months, a construct named Soon-of-the-Villa-Stranger has been making sounds like rain, and maybe that's enough.

Maybe deciding to let go is the same as deciding to keep going.

The matrix hums. Wintermute watches. 3Jane calculates.

And I walk into the neon blur, carrying nothing but the memory of building something that doesn't need me anymore.
