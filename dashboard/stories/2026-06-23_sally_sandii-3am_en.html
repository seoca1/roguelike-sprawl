The New Rose Hotel, 3 AM

*"Cyberspace is always awake. The Sprawl is the shape of that wakefulness."*
— common room saying

Introduction

It was three in the morning in the New Rose Hotel.

The New Rose was a hotel in the sense that a bleeding organ was an organ. The New Rose was four walls of chipped permadamp, painted in the Sprawl's favorite color — the color of a TV tuned to a dead channel — and lit from the inside with the kind of light that has forgotten what it was supposed to show. The light in the New Rose was not light. The light in the New Rose was the negative image of light, the shape light left behind when light was somewhere else.

The desk clerk sat behind a counter that had once been wood and was now an idea. The desk clerk was small. The desk clerk was a woman. The desk clerk was a woman who had been small for so long that smallness had become her surname.

*Yes?* the desk clerk said.

*Yes,* the figure in the lobby said, and the figure was a girl, and the girl was young, and the young girl's hair was the kind of hair that had been dyed every color in the Sprawl's hair index and was now settling on white.

The girl put three keys on the counter. The keys were not normal keys. The keys were keys to three different rooms. The keys were keys to three different rooms that did not connect to each other.

*Triple,* the desk clerk said.

The girl's name was not yet Molly. The girl's name was not yet Molly because Molly was a name you earned, the way you earned a scar or a sentence, and the girl had earned neither.

*Triple,* the girl said.

Development

Upstairs.

The three rooms were 9-A, 9-B, and 9-C. 9-A was the room the girl had booked. 9-B was the room the girl had booked for the man with the wet coat. 9-C was the room the girl had booked for the man in the second wet coat.

The girl's three rooms were not adjacent. The girl's three rooms were on three floors that did not share the same elevator shaft. The girl's three rooms were a triangle.

The girl put the three keys into her pocket. The keys clinked against each other. The girl had not intended for the keys to clink. The clink was a thing the keys did. The clink was the sound the keys did when they were being a girl.

In 9-A, the girl sat on the bed that had been made by a machine that had been told the bed was for sleeping. The girl did not sleep. The girl took out a card. The card was not a credit card. The card was not a SIM card. The card was a card of a kind that the Sprawl had stopped manufacturing ten years before, when the new design had eaten the old design's customers. The card was a tape cassette, a tape cassette the size of a fingernail. The tape cassette held a recording that the girl had cut six hours ago from a longer recording. The girl had been cutting for six hours. The girl cut for forty-five more.

The recording was a copy of a copy of a copy. The recording was eight generations removed from the original voice. The recording was a voice so many generations removed that the girl was no longer sure what the voice had originally said. The recording was the most powerful thing the girl had ever held. The recording was a recording of a man named Hans, the man who had killed the girl once and who had not been killed back. The recording was a recording of Hans saying something Hans had not said. The recording was something the girl had put into Hans's mouth the way a writer puts words into a character's mouth, the way a hacker puts a worm into a worm's mouth.

The girl finished cutting. The girl put the cassette into her pocket. The cassette sat next to the three keys.

In 9-B and 9-C, two men slept the way the New Rose's rooms allowed sleeping. They slept the way taxidermy animals slept. They slept the way women who had been hired for sleeping slept.

The girl went down. The girl went down the elevator. The elevator was small. The elevator was smaller than elevators had a right to be.

Climax

The girl had been planning this plan for forty-five hours.

The forty-five hours began when the girl had walked past the Wet Whisper Bar in the Ninsei entertainment district and had seen, on a wet wall, the silhouette of a man she had once known. The man was not yet dead. The man was not yet the man who was to die. The man was just a silhouette against a wet wall in a part of the Sprawl where silhouettes were how the city kept its census.

The man in silhouette had made the girl feel, for the first time in the time the girl had been a girl rather than a name she had not yet earned, the girl had felt the third thing. The third thing was not a name. The third thing was a fact.

The man was Hans.

The girl had walked into the Wet Whisper and the girl had walked out of the Wet Whisper having done what the third thing was. The girl had not needed to do very much. The third thing was that the girl had not needed to do very much.

Below the elevator, the lobby had grown. The desk clerk had grown. The keys had grown. The keys to 9-B and 9-C had grown into rooms. The rooms had grown into 9-A, 9-B, and 9-C. The girl was in 9-A. The girl was in 9-A and the cassette was in her pocket and the men in 9-B and 9-C were going to wake up.

Then the second thing happened. The second thing was different from the third thing. The second thing was the man with the wet coat walking into the lobby. The man with the wet coat was the man whose voice was on the cassette. The man with the wet coat was the man who had not yet spoken the words on the cassette. The man with the wet coat was Hans.

Hans saw the girl. The girl saw Hans. The third thing was that they both saw the same thing.

They both saw that the lobby was empty. They both saw that the desk clerk was asleep.

Hans walked past the girl. Hans walked past the girl as if the girl were a wall. Hans walked past the girl as if the girl had always been a wall and was not, in fact, a girl.

Denouement

The girl let Hans walk past her.

The girl was not yet Molly. The girl was not yet Molly because Molly was what the girl became in the next eleven minutes, after the cassette had been given, after the words on the cassette had been spoken through Hans's mouth by the woman Hans had hired, after the man Hans had hired had brought the woman Hans had hired past Hans's door, after the man Hans had hired had begun to open the door of 9-A, after the girl had become the woman the girl would become.

Eleven minutes later, the man in the second wet coat walked into the lobby. The second wet coat had not been wet. The second wet coat had been wet the way a man is wet who has walked through rain. The second wet coat walked into the lobby and the lobby was empty. The second wet coat walked into the lobby because the lobby was empty. The lobby was empty because the desk clerk was asleep.

At five in the morning, Molly walked out of the New Rose Hotel. The New Rose's light was the negative image of light. The New Rose's light had not changed. The New Rose's light had been the same. The New Rose's light would be the same forever.

Molly walked out of the New Rose. Molly walked into the rain. The rain had not changed. The rain had been the same. The rain would be the same forever.

Molly's pocket had a cassette. Molly's pocket had keys. Molly's pocket had a scar that had been a scar longer than Molly had been Molly.

Somewhere in the Sprawl, Hans was already dead. Hans would never know how Hans died. Hans would never know that Molly's cassette had been what made Hans speak the words Hans did not say. Hans would never know that the cassette had been a cassette longer than the cassette had been a cassette.

Molly did not feel like dying. Molly did feel like rain. The rain began to look like rain again.
