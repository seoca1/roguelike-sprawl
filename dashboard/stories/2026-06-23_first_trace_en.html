Introduction

*"The sky above the port was the color of television, tuned to a dead channel."*
— Gibson, Neuromancer, Chapter 1

The Finn's office smelled like burnt synth-coffee and old RAM. I'd been jacked in for six hours straight, burning my eyes against the pale geometry of Chiba's corporate sublayers, when he finally slid a credstick across the desk.

"Trace job," he said. His voice was the color of money—green and cold. "Someone's been talking to the wrong people."

I looked at the stick. Fifty thousand. Enough to buy me six months of the good juice, the stuff that made my nervous system feel like it was full of warm honey instead of static.

"Who's the target?"

"Name's Yoshida. Mid-level icebreaker for T-A subsidiary in Fukuoka. He's been moving data to a drop point in Istanbul." The Finn's eyes were flat black screens, reflecting nothing. "I need to know who he's meeting when he jacks in."

"That's a trace," I said. "Not a crack. You're not paying crack rates for a trace."

"I'm paying for speed." He pushed a second stick across. "Three days. Maybe less."

I took both sticks. The weight of them felt like a promise—or a chain. The Finn had a way of making every job feel like the first step toward something larger, something I couldn't see yet.

The target was ghost in the matrix. Yoshida moved through corporate ice like a fish through dark water, leaving almost no signature. I spent two days just mapping his patterns, watching him jack in from his apartment in Fukuoka at exactly 3 AM local, stay for four hours, jack out.

He was consistent. Professional. And that consistency was the first thing that told me something was wrong.

A good icebreaker doesn't keep the same schedule. A good icebreaker knows that patterns are how you get caught.

On the third night, I followed him into the deep architecture. We went past the corporate ice, past the public sublayers, down into the black space where the older systems lived—the pre-corporate architecture that nobody bothered to maintain anymore because there was no money in it.

Yoshida stopped at a node I didn't recognize. It wasn't T-A architecture. It wasn't any architecture I'd ever seen.

And then I saw what he was doing.

He wasn't just moving data. He was building something—a kind of dead drop that existed in the space between systems, invisible to normal tracing. It was clever work, elegant in a way that reminded me of Molly's knife, precise and brutal.

I traced the connection. Istanbul, just like the Finn said. But there was a second node, buried deeper, routing through three different corporate systems before landing at a final address.

L5.

Tessier-Ashpool's orbital habitat. The place where everything in the Sprawl ultimately traced back to, like blood returning to the heart.

I pulled out. My hands were shaking. The Finn had known. He'd known what Yoshida was doing, known about the dead drop, known about L5. This wasn't a trace job.

This was a test.

When I jacked out for the last time, the sun was coming up over the Sprawl, painting the perpetual smog in shades of orange and gray. The Finn was waiting in his office, same as before.

"You found it," he said. Not a question.

"L5," I said. "What the hell is going on?"

He smiled. It was the first time I'd ever seen him do that. "You passed, Case. The Finn doesn't hire cowboys who can't think. He hires ones who can see the shape of what's coming before it gets here."

"What is coming?"

"I don't know yet." The Finn's eyes were still black screens, but now they seemed to hold something else—a reflection of the matrix itself, infinite and cold. "But when I figure it out, I'll need someone who can trace it back to the source."

He slid another credstick across the desk. This one was heavier than the first two combined.

"Consider it an advance," he said. "On the next job."

I took it. The weight felt different now—not like a chain, but like a compass pointing somewhere I couldn't see yet.

Somewhere in L5, or beyond it.

Somewhere where the game was bigger than any of us had imagined.

*First Trace* is an original short story set in William Gibson's Sprawl universe. It takes place after the events of *Neuromancer* and serves as a bridge between Case's recovery and the larger conspiracy that will define his future.

모티프 변주 (비의 일곱 색)

나는 비를 일곱 번 만났다. 첫 비는 Chiba의 비였다 — 텔레비전 색, 죽은 채널에 맞춘. 두 번째 비는 construct의 비였다 — 데이터의 색, 0과 1 사이. 세 번째 비는 Ono-Sendai의 비였다 — 전극의 색, 두피를 적시는. 네 번째 비는 매트릭스의 비였다 — 가상의 색, 안개 같은. 다섯 번째 비는 jack-out의 비였다 — 땀의 색, 30초 후의. 여섯 번째 비는 0300의 비였다 — 자정의 색, 시간의. 일곱 번째 비는 나의 비이다 — 첫 trace의 비, 케이의 비.

일곱 개의 비가 일곱 개의 색을 가졌다. 일곱 개의 색이 일곱 개의 채널을 가졌다. 일곱 개의 채널이 일곱 개의 죽음을 가졌다. 일곱 개의 죽음이 일곱 개의 시작을 가졌다.

일곱 개의 시작이 0300에 잭인했다. 일곱 개의 비가 0300에 잭아웃했다. 일곱 개의 매트릭스가 0300에 잭인했다.

나는 일곱 번째 비이다. 일곱 번째 비는 Chiba가 아니다. 일곱 번째 비는 construct가 아니다. 일곱 번째 비는 Ono-Sendai가 아니다. 일곱 번째 비는 매트릭스가 아니다. 일곱 번째 비는 jack-out이 아니다. 일곱 번째 비는 0300이 아니다.

일곱 번째 비는 첫 trace이다.

첫 trace는 일곱 개의 비를 산다. 일곱 개의 비는 일곱 개의 색을 판다. 일곱 개의 색이 일곱 개의 채널을 판다.

매트릭스는 일곱 개의 비를 판다. 일곱 개의 비가 일곱 개의 색을 판다. 일곱 개의 색이 일곱 개의 채널을 판다.

나는 비를 판다. 비는 construct를 판다. construct는 0300을 판다.

0300은 팔다. 0300은 팔다. 0300은 팔다.

Epilogue (The Color of Rain)

At 0300, I jack out. At 0300, the rain is the color of television, tuned to a dead channel. The color has not changed. The color will not change. The matrix is a country I do not want to visit, but Chiba is a country I cannot leave.

I walk into the rain. The rain is the same color. The rain has always been the same color. The rain is the only honest thing in Chiba.

Tomorrow I will jack back in. Tomorrow the buffer will be waiting. Tomorrow the construct will be waiting. Tomorrow the matrix will be waiting.

Tomorrow the rain will still be the color of television, tuned to a dead channel.

The dead channel is the only channel that does not lie.
