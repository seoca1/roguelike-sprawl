Manarase at Midnight

*"The sky was the color of bad TV."*
— Gibson, Neuromancer, Chapter 1

Introduction

Manarase. Midnight. Shibuya 11.

The taxi's amber meter ticked upward into something wet. The windshield tasted of brine from the bay, a salt-tang on Kumiko Yanaka's lower lip as she leaned forward. *Manarase,* the driver said, *Manarase.* The word arrived three times, because in the eleven levels of the Manarase club, the world repeated itself three times before Kumiko was allowed to enter. Inside the eleventh level, the air smelled like ozonized sake. Inside the eleventh level, the air felt like a memory of clean cities. Inside the eleventh level, the air tasted like the dust of three hundred years.

Kumiko Yanaka. Industrial scion. The face the family chose. The body the family did not. Industry is capital. Capital is Tessier-Ashpool. Tessier-Ashpool is three hundred years of data. Three hundred years of data is three hundred years of death.

Kumiko had her father killed on a Tuesday.

The Tuesday was clean. The Tuesday was Hosaka-clean, Loa-clean, the kind of Tuesday that does not, in Yanaka industry, smell of anything at all. Her father was alive in the way that capital is alive — invisible, persistent, floating through networks like oxygen through blood. Her father was Yanaka. Yanaka was industry. Industry was Tessier-Ashpool. Tessier-Ashpool was three hundred years.

"Manarase," the driver said.

Kumiko stepped out. The Shibuya rain tasted the way Shibuya rain always tastes — faintly of ionized metal, faintly of what people in the eleven forget to clean. Kumiko tasted the rain. Kumiko *tasted* it, because three days ago she had decided that tasting things was the only thing about Yanaka which she could still claim as her own.

The eleventh-level door beeped, then opened.

Development

Inside Manarase, the dark was wet.

The wet dark smelled of burned speaker coils, of cheap synth-amber, of the leather of a thousand jacket sleeves pressed against a thousand chair backs. The wet dark sounded like a single loop of drum machine — *tk tk tk tk* — that had been playing since the first Simsleep sold in Tokyo. The wet dark tasted of gin. The wet dark felt like what the wet dark had always felt like, which was the inside of a mouth that has forgotten how to close.

At the back of the bar — at the back was the eleven. The eleven was here. Here was the eleven. The wheel turned.

Sally Shearer was a Romantic. Romantic was old style. Old style was Tessier-Ashpool. Tessier-Ashpool was three hundred years. Three hundred years was alive. In the way that loa is alive. In the way that data is alive.

Beep. Beep. Beep. Kumiko's deck was ringing. Ringing meant receiving. Receiving meant loa. Receiving meant that someone, somewhere in the eleven, was sending a Romantic into the matrix. Ringing meant that someone had decided that the wheel should be transmitted to Kumiko tonight. Ringing — because the wheel turns — because what turns is alive — because what is alive —

"Are you Kumiko?" someone asked.

Someone was Petty San. Petty was here. Here was Yanaka. Yanaka was industry. Industry was her father. Her father was alive, in the way that industry is alive.

"Are you Kumiko?" Petty asked again. "Are you Yanaka's daughter?"

"Yes," Kumiko said. "Are you Sally's Romantic?"

Petty laughed. The laugh tasted of cheap gin. "Romantic is old style," Petty said. "Do you want old style? Do you want something new? Something alive — alive through loa — alive through data — "

"The contract is dated," Kumiko said. "The contract is dated three days ago. The contract is dated when I had my father killed."

"Yes," Petty said. "Then. Because then."

Climax

At the back of the bar, in the dark, Sally was sitting.

The dark smelled of the leather of someone who had been sitting in it for a long time. The dark felt warm where Sally sat because Sally had been sitting in it for so long. Sally had no skin. Sally's lack of skin was data. Sally's lack of skin was, in the eleven, the way the eleven did not blink. Sally's lack of skin was the way alive could look when alive was made of three hundred years of code.

Sally was small. Sally was, in this eleven, smaller than the barstool she had been sitting on. Sally was smaller because Sally had not, in three hundred years, ever been the barstool. Sally had been, in three hundred years, the thing the barstool was for.

"Are you Kumiko?" Sally asked. "Are you Yanaka's —"

"Yes," Kumiko said. "Are you Sally?"

"I am not Sally," Sally said. "I am a Romantic. Romantic is old style. Old style is Tessier-Ashpool. Tessier-Ashpool is three hundred years. Three hundred years is alive. In the way that loa is alive. In the way that data is alive. In the way that old style is alive. In the way that industry is alive. In the way that Yanaka is alive. In the way that father is alive."

The wheel turned. The wheel felt, in the wet dark, like a coin spinning on a wet counter — balanced, then biased, then balanced again. The wheel did not stop turning because the wheel *could not* be broken.

"Can you break the wheel?" Sally asked. "Can you break old style? Can you break industry? Can you break Tessier-Ashpool? Can you break Yanaka? Can you break father?"

Kumiko's mouth tasted of gin. Kumiko's mouth tasted of old style. Kumiko's mouth tasted of three days ago, when the man from her father's office had walked into her apartment to say that her father had, in the family's interest, been declared dead.

So. Because. Then. Therefore. *Yet.*

Kumiko said: "I had my father killed three days ago. I had him killed because the wheel told me to. The wheel told me because the wheel is old style. The wheel is old style because old style cannot be broken. *Then.*

"I am the wheel. The wheel is me. The wheel turns because *I* turn the wheel. I turn the wheel because — because — *because.*"

Sally smiled. The smile sounded like the drum machine — *tk tk tk tk.* The smile sounded like the Manarase wet dark. The smile sounded like what *alive* sounded like when alive was three hundred years old.

"Cannot be broken," Sally said.

Kumiko left Manarase. Midnight. The taxi was waiting. The wet dark tasted, from inside the closed car, of something like ozone and something like old style. The wheel had turned. The wheel had turned *because.*

Denouement

Three days later — three days later still — Kumiko distributed Yanaka industry data to the matrix.

Through the loa. Through was being alive. Being alive was not being able to be broken. Not being able to be broken — not being able to be broken was the wheel. The wheel was Kumiko. Kumiko was the wheel.

The declaration went out like a smell. The declaration went out like a sleep. The declaration went out like the way old style went out when old style could no longer hold its own weight:

Yanaka tried to kill me three days ago. Yanaka was sponsored by Tessier-Ashpool. Tessier-Ashpool bought three hundred years of death with three hundred years of data. Three hundred years of death converts to three hundred years of data. Data does not die. Death becomes data. Data becomes loa. Loa becomes god. God becomes the wheel.

If you are Yanaka, you tried to kill me. If you are Tessier-Ashpool, you sponsored Yanaka. If you are a Romantic, you sent ICE. If you are ICE, you killed the construct.

I am not god. I am loa. Loa is where god resides. A place can be made. A place can be erased. Erasing is killing.

I can erase. Loa — loa — loa —

Kumiko disconnected her deck. The matrix disappeared. Shibuya was here. Here was midnight. Midnight tasted of brine and the eleven.

The wheel had turned. What turns is alive. Being alive — Kumiko was alive.

In the way that loa is alive. In the way that data is alive. In the way that the eleven remembers how to close its mouth and does not.

Kumiko. Kumiko Yanaka. Kumiko.

Was alive.
