Molly's Razor

*"She understood violence the way a surgeon understands anatomy."*

Introduction

The subroutine was hidden where nobody would think to look — three layers deep in a Yakuza entertainment node, a Chiba porn server carrying encrypted ICE that was never meant to be anything more than a toy. A distraction. The kind of security that whispered to a runner that the real prize was elsewhere, that the real data was one layer up, one more run, one more credit's worth of rig time.

But Kas had stopped believing in "elsewhere."

She'd pulled the node apart with a Hosaka burning rig, patient as a dentist working on a root canal, and found the subroutine waiting inside like a splinter under the nail. It moved wrong. Not like a program. Like a thing that remembered being a person. The code unfolded in her deck's display like a flower blooming in reverse — petals of logic collapsing into a core that was older than the Yakuza's hardware, older than the node it lived in, old enough to remember when Molly Millions was still alive.

The razors. That was what Finn called them, when she showed him the printout. He'd gone quiet for a moment — that particular Finn silence, the one that meant he was remembering something he didn't want to remember. Then: "Molly's razors. Haven't seen those in years."

"Whose?"

"Molly's. Molly Millions. She wrote them during the T-A runs — back when she was still running for herself, before she went full construct. The razors were her signature. Cut through anything. Hosaka, Tessier-Ashpool, the old BMA security — didn't matter. If it had ICE, Molly's razors got through it."

Kas looked at the printout again. The code was beautiful, in a cold way. A scalpel, not a sword.

"How much is it worth?"

"On the open market? Twenty thousand credits, minimum. On the right market?" He shrugged. "More than either of us will see in a year."

Development

The Maas Biolabs researcher arrived at the Finn's office at 1600, which meant she'd been awake for at least twenty hours. Dark circles under her eyes like bruises. A Finnish surname that Kas filed away and forgot. She wanted the subroutine, and she wanted it bad.

"Five thousand credits," the researcher said. "Wired to any account you name."

"What's it worth to you?" Finn asked. He never asked that question unless he already knew the answer.

The researcher didn't blink. "My work. It's my work."

Kas watched the woman from the corner where she'd positioned herself — a shadow with good posture, her hand resting on the Ono-Sendai rig at her hip like she was ready to draw. The subroutine was still burning in her deck's cache, a ghost of a ghost. Molly had built something that cut through corporate ICE like a scalpel through wet paper. You could see it in the code structure: the way it found structural weaknesses, the way it didn't stop when it hit a wall, the way it kept going until something important broke.

The Yakuza had been running it for three years without knowing what they had. Kas had seen their security setup — competent, layered, professional. Shimada-gumi ran the node, and their security people were good, the kind of good that came from decades of practice and a corporate culture that treated failure as a personal insult. They'd never looked inside the subroutine because it was buried in their own systems and it was doing their job for them. Why audit a tool that's working?

Molly's ghost, keeping the Yakuza safe.

The thought made Kas want to take a shower. She'd spent enough time around ghosts. The last thing she needed was another one.

The researcher finished her pitch. Five thousand credits, wired to any account. Maas Biolabs wanted the subroutine for their neural research division — some project Kas didn't understand and didn't try to. She had a rule about asking too many questions. The rule was: don't. Runners who asked too many questions ended up in places they didn't want to be.

Finn made the deal. The researcher left. The subroutine changed hands at midnight, transferred through a chain of clean servers that Kas didn't try to trace.

Molly's razors, sold to the highest bidder.

Kas thought about that as she walked home through the Sprawl's night corridors — the wet neon of the Ninsei, the smell of frying oil and burnt plastic, the hum of a thousand rigs running a thousand scams. Molly had been dead for three years, and her programs were still out there, still cutting, still making people rich.

Molly's ghost, sharpening the blade. Molly's ghost, cashing the check.

Climax

The Yakuza found out about the sale six hours before it was supposed to close. Kas got the message through channels that were already compromised — a courtesy, probably, from someone who'd decided she was worth warning. The Shimada-gumi wanted their subroutine back, and they wanted it back in pieces.

"Take the deal," Finn said. "We close tonight, wire goes through, you disappear."

"And the Yakuza?"

"The Yakuza gets over it. They always do."

Kas looked at the mirror across the street from the Finn's office. It was a real mirror, not a screen — old glass, slightly warped, the kind of thing they didn't make anymore. Her reflection stared back. She tried to find Molly in her own face.

The Finn was wrong. The Yakuza didn't always get over it. Sometimes they got even.

Denouement

She took the deal. The money cleared at 2300.

Three days later, the researcher went dark. Maas Biolabs had no comment. The Shimada-gumi had no comment. Kas read about it in a news feed while eating cold noodles in a corridor vendor, and she thought about the razors — Molly's work, still out there, still cutting.

Still killing people, probably.

The mirror in her rented room showed her a face she no longer recognized. That wasn't new. That had been true for months. What was new was the certainty that somewhere in the Sprawl's dark code, Molly was still watching. Molly's razors, still sharp.

Molly's ghost, sharpening the blade.
