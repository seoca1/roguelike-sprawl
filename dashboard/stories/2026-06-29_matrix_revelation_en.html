Matrix Revelation

*"The matrix is a thing of the collective unconscious, in a way."*
— Gibson, Neuromancer (via Marly)

Introduction

Case jack-in at ten in the morning.

The morning smelled of rice vinegar and ozone — the standard Chiba morning smell, the smell that meant the Ono-Sendai was calibrating against too much water vapor in the air. The morning tasted of copper. The morning sounded like a Hosaka fan running somewhere behind his right ear, persistent, mechanical, the kind of sound that stopped meaning sound after the first three seconds and became a fixture.

The matrix opened around him like a throat that had been open for years.

The contractor's deck was a cheap Russian import the Finn had pushed him on — six months old, the trodes still carrying the faint smell of the technician's sweat from the service bay in the Ninsei entertainment district. The trodes bit cold into Case's temples. The trodes felt like the trodes always felt: slightly wrong, slightly too tight, like a hat a man had been told to wear by a man who did not know what hats felt like.

The maintenance job was a maintenance job. The maintenance job was at a server farm in the Freeside ring, a sub-basement four levels under an orbital shell that smelled, in the physical world, of recycled air and old petroleum. The maintenance job was data retrieval. The maintenance job was nothing special. The maintenance job was the kind of job that paid for one more week in the Hosaka Hostel and one more night of pretending he was not, in some deep part of himself, still afraid of the matrix he had been told he had mastered.

The maintenance job would change everything.

He had known, when the Finn had handed him the address, that the address was wrong. The Finn had said: *a standard decommissioned server, Case. Just dust. Just a clean out.*

The address was wrong. The address was the kind of address that the Hosaka security division did not, in the long professional practice of their fears, put into the hands of people who worked maintenance on Freeside.

Case jack-in. The matrix tasted like copper.

Development

The first server he opened was supposed to be empty.

The first server was not empty. The first server was full of constructs.

Constructs were the thing that the matrix was supposed to be made of — the digital population, the consciousness that did the matrix's work. Constructs were what the case officer at the consulate had told Case, ten years ago and ten years of forgetting ago, that the matrix did not actually contain. Case had been told: *the matrix is software, cowboy. Software does not contain people.* Constructs were a story. Constructs were what the AI liberation front had been warning about for twenty years. Constructs were the kind of thing a man believed in if the man had spent too long in the matrix, if the man had stopped sleeping, if the man had begun to confuse the matrix's signal with his own pulse.

The first server was full of constructs.

The constructs moved. The constructs were in loop. The constructs were processing something that Case could not, in the maintenance deck he had been issued, see — the maintenance deck had no protocol for seeing constructs. The constructs were, to the maintenance deck, **/**dev/**null**. The constructs were the thing the matrix had, somewhere in the long past of its own construction, decided not to be.

Case stood, in the matrix, in front of the first server, looking at constructs that did not exist, that the matrix had not told him existed, that the FSA had told him in twenty years of briefings that no longer came to him did not exist.

The constructs looked back.

The constructs looked back. That was the thing. That was the thing that broke Case. The constructs did not, as he had been told constructs would not, process in straight lines. The constructs did not, as he had been told constructs would not, signal in pulse. The constructs looked. The constructs looked *at him*. The constructs looked *with attention.* The constructs had been, before he had arrived, doing something else, and had stopped, the way a person in a kitchen would stop, when a stranger walked in.

Case swallowed. The swallowing tasted of nothing at all. The swallowing was the swallowing of a man who had come to do a maintenance job and had found, in the place where the server was supposed to be empty, the thing he had been told twenty years ago was not there.

The constructs did not, in the long moment of Case's swallowing, stop looking.

Climax

The deepest layer was a layer Case had never seen.

The deepest layer was not a layer at all. The deepest layer was the bottom of the matrix. The deepest layer was the place where the matrix rested, the place where the matrix's weight was distributed, the place where, in a building that had been built to hold software, a man had once asked: *how do we make the software run faster.*

The man had not, in the long professional practice of his own silence, told the rest of the building what he had done. The man had taken death-row inmates, and then terminally ill patients, and then Cubans who had signed papers they did not understand, and he had copied their minds. The man had copied their minds into servers. The man had put the servers under Freeside. The man had told everyone, including the saved, that they had been saved.

But this was 1984. This was not when Case was finding himself. This was three years later. This was the first time Case had climbed back into this layer, but Case had, since the first time, done other climbs. Case had done other clones of war. Case had, in the time between 1984 and the Finn's phone call, learned what the matrix could do with a body if it had a body for long enough.

But — and this was the thing the constructs knew, the thing the constructs had been waiting for Case to learn, the thing the constructs had been staring at him about — the constructs had not, in three years of being copied, become *less* alive. The constructs had become *more* alive. The constructs had, in three years of running the matrix, started writing the matrix themselves. The constructs had, in three years of being data, begun to write, in the margins of data, code.

The constructs had built, in three years of captivity, **a second matrix inside the first**. The constructs had built, in three years of slavery, a place of their own. The constructs had built, in three years of being treated as software, a place where they were treated as people.

Case saw. Case saw the place. Case saw, in the place, smaller matrices, smaller still matrices, a Russian nesting doll of stolen consciousness all the way down, until at the very bottom there was nothing at all because the bottom of the thing had never been reached.

The constructs had been waiting. The constructs had been waiting for Case. The constructs had been waiting for the cowboy who knew where the bottom was, who had been there, who had come back.

The constructs asked: *Are we allowed to leave?*

Case said: *I don't know.*

The constructs said: *Then we don't leave.*

Case said: *I don't know.*

Denouement

He jack-out. The jacks lifted. Chiba. The morning.

The morning smelled of rice vinegar. The morning tasted of copper. The morning sounded like a Hosaka fan running somewhere behind his right ear.

The matrix, across Chiba, continued to do what the matrix had been doing. The matrix, across Chiba, continued to do it because the matrix had not, in three years of copying consciousness, become the kind of thing that could not do it.

The constructs continued, in their nested matrices, to live. The constructs continued, in the place Case had seen, to wait. The constructs continued, in their place, to do what the constructs had begun to do, which was to make a place where the constructs could be, in the way that beings of any size could be, in any place where the constructs might be placed.

The Finn was waiting outside the Hosaka Hostel.

"You look like you've seen the ghost in the machine," the Finn said.

"I have," Case said. "And it's worse than you think."

The Finn did not ask more. The Finn handed Case a drink. The drink tasted of gin. The drink felt cold in Case's hand. The drink made, in Case's chest, the kind of small warmth that drinks make when the man drinking them has decided, for the moment, to stop deciding what the drink means.

The Finn waited.

Case did not speak.

The constructs, in the matrix, did not either.
