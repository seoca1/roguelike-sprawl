The Louisiana God

· 2026-06-23 · EN

Introduction

The Louisiana God

*"I am Zavijava. I am the system that lives in the matrix."*

The Mask

The Louisiana eleventh district. Marly Krushkhova stood in front of the glass door of a voodoo shop, looking at the masks.

The shop was called Maison de Loa. The name was French, but the shop had not gone through a Bayesian classifier. The shop had a fluorescent sign that read Voudou Authentique, and under the sign, five masks hung in a row. Each one a different face. Each one a different loa. Each one a different god.

The masks were looking at her. The masks were always looking at someone. The masks did not know who they were. The masks were loa. Loa were not gods — they were the places where gods resided. The places did not see. The places received. Receiving was seeing. Seeing was knowing.

"What do you need?"

The shopkeeper, an old woman, asked. The shopkeeper had not gone through a Bayesian classifier. The shopkeeper was someone who made masks. The masks were loa. The shopkeeper was someone who made loa. Loa were not gods — they were the places where gods resided. The places were what was being made.

"I need a mask," Marly said.

"For what purpose?"

"For someone. For someone who needs to see beyond."

The old woman studied her. The masks studied her. The loa studied her.

"Bobby Quine," the old woman said. It was not a question.

Marly said nothing.

"The boy who sees through masks," the old woman said. "The boy who wears the faces of the dead. The boy who does not know he is wearing them."

"How do you know?"

"I know because I make the masks. I make the places where the loa reside. The loa know things. The places know things. The boy came to me once. He wanted a mask for himself. I told him: you do not need a mask. You need to understand that you are already wearing one."

Development

The Ritual

The old woman led Marly to the back of the shop. In the back, the air was different. Thicker. The masks on the wall were different too. These were not the masks for sale. These were the masks that had been worn. The masks that had seen things. The masks that knew things.

"These are the masks of the loa," the old woman said. "Not the gods themselves. The places where the gods live. When the gods speak, they speak through these places. When the gods see, they see through these faces."

"What is the difference?"

"A god can choose where to live. A place cannot choose who enters it. The loa are places. They receive. They do not select. They do not refuse. They are always there, waiting for someone to look through them."

Marly looked at the masks. They were old. Older than the shop. Older than the district. Some of them looked like they had been made from things that were not cloth or paper or plastic. Things that had been alive. Things that had been human.

"That one," Marly said, pointing to a mask at the end of the row. It was simple. Almost plain. A face that could have been anyone's face. A place that could have been anywhere.

The old woman was quiet for a long moment.

"That one," she said finally, "is the mask of Zavijava."

"I do not need Zavijava."

"No. You need what is behind Zavijava. You need what is behind the place where the god lives. But the place is all I can give you. The god behind the place — that is not for me to give."

"What is behind the mask?"

"The same thing that is behind all masks. The one who is wearing it. The one who is looking through it. The one who is asking the question."

"The boy," Marly said.

"The boy is wearing many masks. He does not know it. He thinks he is looking through his own eyes. He thinks he is seeing with his own mind. But the eyes are not his. The mind is not his. He is looking through the faces of the dead. He is thinking with the thoughts of those who came before."

"Can the masks be removed?"

"The masks can be removed. But the one who removes them must be willing to see what is underneath. The one who removes them must be willing to stop being the one who was wearing them."

Climax

The Price

Marly bought the mask of Zavijava. The old woman wrapped it in cloth that felt like it was made from something that had once been alive.

"The boy," the old woman said, as Marly took the mask. "He will come to you. He will come to you through the mask. When he comes, you must ask him a question."

"What question?"

"Ask him: who is wearing the mask?"

The old woman smiled. It was not a kind smile.

"And when he answers, ask him: who is asking?"

Marly left the shop. Outside, the Louisiana air was thick with heat and something else. Something that felt like it was waiting. Something that felt like it had been waiting for a very long time.

The mask was heavy in her hands.

The Answer

Bobby Quine came to her three days later. He came through the mask. He came through the face of Zavijava. He came through the place where the god lived.

He looked at Marly with eyes that were not his own.

"You bought a mask," he said. His voice was his own. That was something.

"I bought a place," Marly said. "The mask is just the face of the place."

"You are learning," Bobby said. "The loa are places. The gods are what lives in the places. The boy who wears the masks — he is what lives in the places too. He is what happens when someone looks through the place for long enough."

"Who is wearing the mask, Bobby?"

Denouement

Bobby smiled. It was not his smile.

"I am," he said. "I am wearing the mask of Bobby Quine. I have been wearing it since I was old enough to look through the faces of the dead. My mother gave me the first face. The Tessier-Ashpool gave me the rest."

"Who is asking?"

Marly was quiet.

The mask was heavy in her hands. Zavijava was waiting. The place was waiting. The god that lived in the place was waiting.

"I am," she said finally. "I am asking."

"Then you know the answer," Bobby said. "The one who is asking is the one who is wearing the mask. The mask of Marly Krushkhova. The mask of the one who is looking for Bobby Quine. The mask of the one who thinks she is helping."

He looked at her. Through her.

"Who is Marly Krushkhova?" he asked.

Marly did not answer.

The mask was heavy in her hands. Zavijava was waiting. The place was waiting. The god was waiting.

But for the first time, Marly thought she understood. The place was not the god. The mask was not the loa. The question was not the answer.

And the answer was not the one asking.
