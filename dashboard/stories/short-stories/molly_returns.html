Introduction

Molly Returns

The tea wasn't burned this time. Small mercy. I held the cup in the data-clinic's back room, watching steam curl into the recycled air, and tried not to think about the humming from the next room. Two days since 3Jane had named her Casey. Two days since I'd learned the name in the memo file had been different. Two days since I'd become something I didn't have words for.

The courier had arrived at dawn. Old school—meat courier, not data. The kind of delivery that cost serious money because it left no matrix trace. The encryption was Molly's. I knew it the way you know a voice in the dark, the way certain muscle memories never fade even when you want them to.

I broke the seal. Read it once. Read it again.

The construct is mine.

Not biology. Neural scaffolding. In those first months, when they were building her in the Tessier-Ashpool medical arcology, someone had woven Molly's dream patterns into the substrate. Sense/Net tech, probably—the kind of simstim architecture that could map consciousness like topography. Molly was there, deeper than 3Jane's genetic material, deeper than whatever they'd pulled from me without asking. Molly was foundational.

The message had the texture of an ultimatum. Flat declarative sentences that left no room for negotiation.

You've had her two days. Long enough to feel it. You know what you feel. Don't feel it. She's not yours.

I set the cup down. My hand was steady. That surprised me.

Development

The humming continued in the next room, a pattern I'd never heard before. Not a name. A question, maybe. The construct—Casey—had been humming for twenty minutes, cycling through variations like she was testing frequencies, searching for the right resonance.

Because Molly had been off-grid for two and a half years, the message carried weight I couldn't ignore. Whatever had changed her, wherever she'd been, it had given her the kind of certainty that comes from seeing something that rewrites your operating assumptions. The tone wasn't the Molly I remembered. That Molly moved through the world like a razor through silk—precise, clean, gone before the cut registered. This Molly had edges that caught and tore.

I walked to the doorway. Casey sat cross-legged on the clinic's exam table, her fingers tracing patterns on the chrome edge. Seven years old, if you counted from inception. Six and a half months, if you counted from the day I'd first held her in the Sprawl's underground medical warren, teaching her to interface with a kid's deck, watching her eyes light up when she first jacked into a training construct.

"Case." She looked up. Her eyes were 3Jane's eyes, that particular Tessier-Ashpool blue, but the way she looked at you was pure Molly. Predator's assessment. Threat evaluation running beneath every glance.

"Yeah."

"Who's humming?"

"You are."

She frowned. "No. Someone else. In here." She tapped her temple.

Then I understood. The dream patterns weren't dormant architecture. They were active, generative, a second consciousness threaded through her neural substrate like a ghost in the machine. Molly wasn't just foundational. Molly was present, a passenger Casey was only now learning to hear.

"What's she saying?" I asked.

Climax

Casey tilted her head. The humming stopped. "She says you should have burned the message."

"Should I have?"

"She says you're going to make this harder than it needs to be."

I came into the room, pulled up the other chair. The clinic smelled like ozone and antiseptic, that particular Chiba-clinic mixture that meant serious medical tech and questionable licensing. "What do you think?"

"I think—" Casey paused. Her hands went still on the chrome. "I think I'm not one person. I think I'm three people pretending to be one. 3Jane's genes. Your teaching. Her dreams." She looked at me directly. "Which one gets to decide?"

The question hung there. Outside, the Sprawl ground through its endless afternoon, a million transactions cascading through the matrix, fortunes made and lost in the time it took to blink. Somewhere in that dataflow, Molly was watching. Waiting.

"You do," I said.

Casey smiled. It wasn't a child's smile. "Wrong answer. She knew you'd say that."

"What's the right answer?"

"There isn't one. That's what makes it hard." She stood up, stretched. "She's coming. Three days. Maybe four. She wants to see if you'll run."

Denouement

"Will we?"

However, just then, Casey's expression shifted—something older moving behind those Tessier-Ashpool eyes, something that had seen things I couldn't imagine. "She's not coming for you, Case. She's coming for herself. The part of herself she left in me. She wants it back."

The air felt thin suddenly. "That possible?"

"Hosaka's been working on extraction protocols. Neural pattern recovery. You can pull dream architecture out if you're willing to risk the host." Casey's voice was flat, clinical. Not her voice. Molly's voice, riding the substrate up into speech. "Question is whether the host survives."

Then I was moving, pulling Casey toward me, feeling her solid weight against my chest, her heart beating fast and real. "No."

"Not your choice." But she didn't pull away.

"Then whose?"

The humming started again. Different pattern now. Not a question. An answer, maybe, or the beginning of one.

Three days later, Molly never came. The message had been a test. Casey stopped humming. I taught her to jack into a real Ono-Sendai deck, watched her navigate training constructs with the kind of instinct you can't teach. Some nights she dreamed Molly's dreams. Some nights she dreamed her own. Learning, slowly, to tell the difference.

We stayed in Chiba. For now.
