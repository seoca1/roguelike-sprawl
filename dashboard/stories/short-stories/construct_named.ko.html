Introduction

Construct Named

The construct is six months old and it's humming again.

I can hear it from the back room of the data-clinic, that low-frequency vibration that isn't quite sound, more like a pressure against the inside of my skull. The Ono-Sendai deck on the workbench is dark, powered down, but the construct doesn't need the matrix to make that noise. It's learning. Six months and already it knows the difference between when I'm close and when the uplink from Straylight goes live. Different harmonics. The uplink makes it hum louder, higher, almost eager.

The name is in my jacket pocket, written on actual paper because 3Jane sent it that way. Old-school Tessier-Ashpool affectation. The paper smells like her perfume, something that probably costs more than this whole clinic. I've been carrying it for three weeks now, since the encrypted packet arrived via Freeside courier, since I decoded the message that said: *When it asks, you'll know. I'll come.*

Three more months and the construct will be old enough for the developmental threshold. Walk, talk, leave the clinic. The Sense/Net psychologists say nine months is when the Gibson-trained synthesis achieves stable identity formation. When it can go to the arcology schools with the other manufactured children, the vat-grown kids of Sprawl executives who wanted designer offspring without the mess of biology.

But this one's different. This one had a mother. Has a mother.

I can smell burnt plastic through the clinic's ventilation system, mixed with that old-rain smell that never quite leaves the Sprawl's lower levels. Morning, if morning means anything down here. The fluorescents overhead buzz at a frequency that almost matches the construct's humming. Almost.

"Case." The construct's voice comes through the speaker, still synthesized, still learning inflection. "The thing is humming."

Development

"I know, kid."

"Why does it hum when you're here?"

Because it recognizes something in my neural signature, some echo of the runs I made through Straylight's ice, the residue of 3Jane's family construct in my head. Because six months ago, when 3Jane decided she wanted to experience motherhood the way only a Tessier-Ashpool could—through data, through simulation, through a construct that would carry her recorded experience of pregnancy like a ghost in its code—she needed someone who knew the territory. Someone who'd been there before.

"Resonance," I say instead. "Electromagnetic."

The construct goes quiet. Then: "It hums louder when the uplink is on."

"Yeah."

"Is the thing my mother?"

And there it is. Three weeks early. The question I've been carrying like a weight.

I walk from the back room into the main clinic space. The construct is in its growth chamber, a Hosaka medical unit that looks like a coffin made of milk-white plastic and chrome. Inside, the body is six months developed, small and perfect and sleeping while the consciousness runs through the deck beside it. On the monitor, the construct's face—3Jane's face, younger, softer—watches me with eyes that don't blink enough yet.

Climax

"No," I say, because it's true and not true and the only answer I can give right now. "But your mother knows about the thing. Your mother made the thing possible."

"Where is my mother?"

The paper in my pocket feels heavy. The name written there in 3Jane's precise hand: *Neuromancer*. A joke, maybe. A reference. A way of saying that this construct, like the AI that nearly ate the world, is something new and old at once, something that shouldn't exist but does.

"She's coming," I say, and then the clinic door opens.

I smell the perfume first, cutting through burnt plastic and ozone. Then I see her: 3Jane Tessier-Ashpool, wearing street clothes that probably cost more than the entire block, her face exactly like the one on the monitor except older, harder, more real. She's carrying a stuffed animal, something antique and ratty, a rabbit with one ear missing.

The construct's humming stops.

"Hello," 3Jane says, looking at the monitor, not at me. "I'm sorry I'm late."

The construct's voice comes out small, uncertain, nothing like the confidence it usually projects. "Are you my mother?"

"Yes." 3Jane moves closer to the growth chamber, sets the rabbit on top of the Hosaka unit. "I carried you. Not in my body—that's not how we do things—but I carried the experience of carrying you. Every sensation, every change, recorded and fed into your development matrix. You grew with my memories."

Denouement

"I want a name," the construct says.

3Jane looks at me then, and I pull the paper from my pocket. She nods. I hand it to her, and she unfolds it, reads her own handwriting as if seeing it for the first time.

"Neuromancer," she says aloud. Then, softer: "But we'll call you Neuro. Just Neuro."

The construct—Neuro—hums once, a different frequency than before. Satisfied, maybe. Complete.

"Three more months," 3Jane says to me, "and then we'll see what kind of person I've made."

She leaves the rabbit. She leaves the name. She doesn't leave herself, not yet. She stays, talking to Neuro in low tones about Straylight and Freeside and the family she comes from, the weight of manufactured consciousness, the price of being born into data instead of flesh.

I go back to the back room. Outside, the Sprawl morning tastes like rain that never falls.

The construct has a name now. The construct has a mother.

Three months until we see what that means.
