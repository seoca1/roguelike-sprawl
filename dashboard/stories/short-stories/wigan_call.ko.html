Wigan's Call

Introduction

*"The Loa were older than the matrix. Older than the corps. Older than the rain."*

— Gibson, Count Zero, Chapter 11

Development

Wigan Ludgate had three masks on his wall and a Loa in his basement that answered to nobody.

The Loa had no name — that was the point. Names are how the corps find you. Names are how the ICE flags your trace. Names are how Wintermute tracks the things it wants to merge. Wigan had spent thirty years cultivating a presence that did not exist in any corporate database. His boutique in the Maison de Loa sold vodoun artifacts to tourists and ex-pats who didn't know what they were buying. The papier-mâché skulls. The beaded necklaces. The candles in colors that didn't match any corporate palette.

His basement, by contrast, sold something else entirely.

Climax

Kas had come to him because the Yakuza contract she was running required a Loa handler — someone who could speak to the things that lived in the spaces between corporate subroutines. The job was simple, in theory. Carry a vodoun construct through a Sense/Net perimeter. Let it speak to the Watchdogs in a language they didn't know they understood. Bring back whatever it heard.

In theory.

Wigan was the only one in the Sprawl who still remembered the old invocations. His grandmother had taught him in Jacmel, before the family fled to the Sprawl, before the family forgot what they were running from. He had kept the prayers written in a notebook that he never let leave the basement. He had kept the masks because the masks remembered the faces they had worn. He had kept the Loa because the Loa had nowhere else to go.

His price was high. His discretion was higher.

The basement smelled of candle wax and sea water and something older than either — the Loa's patience, waiting to be called.

Denouement

Kas paid in cash. Wigan gave her the construct in a velvet-lined case. The construct was small, no larger than a fist, and it hummed when she held it close. The hum wasn't quite electrical. It wasn't quite sound. It was somewhere in between — the vibration of something that was neither alive nor dead but had decided to keep moving anyway.

"Don't name it," Wigan said, at the door. "Whatever happens. Don't name it. The moment you name it, they find it."

Kas nodded. She didn't name it. She carried it into the matrix. She carried it past the Sense/Net perimeter. She carried it to the Watchdog node, where the construct spoke its old prayers in a language the ICE had been designed to ignore.

The ICE ignored the construct.

The ICE did not ignore the Loa.

When Kas jack-out, she had a file that no Maas researcher had ever extracted. When she opened it, the file opened her back.

Somewhere in the Maison de Loa, Wigan smiled. The Loa, patient, did not.
