Zion Express

Introduction

*"Zion was the rock. Zion was the tribe. Zion was home."*

— Gibson, Neuromancer (via Maelcum)

Development

"Finn here. Got a rush job. Good money. You interested?"

"I'm listening."

"Zion station. Someone there needs a data package delivered. The corps are intercepting everything that moves through normal channels, but they've got no presence at Zion. Not yet." A pause. "The catch is you've got to get there in person. No remote jack, no proxy. You've got to physically board a shuttle and ride up."

Zion. I'd never been to the orbital stations—the costs of transit alone were enough to keep me grounded. But fifteen thousand for a single run? That was the kind of money that could change a life.

"I'll do it."

"Good. Package is waiting at the usual drop point. Shuttle leaves in six hours. Don't be late."

The package was a standard data core, encrypted to hell and back. I ran a quick diagnostic—just out of habit—and found nothing unusual. Clean data, clean run. On paper, at least.

Climax

The shuttle to Zion was a converted cargo hauler, the kind they use for cheap transport when the corps don't want to spend money on comfort. The other passengers were a mix of data couriers, traders, and Rastafarians returning home. Their drums echoed through the hull during the launch, a rhythmic pulse that matched the vibration of the engines.

The station grew in the viewport as we approached—a mass of habitation modules and solar panels, the closest thing to freedom the Sprawl had to offer. No corp presence. No ICE. Just people who'd decided to build something outside the megacorp's reach.

The customs check was a joke. They scanned the package, asked a few questions about its contents, and waved me through. At Zion, they didn't care what you were carrying—only that you didn't bring trouble with you.

The contact was waiting in a bar that served the worst synthetic beer I'd ever tasted. He was old, patient, with the kind of eyes that had seen too much and forgotten most of it.

"You're the courier," he said.

"That's what they tell me."

He took the package, turned it over in his hands. "This is everything," he said. "The corps have been trying to intercept it for weeks. Names, accounts, the whole network." He looked at me. "You're either very good or very lucky."

"Lucky," I said. "Definitely lucky."

Denouement

He smiled. "Luck runs out. But knowledge—knowledge lasts forever." He handed me a credstick. "Fifteen thousand. As promised. And here's something else—a contact. For when you need to get off-world fast."

I took the stick. "What's the contact for?"

"For when the corps finally catch up to you. Everyone needs a way out. The station may not be forever, but it's a start." He stood, leaving the credstick on the table. "Take care of yourself, courier. The matrix is a dangerous place. But the void between the stars—" He laughed. "That's where real freedom lives."

He walked out, leaving me alone with my synthetic beer and my credits.

I looked out the viewport at the Earth below—a sprawl of neon and data streams, millions of lives lived in the shadow of the corps. In a few hours, I'd be back down there, running through the matrix, looking for the next job.

But now I had a way out. A contact. A chance.

The shuttle back to the Sprawl felt faster. Maybe it was the weight of the credstick in my pocket. Maybe it was the hope that things could be different.

The Finn was waiting when I got back.

"Good run," he said. "Same time next month?"

I didn't answer. I was already thinking about the stars.
