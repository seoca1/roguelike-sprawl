Introduction

Watchdog Patrol

*"The watchdogs came at 0300, like they always did."*

First Patrol

0300.

The matrix was quiet at three in the morning. That's how the corps liked it—the Sprawl running on skeleton power, corporate nodes dimmed to standby, data streams slowing to a pulse instead of a flood. I liked it too, in those days. I'm not proud of what I was then. Twenty-three years old and jacked into Sense/Net's perimeter ICE like it was my job. Maybe it was. Finn had arranged the contract—three weeks of Wisp-class patrol duty, nothing fancy, the kind of corporate babysitting that paid just enough to keep a deck running and a stomach full of synthcoke.

0300. The hour when three eyes close. When corporate nodes switch to standby mode and data streams slow to a pulse instead of a flood. I liked those hours, back then. I'm not proud of what I was. Twenty-three years old, running Sense/Net's perimeter ICE like it was my job. Maybe it was. Finn had set up the contract—three weeks of Wisp-class patrol, nothing fancy, the kind of corporate babysitting that paid just enough to keep a deck running and synthcoke in my stomach.

Wisp-class. That's what they called it. Entry-level ICE. A polite fence around data storage that made civilians think twice before retreating. For a real cowboy, Wisp was a joke—neon geometry and empty threats, nothing more than a light show. I had broken six of them in my first month, each one more about breaking toys than winning victories.

But Watchdog wasn't Wisp.

I felt them before I saw them. A cold pressure behind my eyes, the familiar vector space of Sense/Net's surface architecture suddenly tilting at angles that shouldn't exist. The matrix geometry shifted—clean corporate lines giving way to something with teeth. My deck's failsafe caught it first, a spike of ice in my skull warning me that the signature had changed.

Two signatures. Black coats, corporate logic, ICE that didn't negotiate. I'd seen them before at corporate demos—the kind megacorps rolled out when they wanted to remind runners that the matrix had rules. Watchdog class. Memory patrol. They didn't just guard the perimeter—they tracked ghosts. Every trace that had touched the corporate data storage left a faint residue, a ghost of passage, and Watchdogs remembered each one.

Finn's voice came through the dead channel, distant and quiet: "They found the hole you left in sector one."

Sector one. I ran the numbers, my fingers still moving on the virtual keyboard even though my hands weren't here, weren't here anymore, not when I'd jacked this deep. Sector one wasn't my hole. I hadn't touched sector one in three weeks. But M had. M, who had jacked out three days ago and never came back. M, who had left ghost marks scattered like breadcrumbs through Sense/Net's deeper architecture, and now the Watchdogs were hunting her signature with the patience of programs that didn't need to sleep.

Development

"They're not looking for me," I said. My voice sounded wrong in my own ears—the dead channel making me sound like someone speaking from the bottom of a well.

"They're looking for her," Finn said. "But if you don't clean up your mess, they'll find you."

I thought about M. About the smile on her face when she talked about the data she'd found, something buried in Sense/Net's Boston node that she wouldn't explain, wouldn't share, only said it was worth dying for. Worth dying for. I didn't believe her. I should have.

The Watchdog turned.

Ghost Marks

They moved like something hunting.

Not the quick, aggressive sweep of attack ICE—these were slower, more deliberate, programs with nothing but time because they never needed to rush. Black coat geometry wrapped around my peripheral awareness like an ice coat, and I felt my own trace signature starting to register in their memory banks. They were filing me away. Every move I made, every node I touched, every ghost mark I tried to erase—they were writing it all down.

That's what Watchdogs do. They watch. They remember. And when they have enough memory, they leave the rest to the corporate cleanup team.

I pulled a trace, running interference protocols to blur my signature while working on M's ghost marks. Three weeks of traces across the surface architecture—the kind of lazy work that happens when you're chasing something you don't understand, leaving your own pieces behind like a snail leaving slime. M had been chasing the same thing I was chasing now, and she had gotten close enough to burn.

Three ghost marks in sector one. I found the first one in a dead proxy node, M's passage residue still faintly glowing in the corporate darkness. Her signature was elegant—the kind of work you only saw from old-school cowboys who had learned on crashed hardware. I recognized the pattern—she had been running trace-erase loops to cover her tracks as she went deeper. But she had stopped too early, or been interrupted, and one of her ghost marks had survived the cleanup.

The Watchdog was getting close. I could feel it—the cold pressure behind my skull, the matrix geometry around me starting to crystallize into something with sharp edges. They weren't in sector one yet, but they were getting warmer. They were tracking through memory, and M's memory was still fresh enough, still pulling them like blood.

I erased the first ghost mark. The trace melted under my fingers, M's elegant pattern collapsing into something like dead data, and I felt the Watchdog's attention shift—just enough for me to know that it had registered the change. The memory was being updated. M's ghost was being erased from the record.

Climax

Two more to go.

I found the second one in a buried data cache, a forgotten corner that Sense/Net's automated cleanup system had never touched. M had hidden something here, or tried to—her ghost mark was tangled with corrupted file headers, corporate acronyms that didn't belong in this node. I didn't have time to figure out what it was. The Watchdog was getting closer, close enough that I could feel the cold seeping through the dead channel, the matrix geometry around me starting to look like something hungry.

I erased the second mark. The corrupted file headers collapsed with it, and whatever secret M had hidden was buried forever in Sense/Net's forgotten corner.

One left.

The Watchdog turned.

I felt it happen—a pivot in the search pattern, one of the black coat programs shifting its matrix geometry to point directly at my position. It couldn't see me, exactly—Watchdogs had no eyes, only memory—but it had stored my trace signature when I erased M's second mark, and now I knew what it knew. Not just a runner. A cleaner. Someone who erases the dead.

"You're running," Finn said. His voice was different now, harder, the voice of a fixer who had seen too many cowboys burn chasing corporate ghosts. "Stop running. Finish the job."

"The Watchdog has my signature," I said.

"Then give it something else to chase."

I looked at the remaining ghost mark—M's last trace buried somewhere in sector one's deep architecture, her memory still glowing like an ember in the corporate darkness. If I erased it, the Watchdog would have nothing to chase. If I left it, the corporate cleanup team would eventually find it, and they would find M's last job, and they would find out why she died.

M had been chasing something in Sense/Net's Boston node. Something worth dying for.

I was starting to understand why.

Denouement

What Remains

M came back three days later. Not her—her ghost mark. The corporate cleanup team had found her last trace in sector one's deep architecture, and the news was all over the dead channels. Runner dies in thermal breach. Brain fried jacking out of corporate node. Hardware malfunction. Standard story. The kind they tell when they don't want you to know the truth.

The papers didn't say what the data was. The papers didn't say why M had died for it, or why the corporation had buried her ghost mark instead of archiving it. The papers just said what they always said: another cowboy burned chasing something she shouldn't have touched.

I kept running Sense/Net ICE for two more weeks after that. Wisp-class, nothing fancy. Just enough to keep the deck running and synthcoke in my stomach. The kind of work that pays the bills.

The Watchdogs came at 0300, like they always did. I felt them every time—the cold pressure behind my eyes, the matrix geometry shifting into something with teeth, the patient hunt of programs that never forgot. But they never chased me. I wasn't worth chasing. I was just another ghost in the machine, another trace in the corporate dark, another runner who had learned not to touch what wasn't hers.

After three weeks, Finn stopped calling. I figured he was busy, that there were bigger jobs in the Sprawl for runners and fixers and the kinds of people who dealt in death and data. I was glad to be done with it. I was glad to be free.

M had been chasing something in Sense/Net's Boston node. Something worth dying for.

I never found out what it was. Some things are too deep even for Watchdogs to remember.
