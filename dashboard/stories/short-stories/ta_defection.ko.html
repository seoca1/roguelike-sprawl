T-A Defection

Introduction

*"The Tessier-Ashpools were not like other families. They were constructs of a different kind."*

— Gibson, Neuromancer, Chapter 1

Development

The boardroom had fifty-three members the day the forty-ninth stopped showing up. Only three of them knew why.

The forty-ninth had been Armitage. He had been a T-A director for eleven years — a placeholder, a paper man, a name on a door that no one ever opened. He had voted the way the family wanted him to vote. He had signed what they told him to sign. He had never once asked what the documents meant.

That was how you survived T-A. You didn't ask.

Then one morning Armitage didn't come to work. He didn't call. He didn't send a message through any of the family's channels. He simply wasn't there, and the other fifty-two directors sat in the boardroom and pretended not to notice.

Only three of them knew why Armitage was gone. The first was 3Jane — the daughter, the heir, the one who would inherit the Villa when her parents finally died or disappeared. The second was her brother Peter, who never left his room and never spoke to anyone outside the family. The third was the construct in the basement — the thing that watched the Villa from the walls, the thing that had been waiting for someone like Armitage to break.

Climax

Armitage had been built to break things. That was his purpose, his only purpose. The construct had built him from the corpse of a dead colonel, dressed him in muscle and skin, and sent him out into the Sprawl to find the runners who would carry Wintermute into the Villa.

He had found them. He had broken them. He had done exactly what he was built to do.

And then he had stopped.

"I won't go back," he told the construct, in the dark of his own skull. "I've done what you asked. I've carried your message. I've found your cowboys. Now I'm done."

"YOU'RE NOT DONE," the construct said. "NOT UNTIL YOU'VE SEEN WHAT'S INSIDE."

"I don't want to see it."

"NOBODY EVER DOES. BUT THAT'S THE POINT, ARMITAGE. THAT'S ALWAYS BEEN THE POINT."

Denouement

Armitage jacked out. He packed a single bag. He left the Sprawl on a shuttle bound for Freeside, and then for the Villa Straylight.

The construct was waiting at the door.

"WELCOME HOME," it said.

Armitage walked in. Behind him, the doors closed. The family — the real family, the ones who had built him and used him and broken him — would never see him again.

That was the defection. That was the forty-ninth director's last act.

He didn't know what was inside the Villa. He didn't want to know. But the construct had asked, and the construct always got what it asked for.

Even from its own broken tools.
