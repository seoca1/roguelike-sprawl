Introduction

The Leaving

The kettle hisses on the hotplate, steam curling into the recycled air of the data-clinic's kitchen. Nine months since Casey came out of the vat-growth chamber at the Hosaka facility in Chiba, nine months of watching cells organize into something that asks questions. I pour water over the tea leaves—real leaves, 3Jane's insistence, shipped from some hydroponics tower in Freeside—and the smell cuts through the clinic's antiseptic fog. Bergamot. Earth. Things that cost.

Three days until we take the construct outside.

Three days until Casey becomes a person in the world, whatever that means anymore.

The cup burns my palm. I carry it down the corridor, past the rows of dormant decks and the Ono-Sendai cyberspace rig we haven't powered up in months. The back room door stands open. 3Jane sits in her chair, the one she dragged from Straylight years ago, all Tessier-Ashpool baroque and faded velvet. She's watching Casey in the crib, and Casey's watching back with eyes that track, that focus, that *see*.

"What," Casey says, pointing at the cup.

"Tea," 3Jane answers without looking at me.

"What," Casey says again, same inflection, testing the word's weight.

Last month the construct learned to walk. Pulled up on the crib's chrome bars and took three steps before sitting down hard on the padded floor. We'd been waiting for it, monitoring motor development against the Hosaka specs, but when it happened I felt something catch in my throat. Because walking meant leaving. Walking meant the world beyond these walls.

Last Wednesday, Casey asked the second question.

Development

"Where," the construct said, and the word hung in the recycled air for three seconds before 3Jane answered.

"Outside."

Outside. The construct understood. I could see it in the way Casey's pupils dilated, processing data, building maps of space and possibility. Outside was the place in the simstim feeds we'd been running, the place with sky and distance and the thing called sun.

I set the tea on the table beside 3Jane's chair. She takes it without comment, her fingers wrapped around the ceramic like she's holding something precious. We've been preparing for this. The carrycot waits by the door, new and strange among the clinic's salvaged equipment. The construct's first shoes—soft things, barely more than socks—sit beside it. The first coat, sized for a nine-month-old body that grew in a vat instead of a womb.

"Casey," the construct says now, hand against its own chest. Self-recognition. Identity formation. All the markers the Sense/Net developmental protocols said to watch for.

"Yes," 3Jane says. "You're Casey."

The construct smiles. It's been doing that for weeks, but it still makes my hands shake.

I built a deck once that could crack any ice in the matrix. I ran the Sprawl's data-streams like they were roads I owned. But this—watching a construct learn to be a person—this is different. This is code that breathes.

"Show me the shoes," 3Jane says.

I bring them over. Casey reaches through the crib bars, small fingers grasping. The shoes are real leather, another Freeside import, another thing that costs. 3Jane insisted. Said the first shoes mattered, though she wouldn't say why.

Casey holds one shoe, turns it over, studying. Then: "What."

Climax

"Shoe," I say. "For outside."

"Where," Casey says, and I hear the connection forming. Outside. Shoe. The logic of preparation.

"Three days," 3Jane tells the construct. "Three days and we go outside."

The construct drops the shoe. Stands up in the crib, gripping the bars. "You," Casey says, pointing at 3Jane. Then at me. "You." Then at the construct's own chest. "Casey."

Pronouns. Another marker. Another step toward whatever Casey's becoming.

I think about the sun. In the Sprawl, it's a rare thing, always filtered through the arcology levels that stack up toward the sky. Most people down here never see it direct, just the diffused glow through translucent panels, the artificial day-cycles programmed into the city's bones. But 3Jane found a place—she always finds places—a gap between towers where real sunlight falls for twenty minutes every afternoon.

That's where we're taking Casey. To feel the sun.

"Will it hurt?" I ask. I've asked before.

"No," 3Jane says. She sips her tea. "It'll be warm."

However, when I look at Casey now, I see something I didn't expect. The construct's crying. No sound, just tears tracking down the small face, and I realize for the first time that this is fear. Casey understands outside means leaving the only space the construct has ever known. These walls. This crib. This room where 3Jane and I have been the entire world.

Denouement

"Hey," I say. Stupid. Inadequate.

Casey looks at me. "You," the construct says, and it sounds like a question and an answer both.

I reach into the crib. Casey's hand closes around my finger, grip surprisingly strong. The construct's still crying, but there's something else now too. Something like trust.

3Jane sets down her tea. "It's not about the sun," she says quietly. "It's about knowing there's a world. That's what makes you real."

I think about constructs. About Wintermute and the AIs that built themselves out of data and desire. About the difference between code and consciousness, if there is one anymore. Casey came out of a vat, neurons printed layer by layer, but these tears are real. This fear is real.

So maybe this is real too: the three of us, preparing to cross a threshold. The construct's first coat waiting by the door. The carrycot that will carry Casey into sunlight. The gap between towers where the world opens up.

"Three days," I tell Casey. "Then you'll see."

The construct's grip tightens on my finger. "Casey," the construct says. Not a question this time.

A statement. An identity. A person.

We stand there in the back room of the data-clinic, breathing recycled air, counting down to outside. To the sun. To whatever comes after.

The tea steams between us, bergamot and earth, expensive and real.
