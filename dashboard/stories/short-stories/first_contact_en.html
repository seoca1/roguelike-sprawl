*"The matrix has its roots in primitive arcade games," said the voice-over, "in early graphics programs and military experimentation with cranial jacks."*

— Gibson, Neuromancer (opening)

전개

You're not surprised, exactly. The Finn mentioned him. "Dixie's my backup," he said. "If things go sideways, he's got your back." But seeing him is different. He's more solid than you expected, more present. A construct is never quite real, but Dixie comes close.

He's standing at a node junction, arms crossed, watching you like you're a puzzle he's trying to solve. His avatar is simple: a cowboy hat, a leather jacket, a face that's seen too much. The lines around his eyes are deep, like they were carved there by time.

"So you're the new one," he says. Not a question.

You nod. "Just starting out."

"Yeah." He looks you over. "Everyone starts somewhere."

절정

The data streams flow around you both, but there's a strange stillness in Dixie. A dead man in a living world. You wonder what that's like—what it's like to know you're a copy, a ghost in someone else's machine.

"Finn says you're good," Dixie says. "Says you've got the hands for this work. The timing." He pauses. "He's usually right about that kind of thing."

"What about you?" you ask. "How'd you end up here?"

Dixie's smile is thin. "Same way everyone ends up in the matrix. Someone needed something, and I was the only one who could get it." He looks away. "I got it. They got me."

You don't ask what he means. Some stories aren't worth telling.

"Listen," Dixie says, turning back to you. "The matrix is dangerous. Not just the ICE—the corps, the other jockeys, the whole game. Finn's a good fixer, but he's got his own agenda. Everyone does." He reaches out, puts a hand on your shoulder. His touch is cold, electric. "Watch your back. Trust your instincts. And don't jack in unless you've got an exit strategy."

결말

You nod. "Thanks."

"Don't thank me yet." Dixie's smile widens, but there's something sad in it. "Thank me when you're still running."

He fades out, dissolving into the data stream like he was never there. But his words stay with you, sharp and cold as the matrix itself.

You start moving.

The node junction is ahead, pulsing with the soft glow of the network's core. You have a job to do—data to extract, credits to earn. But as you move, you can't help but think about Dixie. About what it means to be alive in a world where the dead can walk.

The matrix hums around you, full of ghosts.

You keep moving.
