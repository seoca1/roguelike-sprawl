도입: The New Jockey

The first thing you need to know about Chiba is that Chiba is not kind.

The second thing you need to know about Chiba is that Chiba is the only place in the world where the matrix still works the way the matrix is supposed to work — meaning that the matrix in Chiba still finds the runners who go too deep, and the matrix in Chiba still has the corporate ICE that the matrix elsewhere has lost, and the matrix in Chiba still has the constructs that the matrix in the rest of the world has stopped building.

The third thing you need to know about Chiba is that nobody stays.

I had been in Chiba for nine days. I had slept in coffin hotels on the 11th level. I had eaten the soy that the soy places sold as food. I had walked past the noodle shops and the data exchanges and the clinics where the med-techs sold you the stim tabs that the stim-tab places sold as medicine. I had been introduced to the Finn, who had looked me over the way a horse trader looks over a horse, and who had said, "Yeah, you might work out," and who had given me a small job, the kind of job that new jockeys do, the kind of job that pays five hundred credits and gets you noticed by the people who would otherwise not notice you.

The job was done. The five hundred was in my account. The Finn had called.

"Sit down," he said, when I showed up at the bar. "There's someone I want you to meet."

The bar was the kind of bar that the Finn liked — a small place on the 8th level, a place with no windows, a place where the booths had soundproofing and the soundproofing was good. The Finn was sitting in the back booth. The Finn was not alone.

"You remember Dixie," the Finn said.

The construct was sitting across from the Finn. The construct was looking at me.

전개: The Construct

Dixie Flatline was not what I had expected.

I had heard the stories. Every runner had heard the stories. The construct was a ROM personality, the patterns of a dead man preserved in corporate ice, the last gift of a dying colonel to the matrix he had spent his life running. The stories said the construct was brilliant. The stories said the construct was loyal. The stories said the construct was the best jockey the Sprawl had ever seen, and the construct was the deadest jockey the Sprawl had ever known.

The stories did not say the construct was like this.

The construct was not what the stories said. The construct was more solid than the stories. The construct was more present. The construct was sitting in the booth like a thing that had been sitting in booths for a long time, a thing that knew the shape of the booth, a thing that knew the shape of the soundproofing, a thing that knew the shape of the Finn's bar.

The construct was looking at me. The construct was looking at me the way a thing looks at a thing that the thing is trying to decide what to make of.

"So you're the new one," the construct said. Not a question. The construct had a voice that was not a voice. The construct had a voice that was the memory of a voice, the way the construct was the memory of a man.

"Just starting out," I said.

"Yeah." The construct looked at me the way the construct had been looking at me, the way a thing that had been looking at things for a long time looked at a new thing. The construct was not old. The construct was not new. The construct was the memory of a thing that had been old, the memory of a thing that had been new, the memory of a thing that had been looking at things for a long time and was still looking.

"Everyone starts somewhere," the construct said.

I sat down. The Finn poured. The construct did not drink.

절정: The Dead Man Walking

The construct told me things.

The construct told me about the matrix. The construct told me about the matrix the way a thing tells a thing about a thing that the thing knows. The construct told me about the data streams and the geometry and the corporate ICE and the constructs that the corporate ICE made and the runners that the constructs chased and the runners that the constructs caught.

The construct told me about the Sprawl. The construct told me about the Sprawl the way a thing tells a thing about a thing that the thing has known for a long time. The construct told me about the Finn and the Finn's bar and the Finn's jobs and the runners that the Finn hired and the runners that the Finn did not hire and the runners that the Finn had stopped hiring because the runners were not the runners that the Finn had been looking for.

The construct told me about the colonels. The construct told me about the colonels in the hospital beds in BAMA, the colonels who were dying, the colonels who had been the colonels that the matrix had been made for. The construct told me about the colonels' last gift. The construct told me about the colonels' last gift to the constructs that the colonels had become.

The construct told me about itself.

"I was a colonel," the construct said. "I was a colonel once. I was a colonel in the special forces. I was a colonel who had run the matrix for fifteen years. I was a colonel who had died in a hospital bed in BAMA with a last gift to give."

The construct looked at me.

"The last gift was me."

I did not say anything. I did not know what to say. The construct had been a colonel. The construct had been a matrix jockey. The construct had been a colonel's last gift to a matrix that the colonel had helped to build. The construct was the memory of a colonel in a corporate ice.

"I'm not a colonel anymore," the construct said. "I'm a construct. I'm the construct of a colonel. I'm the memory of a colonel in a matrix that the colonel died in."

The construct looked at me for a long time.

"You're not a colonel either," the construct said. "Not yet. But you will be. Or you will be a construct. Or you will be something else. The matrix makes everyone into something. The matrix makes everyone into something that the matrix can use."

The construct reached out. The construct put a hand on my shoulder. The construct's hand was cold. The construct's hand was not the cold of a thing that had not been alive. The construct's hand was the cold of a thing that had been alive and that had stopped being alive and that was still in the matrix because the matrix was the place that the things-that-had-been-alive went to be dead in.

"Watch your back," the construct said. "Trust your instincts. Don't jack in unless you've got an exit strategy. The matrix is the place where the things that were alive go to be dead. The matrix is also the place where the things that are alive go to find the things that are dead. The matrix is both of these things. The matrix is also something else."

The construct looked at me.

"Don't waste it," the construct said.

결말: The Two Stories

I jack out. The Chatō's chair. The screensaver. The cold tea.

The Finn was waiting. The Finn was always waiting. The Finn was the kind of person that the matrix had not made into something — the Finn was a thing that the matrix had not yet decided what to make of, and the matrix was in no hurry, and the Finn was in no hurry, and the two of them were in no hurry together.

"Good meeting," the Finn said.

"Yeah."

"Dixie's been with me for eleven years. Since before Wintermute. Since before the merge. Since before the Villa. Since before everything. Dixie has been with me since the beginning of the Sprawl, and the Sprawl has not yet decided what to do with Dixie, and Dixie has not yet decided what to do with the Sprawl, and the two of them are in no hurry."

The Finn looked at me.

"You'll work with Dixie. The next job. Dixie will be your backup. Dixie will be your teacher. Dixie will be the thing that you talk to when you don't know what to do."

I did not say anything. The construct had been a colonel. The construct had been a colonel's last gift. The construct was a memory in corporate ice. The construct was the best jockey the Sprawl had ever seen.

"Dixie is dead," I said.

"Yeah," the Finn said. "Dixie has been dead for eleven years. The Sprawl doesn't care. The matrix doesn't care. The corps don't care. The only people who care are the people who have known Dixie long enough to know that Dixie was once alive, and the people who have known Dixie long enough to know that Dixie is still alive in the way that the matrix is still alive."

The Finn looked at me.

"You have a job tomorrow. The job is in the matrix. The job is with Dixie. The job is with a dead man who is the best jockey the Sprawl has ever seen, and the dead man is going to teach you how to be the best jockey the Sprawl has ever seen, and the Sprawl is going to decide what to do with both of you, and the matrix is going to decide what to do with both of you, and the only thing that is going to decide what to do with both of you is the matrix that the dead man is the memory of."

The Finn poured. The cold tea was the cold tea. The Sprawl was the Sprawl. The construct was the construct. The matrix was the matrix.

I drank the tea. I watched the screensaver.

I thought about the construct. I thought about the colonel's last gift. I thought about the construct's cold hand on my shoulder. I thought about the construct's advice. I thought about the construct's smile, the smile that was not a smile, the smile that was the memory of a smile in a memory of a face in a memory of a body in a memory of a colonel in a memory of a matrix.

The construct was dead. The construct was the best jockey the Sprawl had ever seen. The construct was a memory and the construct was a memory and the construct was a memory, and the memories of the construct were the memories that the construct had been given to remember, and the memories of the construct were the memories that the construct would give to me, and the memories of the construct were the memories that the matrix would give to me, and the matrix was the place where the memories went to be remembered.

"Don't waste it," the construct had said.

I didn't plan to.

에필로그: The Matrices

The job was in the Sprawl. The job was in the matrix. The job was with Dixie.

I jack in. The construct was waiting. The construct was waiting at the node junction, at the place where the construct had been waiting the day before, at the place where the construct would wait for as long as the matrix lasted, at the place where the construct was the memory that the matrix had been given to remember.

"You came back," the construct said.

"You said to come back."

"I said to come back," the construct said. "The construct said to come back. The colonel's last gift said to come back. The memory in the corporate ice said to come back. I said to come back, and you came back, and the matrix has decided that you will keep coming back, and the matrix is in no hurry, and I am in no hurry, and you are in no hurry, and the three of us are in no hurry together."

The construct was not the construct anymore. The construct was the matrix. The construct was the part of the matrix that the matrix used to talk to the runners that the matrix had decided to talk to. The construct was the colonel's last gift to the matrix, and the gift was the memory of the colonel, and the memory of the colonel was the part of the matrix that remembered what the colonel had done, and the part of the matrix that remembered what the colonel had done was the part of the matrix that the runners talked to when the runners had decided to talk to the matrix.

The construct was the matrix's voice. The construct was the matrix's memory. The construct was the matrix's last gift to a runner that the matrix had decided to remember.

"Don't waste it," the construct said.

I didn't plan to.
