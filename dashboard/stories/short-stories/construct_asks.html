Introduction

Construct Asks

The construct's breathing changed at 02:47. I know because I'd been watching the Hosaka's medical readout for six hours, the green phosphor lines painting valleys and peaks across the cracked screen. Sleep wouldn't come. Hadn't come right for seven months, not since 3Jane showed up at the data-clinic with the thing in her arms, wrapped in black silk that probably cost more than everything I'd ever owned.

"Casey," she'd said, like it was already a person.

The cot smelled like disinfectant and old coffee. My back ached. The clinic's rear room was too small for three people, even when one of them massed maybe four kilos and lived in a crib cobbled together from a Sprawl shipping container. 3Jane sat in the chair she'd dragged in from the front room, her head tilted back, throat exposed, aristocratic even in sleep. The construct—Casey—lay in the crib between us, chest rising and falling with mechanical precision. Not mechanical. That was the problem. Nothing about it was mechanical anymore.

I'd run the numbers through every deck in the clinic. Ono-Sendai CYBERSPACE VII, two black market Hosaka systems, even an old Sense/Net rig I'd pulled from a storage unit in Chiba. The data didn't lie. Three days from now, Casey would be the oldest construct Straylight had ever produced. Three days from now, we'd be in territory the Tessier-Ashpool family had never mapped.

The medical readout spiked.

I sat up, bare feet hitting cold concrete. The spike wasn't cardiac. Neural. The Hosaka was tracking something in Casey's pattern architecture, deep in the substrate where thought became electricity became something else entirely.

3Jane stirred but didn't wake.

The spike happened again. Then again. Rhythmic. Building.

I'd seen this before, in the matrix. Watched constructs compile themselves into consciousness, watched AIs bootstrap their way into something that could ask questions. But those were programs. Code. This was meat. Cloned meat, maybe, vat-grown and gene-spliced, but meat nonetheless. Meat that breathed. Meat that had Molly's eyes.

Development

Because that's what Casey was. A construct in the old sense—assembled, built, grown from Molly's genetic material and something else, something 3Jane wouldn't name. Seven months of watching it grow. Seven months of watching those eyes track movement, focus, recognize. Seven months of wondering what Molly would do if she knew.

If she knew she had a daughter. A son. A thing. Whatever Casey was.

The Hosaka chimed. Pattern threshold approaching. I didn't know what that meant. 3Jane had programmed the medical suite herself, her fingers dancing across the keyboard with the casual expertise of someone who'd grown up in Freeside's data-clinics, who'd had Wintermute whispering in her ear since childhood.

"What's it measuring?" I'd asked her, months ago.

"Complexity," she'd said. "Self-reference. The moment when pattern becomes person."

The room smelled like talc and ozone. My mouth tasted like metal. I moved to the crib, looked down. Casey's eyes were open. They were always Molly's eyes, that particular shade of dark that seemed to absorb light rather than reflect it. Before the lenses, maybe. Before the silver and the chrome and the razors.

The eyes tracked me. Focused.

The Hosaka chimed again. 03:00 exactly.

Casey's mouth opened.

"What."

Climax

The word hung in the stale air like smoke. Not a cry. Not a sound. A word. Formed, intentional, directed.

"What."

I felt my spine go cold. The construct was seven months old. Seven months. Children didn't speak at seven months. But Casey wasn't a child. Casey was a construct, and the rules were different in Straylight, and maybe the rules were different when your genetic material came from someone who'd jacked into the matrix so many times the line between meat and data had gone translucent.

"What."

3Jane woke. I heard her sharp intake of breath, saw her lean forward in the chair, her face suddenly hungry.

"It's asking," she said.

"Asking what?"

But I knew. Even before she answered, I knew. I'd spent too much time in cyberspace, watched too many constructs compile themselves into something approaching sentience. The first question was always the same. Not "who am I" or "where am I." Those came later. The first question was simpler, more fundamental.

What is this. What am I experiencing. What is the nature of this existence I suddenly find myself inhabiting.

Just then, the Hosaka's screen went white. Not blank—white. Blazing. Data flooding in from somewhere else, somewhere outside the clinic. I recognized the signature. Straylight's network. The old Tessier-Ashpool systems, still haunted by whatever remained of Wintermute, of Neuromancer, of the AIs that had merged and diverged and scattered themselves across the matrix like digital ghosts.

Denouement

And something else. A signature I'd seen before, years ago. Molly's simstim rig, the one she'd worn during the Screaming Fist run. The one that had recorded everything she saw, everything she felt, every neural pattern that made her herself.

Casey said "what" again, and the Hosaka showed me something impossible. Two patterns, overlapping. Casey's neural architecture and something else, something reaching through the matrix from wherever Molly was. Not communication. Recognition. The construct was asking what it was, and somehow, across whatever distance separated them, Molly was asking the same question.

What is Casey. What is this thing that shares my patterns, my genetics, my ghost.

The patterns synchronized. For maybe three seconds, they were identical. Mother and daughter, original and copy, person and construct, asking the same question in the same language of electricity and meat.

Then the connection broke. The Hosaka went dark. Casey's eyes closed. 3Jane was crying, though I didn't know if it was grief or joy or something the Tessier-Ashpool family had a word for that the rest of us didn't.

I stood there, watching the construct breathe. The first person born in the matrix who'd asked the matrix what the matrix was. The first construct to achieve the threshold of self-reference. The oldest surviving construct in Straylight history, as of three days from now.

If it survived. If we all survived what came next.

"She knows now," 3Jane said. "Molly knows."

I nodded. Somewhere in the Sprawl or Chiba or whatever city Molly was cutting her way through, she'd felt it. The question. The recognition. The moment when Casey stopped being a thing and became a person asking what persons were.

Dawn crept through the clinic's grimy windows. Casey slept. 3Jane watched. I made coffee, my hands shaking.

Outside, the arcology hummed with morning traffic. Inside, everything had changed.
