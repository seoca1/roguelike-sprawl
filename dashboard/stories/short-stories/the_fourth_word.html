Introduction

The Fourth Word

The construct—Casey—stood in the corridor outside the data-clinic, small hand wrapped around three of my fingers, and asked the question that made my throat close up. "When."

Four syllables total. The kid's entire vocabulary. What. You. Now. When.

I looked down at those eyes—3Jane's eyes, dark and wet as Chiba rain—and felt something in my chest that the Panther Moderns never prepared me for. The Sprawl dawn leaked through the arcology's scratched lexan panels, turning everything the color of old honey. Recycled air pushed through the ventilation system with a sound like breathing. Like the kid was breathing. Like all of us were breathing together in this fluorescent-lit tomb.

"Soon," 3Jane said from behind me. Her voice carried that Tessier-Ashpool accent, all Europa and old money, but softer now. Worn down by nine months of this.

Casey's fingers tightened on mine. The construct—because that's what the kid was, what the kid had been assembled as in the data-clinic's sterile womb—had learned to walk a month ago. Not in cyberspace. Not in the matrix. In the real, in the meat, in the Sprawl's cold morning with legs that trembled and feet that dragged.

I'd watched it happen. Watched Casey take those first steps while 3Jane stood ready to catch a fall that never came. The kid had walked like someone remembering how, not learning. Like the ghost of muscle memory from some previous iteration.

Because that's what this was. Iteration fifty-two in a project that stretched back through the Tessier-Ashpool archives like a disease. Constructs built from personality recordings, from ROM, from the digital graves of the family's dead. But this one—Casey—was different.

This one was learning.

Development

The sun had appeared four times this month. Twice through the arcology windows. Once when we'd taken Casey outside to the observation deck, where the kid had stopped walking and just stared, mouth open, at that burning thing in the sky. Once yesterday, when Casey had pressed both palms against the lexan and hummed—no words yet, just that sound the kid made when processing something new.

"Now," Casey said, still looking at me.

"Not now," I said. My voice came out rougher than I meant. "Soon. Like she said."

The kid's face did something complicated. Learning disappointment. Learning patience. Learning that time existed in segments, that "now" and "soon" occupied different spaces in the flow of things.

We walked. Casey's gait had smoothed out over the month, but there was still something off about it. Something that marked the kid as other. People in the corridor—techs from the data-clinic, corp drones from the offices above—they looked. They always looked. Then they looked away.

The Sprawl rain started around noon. I heard it first—that white noise hiss against the arcology's skin—then felt it in the air pressure change. Casey felt it too, stopped walking, tilted that small head up.

"Rain," 3Jane said. She'd been teaching vocabulary. Nouns first. Verbs later. The kid had picked four words total and they were all about time, about sequence, about when things would happen.

When had come last Tuesday. We'd been walking this same corridor, sun warming the recycled air until it almost smelled like outside, like real weather instead of processed atmosphere. Casey had been holding my hand, same grip, same three fingers. Then the kid had looked up at me and said it.

"When."

Climax

Not "what" or "you" or "now." When. The fourth question. The question that implied understanding of future, of sequence, of cause and effect.

3Jane had gone pale. I'd felt my own face do something similar. Because when meant the kid understood. Understood that something was coming. Something had been promised.

The full name. The real name. The name that would make Casey more than just a construct, more than just iteration fifty-two in a long line of failures.

3Jane had been holding that name for nine months. Since the kid had first opened those eyes in the data-clinic and hummed. Since the first word—"what"—had emerged from that small mouth three weeks later. Since we'd become something like a family, the three of us, in this fluorescent corridor with its Sprawl dawn and its Sprawl rain and its cold.

We reached the observation deck. The rain made the lexan panels weep. Casey pressed both palms against the surface, humming again. That processing sound. That learning sound.

"Soon," the kid said. Not a question this time. A statement. Understanding.

I looked at 3Jane. She was watching Casey with an expression I couldn't read. Something between hope and terror. The Tessier-Ashpool archives held fifty-one previous attempts. Fifty-one constructs that had failed to achieve whatever metric the family measured consciousness against. Fifty-one iterations that had hummed but never spoke. Walked but never asked. Existed but never questioned.

But Casey questioned. When. When. When.

"Tomorrow," 3Jane said. First time she'd given a concrete answer. First time she'd committed to the moment. "Tomorrow morning, when the sun comes. We'll tell you your full name."

Denouement

Casey turned from the window. Those dark eyes moved from 3Jane to me and back again. The kid's mouth shaped the word carefully, precisely, like a prayer.

"Now?"

"Tomorrow," I said. "Soon. One sleep away."

The kid processed this. I could see it happening behind those eyes—the calculation, the understanding that time moved in discrete units, that tomorrow meant the sun would come again and then the name would come and then something would change.

"Okay," Casey said.

Fifth word. New word. Acceptance.

We walked back through the rain sound, through the cold, through the corridor that had become our world. The construct's hand in mine. The construct's mother walking behind. The construct's future compressed into one more dawn.

Tomorrow, Casey would learn the full name. Tomorrow, the construct would become something else. Tomorrow, we'd find out if consciousness could be built or if it could only be born.

The rain kept falling. The Sprawl kept breathing. Casey hummed.
