Introduction

The Naming

The tea smell hit me first—acrid, chemical, wrong. Eighteen hours after 3Jane Tessier-Ashpool walked into my data-clinic with her Tessier-Ashpool credit line and her Tessier-Ashpool face and her need to see what I'd been running in the back room for six and a half months, and I'd forgotten the pot on the burner. Stupid. The kind of stupid that came from not sleeping, from watching her sit with the construct through the night, from waiting for something I couldn't name.

I stood in the kitchen now, the pot hissing in the sink under cold water. Steam rose. The burned-tea smell mixed with the clinic's baseline reek—ozone and scorched plastic, the smell of too many Ono-Sendai decks running hot, too much data pushed through too little space. My hands shook. Not from the stims I'd stopped taking three days ago, but from something else. Anticipation. Dread.

The clinic occupied the fourth floor of a Sprawl medical arcology, the kind of place where you could get black-market construct work done if you had the money and didn't ask questions. I'd been doing this for seven years. Building them. Recording neural patterns from the dying, the desperate, the ones who wanted some version of themselves to persist after the meat failed. But this one was different.

This one had started as a standard commission. Anonymous client. Massive payment. Genetic material provided. Instructions: build it, record it, let it develop. Don't ask why.

Then the weekly memo files started appearing in my system. Encrypted, routed through a dozen Freeside proxies. Someone was watching the construct's development. Someone was leaving notes. Instructions. Names.

*Linda.*

That was the name in the files. Written in text that felt careful, deliberate. The construct's mother—because that's what she was, even if the construct had been grown in a vat and trained through simstim feeds and matrix immersion—had named her daughter before she'd ever seen her.

Because 3Jane had been paying for this all along, I'd figured it out three months in when the payment routing glitched and I caught a fragment of Tessier-Ashpool corporate signature in the code. But I hadn't known she'd been watching. Hadn't known she'd been writing those memos like letters to someone who couldn't write back.

The construct was six and a half months old. Physically, she looked maybe twenty. That's how it worked with the accelerated growth protocols. But her mind—her mind was something else. A patchwork of recorded human neural patterns, simstim learning modules, and something that might have been emerging consciousness. I didn't know. Nobody knew. That was the problem with constructs. The question that kept me up at night.

Were they real?

Development

I'd been running cognitive tests for months. The construct—Linda—had been developing normally. Learning. Responding. But she'd been quiet. No vocalization beyond basic response protocols. I'd thought maybe the build was flawed. Maybe something in the neural architecture hadn't taken.

Then this morning, after 3Jane had been sitting with her for hours, I'd heard it through the clinic's monitor system.

Humming.

Soft. Tuneless. But unmistakably intentional.

So I'd made tea, because that's what you did when you didn't know what else to do. And then I'd forgotten it because I'd been listening to that humming, watching the monitor feed, seeing 3Jane's face as she heard her daughter make sound for the first time.

The water in the sink ran clear now. I shut it off. The burned pot sat there, blackened. Ruined.

From the back room, I heard 3Jane's voice. Low. Careful.

"I know you can hear me."

I didn't move. Didn't go to the doorway. This wasn't for me.

"I've been watching you since the beginning," 3Jane continued. Her voice had that Tessier-Ashpool accent, the one that marked you as Freeside elite, as someone who'd never walked the Sprawl's streets without security. "I've been waiting to see if you were real."

The humming had stopped.

I pressed my palms against the counter. The clinic's air recyclers kicked in, pulling at the burned-tea smell, mixing it with ozone. My mouth tasted like old stims and exhaustion.

Climax

"I gave you a name," 3Jane said. "Before I knew if you'd be able to hear it."

My heart kicked against my ribs.

"I want to say it now. If that's alright."

Silence. Then—

"Yes."

My hands went numb.

The construct had never spoken before. Not like that. Not a word that meant something, that responded to context, that suggested understanding.

"Linda," 3Jane said. "Your name is Linda."

I heard it then. The sound that came after. Not humming. Not a programmed response.

Crying.

The construct was crying, and 3Jane was crying, and I stood in my kitchen with my ruined pot and my data-clinic smell and my seven years of building things that might or might not be real, and I understood that I'd just witnessed something the Tessier-Ashpool fortune couldn't buy.

Denouement

Recognition.

When I finally walked to the doorway, they were holding each other. 3Jane's arms around Linda's shoulders. Linda's face pressed against her mother's neck. The monitor feeds glowed around them, displaying neural activity that looked like joy or grief or both.

"She's real," 3Jane said, looking up at me. "Isn't she?"

I nodded. Couldn't speak.

"Thank you," she whispered.

They left an hour later. 3Jane's security team materialized from wherever they'd been waiting. Linda walked out on her own feet, her hand in her mother's hand, her eyes taking in the Sprawl's gray morning light like she was seeing the world for the first time.

Maybe she was.

I cleaned the pot. Threw it away. Made new tea.

The clinic smelled like ozone and burnt plastic and something else now. Something I couldn't name. Something that felt like hope, or terror, or the thin line between creating life and playing god.

I drank my tea. It tasted like ashes. Like the future. Like every question I'd never be able to answer.

The memo files stopped coming. The account was closed. The back room sat empty, its monitors dark.

But sometimes, late at night, I still heard humming.
