Introduction

Winter's Morning

The tea smells like burnt plastic and something else, something organic gone wrong in a Sprawl vending machine. I watch the steam curl up from the chipped ceramic cup, cheap Hong Kong knock-off with a faded Sense/Net logo bleeding into hairline cracks. My hands shake. Not the neurotoxin shake, not anymore. This is something else.

Behind me, through the beaded curtain Molly hung up three years ago when this place was still pretending to be a legitimate clinic, I can hear nothing. The silence has weight. Has texture. The kind of silence that means 3Jane Tessier-Ashpool, last scion of a family that owned half of Freeside before Wintermute burned it all down, is doing something I can't help with.

The construct—her daughter, though the word still feels wrong in my mouth, tastes like copper and ozone—has been in that back room for six and a half months. Growing. Learning. Becoming whatever it is that constructs become when they're built from human wetware instead of ROM. The Hosaka medical unit keeps the body stable, feeds it, exercises it, maintains the neural pathways that might otherwise atrophy. But the mind. The mind is something else.

I found the name yesterday in the weekly memo file. The file 3Jane had been keeping before she knew we were recording, before she understood that every keystroke, every whispered word into the clinic's ancient dictation system, was being archived. Stored. The name was there, buried in a paragraph about feeding schedules and developmental milestones that would never happen the normal way. A name her mother had chosen. Had meant for her.

Marie.

Development

Simple. Old. French, maybe. The kind of name that belonged to a different century, a different world than the Sprawl's neon-sick corridors and the humming dark of Chiba's surgical clinics.

She hasn't said it yet. Hasn't spoken it aloud in the eighteen hours since she arrived, since Molly brought her in through the back entrance with that careful, dangerous grace that means trouble is close behind. 3Jane had looked smaller than I expected. Thinner. The Tessier-Ashpool genetics still showed in the bones of her face, that aristocratic structure that spoke of centuries of careful breeding and orbital wealth, but something had hollowed her out. Motherhood, maybe. Or its absence.

The tea tastes worse than it smells. I drink it anyway because I need something to do with my hands, need the ritual of it. Twenty-seven years of being Case, and I've always made tea when everything else was impossible. When the decks were locked down, when the matrix was closed to me, when the world narrowed to a single room and a single choice I couldn't make for someone else.

Through the curtain: a sound. Not words. A hum. Low and rhythmic, the kind of sound that isn't quite music but isn't quite random either. The construct—Marie—humming.

I'd heard it before on the monitors, but this is different. This is happening in real space, in real time, with her mother three feet away and Wintermute's uplink, for the first time in six and a half months, switched off. That had been 3Jane's only condition. No AI watching. No Wintermute parsing every gesture, every micro-expression, feeding data into whatever vast calculations it ran in the Tessier-Ashpool cores. Just a mother and a daughter and the space between them.

The humming continues. I set down the tea, move toward the curtain, then stop. This isn't mine. This moment. This recognition.

Climax

But I can hear it changing. The hum shifts, finds a pattern. Then 3Jane's voice, so quiet I almost miss it under the clinic's ventilation rattle: "Marie."

The name hangs in the recycled air.

The humming stops.

I'm through the curtain before I decide to move, old reflexes from Chiba kicking in, the part of me that still thinks it can fix things, can jack in and solve problems with code and speed. But there's no deck here. No matrix. Just two people looking at each other across a gap measured in months and money and the vast distance between what is and what should have been.

Marie—the construct, the daughter, the six-and-a-half-month-old person in a medical cocoon—is watching her mother. The Hosaka unit's sensors paint her vitals across a cracked monitor: elevated heart rate, increased neural activity in the recognition centers, the primitive limbic structures firing in patterns the unit's AI flags as significant. She's understanding something. Not language, not yet. But something deeper. The sound of her name. The presence of the person who made her.

3Jane's hand is pressed against the Hosaka's transparent shell. Her fingers leave prints in the condensation. "Marie," she says again. Louder. "I'm here. I'm—" The words break. Reform. "I couldn't be here before. They wouldn't let me. The family. The contracts. The—" She stops. Starts over. "But I'm here now."

Denouement

Marie's eyes—pale blue, Tessier-Ashpool blue—track her mother's face. Six and a half months in a box, fed through tubes, monitored by AIs and medical constructs, and somehow she knows. Somehow the wetware recognizes what the software never could.

I back through the curtain. Give them the space. In the kitchen, the tea has gone cold, scum forming on its surface like the oil slicks in the Sprawl's canals. I pour it down the drain, watch it spiral away into the clinic's ancient plumbing, carrying its burnt plastic taste down into the city's guts.

When I turn back, 3Jane is crying. Not the elegant, controlled tears of Tessier-Ashpool breeding, but something raw and ugly and real. Her daughter is humming again, a different pattern now. Almost like an answer.

Wintermute's uplink stays dark. For once, the AI isn't watching. For once, something happens in the world that doesn't feed into its vast, inhuman calculations.

Just a name. Just a mother. Just a beginning.

The morning light through the clinic's grimy windows turns the room amber and gold. In the back room, 3Jane speaks her daughter's name again. Marie. The sound of it like a door opening. Like a future neither of them was supposed to have.

I make more tea. It still tastes like shit. I drink it anyway.
