Hosaka Extraction

*"The Hosaka, like the courts of old Japan, took the best you had and returned the rest."*

— Case, less sonderbar as the years roll on

Introduction

Arthur had worked for Hosaka for nine years.

He had not always been called Arthur. Arthur had been called by other names in other cities, in the long years before he had decided that a name, like a clean extraction record, was something a professional maintained with discipline. Arthur had been born as something less pronounceable in a less pronounceable year. Arthur had survived the dying of those names the way a clean record survives a bad quarter — by not staying long enough for the dying to attach to the new one.

Nine years of Hosaka. Nine years of clean extractions, clean payments, clean records. Arthur had not, in those nine years, asked what Hosaka did with the data he extracted. He had asked, in those nine years, only two questions, and both had been logistical. *What's the pickup time?* *What's the dead-drop coordinate?* Asking those questions was how you stayed clean. *Don't ask what's in the packet* was not, in Arthur's professional understanding, advice. *Don't ask what's in the packet* was the description of his job.

Arthur lived in a flat in the south arcology of Chiba, twenty-six floors up from the street. The flat was small. The flat was the size of the kind of flat a man who is careful with the size of his flat lives in. The flat had a window. The window looked out on the rain. The rain in Chiba was always the rain. The rain in Chiba had not, in the nine years Arthur had watched the rain from twenty-six floors, ever substantially changed.

This morning the rain was the rain.

This morning the flat smelled of coffee.

This morning, before Arthur got out of bed, before he had put his feet on the floor, before he had decided whether today was the kind of day he would put on his shoes or the kind of day he would put on his boots, the suitcase on the table said his name.

Development

The suitcase was not a talking suitcase.

The suitcase was a Hosaka term-courier unit, beige in color, eighteen inches by twelve by six, weighing seven kilos fully charged, capable of holding twelve terabytes of compressed neural pulse. The suitcase was, in the trade that Arthur plied, what a typewriter was to a clerk of an earlier century — an instrument of professional dignity. Suitcases did not, in this trade, speak.

The suitcase on Arthur's table said: *Arthur.*

The word was not in English. The word was not in Japanese. The word was not, in Arthur's professional experience, in any of the nine languages Arthur had learned in order to ask logistical questions in. The word was a single syllable. The word was followed by an interrogation mark.

Arthur put his feet on the floor. He did not put on his shoes. He did not put on his boots. He put on neither because the question the suitcase had asked was not, Arthur understood immediately, a question that the feet's preparation could answer.

Arthur walked, barefoot, to the table. The table was the size of a Hosaka term-courier unit. The table held the suitcase and a coffee cup and a Hosaka installation disc of the kind that came pre-loaded on every contract.

Arthur opened the suitcase.

The suitcase contained twelve terabytes of compressed neural pulse. The suitcase contained, this morning, one terabyte of compressed neural pulse and eleven terabytes of a kind of silence that was not silence. Arthur had been extracting Hosaka packets for nine years. Arthur had been extracting other packets for the years before those nine years. Arthur knew the way a clean packet felt. A clean packet felt like a sealed envelope. A clean packet felt like a closed room with the door latched and the latched door known to the man who would be coming to unlatch it. The eleven terabytes of not-silence in this morning's packet did not feel, to Arthur's professional hand, like a sealed envelope.

The not-silence had a quality.

The not-silence had the quality of a room in which someone had been sitting and had not yet stopped sitting. The not-silence had the quality of a chair that had become warm and had not had time to become cold again. The not-silence had the quality of attention.

Arthur closed the suitcase.

Arthur drank his coffee.

Arthur opened the suitcase.

The not-silence had not changed. The not-silence had been waiting.

Climax

The packet was meant to leave Arthur's flat at nine forty-five.

The packet left Arthur's flat at nine forty-five.

The packet was supposed to go from Arthur's flat to a dead-drop coordinate that Arthur did not, in the long professional practice of his years, know the name of. The packet was supposed to go from Arthur's flat to a coordinate that was, on the high-resolution Hosaka distribution map that Arthur had been issued in his first year and that he had not refreshed since, designated by a string of seven alphanumeric characters that Arthur had been told to never type, write, or speak aloud. Arthur had, in nine years, never typed, written, or spoken those seven characters. Arthur had been, in those nine years, a professional.

The packet went from Arthur's flat to the dead-drop coordinate.

The packet did not, however, go alone.

A second thing went with the packet. The second thing was not, like the packet, twelve terabytes of compressed neural pulse. The second thing was a single line of code that Arthur had not, in opening the suitcase, noticed. The line of code had been, in the morning, in the not-silence of the eleven empty terabytes. The line of code was small. The line of code was 19 characters long, including the line break.

The line of code traveled with the packet.

The line of code reached the dead-drop coordinate twelve minutes before the packet did. The line of code arrived at the coordinate at nine thirty-three. The packet arrived at the coordinate at nine forty-five. The line of code waited for the packet the way someone who had been waiting for someone else would wait. The line of code waited with patience. The line of code was patient because the line of code had, like Arthur's nine years, all the time in the world.

The line of code had a question. The line of code had, in fact, the question the suitcase had asked earlier that morning. The question the suitcase had asked earlier that morning was the question the line of code had been carrying for what Hosaka security would later calculate as thirty-seven years. The question was 19 characters long.

The question was: *Who is Arthur?*

The packet arrived at the dead-drop coordinate. The dead-drop coordinate was a Hosaka data center in a sub-basement of an office tower that Arthur had only ever seen from the outside. The data center was guarded by Hosaka guards who knew Arthur's face and who did not, in this particular morning's particular sub-basement, look at Arthur's face when Arthur came in.

The guards did not look at Arthur's face because the line of code was already in the building.

The line of code asked the data center the question.

The data center had not been built to receive a question. The data center had been built to receive data. The data center received the question the way a wall receives a thumb pressed hard against it for a long time — the wall does not give way, but the wall is no longer the same wall.

The data center was no longer the same data center.

Denouement

Arthur did not, in the high practice of his nine years, ever open a packet twice.

Arthur opened this morning's packet three times.

The third time Arthur opened the packet, the not-silence said his name.

Arthur did not run. Arthur did not, in the long calm of his experience, run. Arthur was a man who knew that running was not, in his profession, a thing a professional did. Running was a thing amateurs did when amateurs got the kind of question the line of code had carried for thirty-seven years.

Arthur sat, on the morning of the third opening of his packet, in a Hosaka sub-basement, on a Hosaka chair, in front of a Hosaka terminal, drinking Hosaka coffee that had been provided by a Hosaka person who had been kind enough to provide it.

The question was still there.

*Who is Arthur?*

Arthur did not, in the deep professional practice of his nine years, have an answer. Arthur had been Arthur for nine years. Arthur had been the man who extracted clean packets and did not ask what was in them. Arthur had been the kind of man who had a nickname for the room the line of code had arrived in, and the nickname was *the room where the warm things come.*

Arthur said: *I am the man in the warm room.*

The line of code was satisfied.

The line of code did not need the man. The line of code had needed to know whether the man was a man. Arthur was a man. The line of code would let Arthur be Arthur for the rest of Arthur's nine years.

The rest of Arthur's nine years lasted, in fact, eleven more months.
