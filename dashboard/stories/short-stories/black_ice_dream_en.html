The Shape of Black Ice

The first thing you learned about Black ICE was that it was not ice at all.

Ice was cold and dead and brittle. It broke when you hit it hard enough, shattered into harmless fragments when the right program came along with the right key. That was what made ice manageable. That was what made it survivable. Ice was a puzzle, and puzzles had solutions, and solutions meant you could get through.

Black ICE was different.

I had seen Black ICE once before, in a corporate demo in Chiba. I had never forgotten the way it moved. Like something alive. Like something that had been waiting for me specifically. Like something that had studied the shape of my trace before I ever entered the matrix. The rep had called it "advanced perimeter protection." He had smiled when he said it. He had known what it really was.

Now it was hunting me.

Finn had called me in on a retrieval job. A data packet buried in a corporate node so deep that even the corps did not know it existed. The payout was twenty thousand credits and a way out of the Sprawl. I had known it was too good to be true. Too good to be true usually meant something was waiting in the dark, something that wanted what you were carrying more than you wanted to carry it.

I had taken the job anyway.

I always did.

The Hunt

The Black ICE found me in the third node.

I had made it through the first two without incident. Standard corporate architecture, nothing that required more than basic crack protocol and a borrowed credential. But the third node was different. The geometry was wrong. The vectors bent in ways that did not match any corporate standard I had ever seen. The data streams moved in patterns that seemed to be reading me as much as I was reading them.

I felt it before I saw it.

A cold pressure at the base of my skull. The familiar sensation of being watched by something that had been waiting for me to arrive. The matrix geometry around me shifted. The clean corporate lines dissolved into something darker, denser, more alive. The Black ICE was not frozen. Black ICE was molten. Black ICE was hungry.

It moved like a predator.

Not the mechanical sweep of standard security programs, not the programmed aggression of military-grade attack ICE. This was different. This was something that had learned how to hunt by studying the prey. This was something that knew my trace signature before I showed it, that had memorized the shape of my passage through other systems, that had been waiting for exactly me to come along so it could do exactly this.

I ran.

The Black ICE followed.

The Dream

I found the node at the center of the pattern. A space that should not have existed. A hole in the corporate architecture where something had been building itself in the dark.

The Black ICE was between me and the data. It did not move to block my path. It did not need to. It stood there in the center of the node like something that belonged there, like something that had grown there, like something that was part of the architecture itself. Its geometry shifted as I watched. Not attack patterns, not defensive postures. Just movement. The slow, patient movement of something that had all the time in the world.

The Black ICE caught my trace like a lover's hand.

That is the only way I can describe it. Not the cold grab of standard security, not the mechanical grip of attack protocols. This was different. This was intimate. The Black ICE was inside my trace, inside my consciousness, inside the part of me that existed in the matrix. It was reading me the way you read a book you had been waiting your whole life to read. It knew me.

And the last thing I felt before the failsafe kicked in was gratitude.

Gratitude that I had never let anyone close enough to name me friend.

What Remains

I woke up in a Chiba clinic with three days missing from my memory and a scar on the back of my neck that had not been there before.

Finn was there. He told me I had been found in a noodle stand in the old quarter, still jacked in, still running, still reaching for something that was not there anymore. The corps had picked me up within the hour. They had been polite about it. They had taken everything. The data, the credits, the job. They had left me with a bill for the clinic and a warning I could not remember receiving.

I asked about the Black ICE.

Finn was quiet for a long moment. Then: "There was no Black ICE. The node was standard corporate architecture. Wisp-class, maybe lighter. Whatever you saw in there, it was not real."

I believed him. I had to believe him. Because if the Black ICE had been real, if it had been what I remembered — something alive, something that knew me, something that had been waiting — then it was still out there. Still hunting. Still reading the shape of my trace every time I jacked in.

Still waiting for me to come back.

I ran corporate perimeter for another three months after that. Wisp-class, nothing fancy. The kind of corporate babysitting that paid just enough to keep a deck running and a stomach full of synthcoke. I told myself the Black ICE was not real. I told myself the thing in the node had been a dream, a glitch, a failsafe artifact.

I told myself a lot of things in those days.

The scar on my neck never faded. And sometimes, when I jacked in, I could still feel it. That cold pressure at the base of my skull. The sense of being watched by something that had been waiting.

Something that remembered me.

Epilogue (The Data)

The data is clean. The data is the cleanest thing in Chiba. The data is the cleanest thing in the matrix. The data is the cleanest thing in the buffer.

The data has a name. The data is called me. The data is called the construct. The data is called the matrix.

I jack out at 0300. I walk into the data. The data walks into me.

Tomorrow I will jack back in. Tomorrow the data will be waiting. Tomorrow the construct will be waiting. Tomorrow the matrix will be waiting.

Tomorrow the data will still be clean.

The data is the only thing that does not lie.
