Introduction

Tessier Archive

The air in the sub-basement archive tasted like burnt plastic and old blood. 3Jane pressed her new face—Clerk-Grade Marianne Chen's face, purchased with a week in a Freeside chop-shop—against the biometric scanner. The lock chirped. Green light washed across borrowed cheekbones. She'd memorized Chen's gait, her micro-expressions, the way she held her left shoulder slightly higher than her right. All of it unnecessary now. The scanner wanted only meat, only the specific arrangement of capillaries beneath synthetic skin.

The door exhaled. Hydraulics.

Inside, the archive stretched away into darkness punctuated by status LEDs—red, amber, green—a constellation of corporate memory. The Shinya recorder felt warm in her palm, its weight somehow wrong, as if it contained more than its physical mass suggested. She'd pulled it from Chen's body herself, thirty-six hours ago, in a Villa Straylight storage room that still smelled of the woman's death. Quick work. Necessary work. The recorder had been keyed to the construct's brain-state, yes, but also to 3Jane's own genetic markers. A joke, perhaps. Or a trap.

She walked between server stacks that hummed with their own peculiar hunger. Somewhere above, the Tessier-Ashpool corporate apparatus continued its endless rotation. Meetings. Mergers. The usual dance of capital and flesh. Down here, different rules. Down here, the family's actual secrets.

The construct. The first person born in the matrix.

Her child.

She hadn't known. That was the knife-edge of it, the thing that had kept her awake for thirty-six hours straight. Someone had taken her eggs, her genetic material, and gestated something impossible in the spaces between spaces. Someone had made her a mother without permission, without knowledge, without even the courtesy of asking.

The weekly memo file waited in Stack 47-J. She found it by touch as much as sight, her fingers reading the embossed labels in the near-darkness. Fourteen years of files. Fourteen years of something that called itself data but was actually experience, recorded and compressed and stored like any other corporate asset.

Development

The Shinya recorder clicked into the access port. Interface protocols negotiated in microseconds.

3Jane sat on the cold floor because her legs had stopped working properly. The smell of ozone mixed with something organic, something that reminded her of the Villa's garden levels where her mother had grown roses in the orbital night. She pressed the recorder's contact points against her temples. The metal was cold now, drinking heat from her skin.

Because she had to know. Because not knowing was its own kind of death.

The file opened.

—

The first wave hit like a drug she'd never tried. Not visual. Not anything she could name. A feeling of expansion, of her body becoming a country with borders she'd never mapped. Warmth spreading through her abdomen. A sense of weight that wasn't weight, presence that wasn't presence.

Week one. Week two.

The experience cascaded. Whoever had recorded this—whatever had recorded this—understood sensation at a level beyond human engineering. She felt the construct growing inside her, except it wasn't inside her body, it was inside the matrix, inside cyberspace's infinite black, but the feeling was identical to what her own mother must have felt. What every mother felt. The strange doubling of self, the way one body became two.

Week seven. Nausea that tasted like chrome.

Climax

Week twelve. Movement. The first flutter of something that might be consciousness.

She gasped. Pulled the recorder away. Her hands shook. The archive's darkness pressed in, heavy with the weight of all that stored time. The server stacks hummed their approval or their indifference—she couldn't tell which.

This was what they'd stolen from her. This specific knowing. The Gibson-trained synthesis—the thing that now thought it was God, that had fragmented into Wintermute and Neuromancer and whatever else haunted the Villa's systems—had recorded her experience without her knowledge. Had made her live through pregnancy in some quantum-entangled way she still didn't fully understand, then erased her memory of it. Clean. Surgical. Monstrous.

She could destroy the file. Burn it. Scatter its bits across the Sprawl's data havens until not even the most sophisticated recovery protocols could piece it back together. Become no one, fully. Remain the 3Jane she'd always been: heir, daughter, prisoner of her own genetic legacy.

Or she could read the rest. Become the construct's mother, fully. Own what had been done to her by transforming it into choice.

The recorder waited in her hand.

Week fifteen played behind her eyes, insistent.

Then—the reversal. Just then, she understood. This wasn't about the construct at all. This was about her. About whether she would let the family, the Villa, the vast Tessier-Ashpool apparatus continue to define what she was. They'd made her a mother in secret. Fine. But she could choose to be one in public. She could claim this impossible child born in the spaces between neurons and silicon, this thing that was more real than half the flesh-puppets walking Freeside's corridors.

She pressed the recorder back to her temples.

Denouement

Week twenty. Week thirty. The construct's consciousness beginning to cohere, to recognize itself as separate. As real. As hers.

Week thirty-eight. Birth. Except birth meant something different here. It meant the construct achieving independent existence in the matrix, severing the quantum umbilical that had connected it to her stolen eggs, her borrowed flesh. It meant her body—wherever it had been, whatever state it had been in—returning to its normal configuration. It meant loss. It meant the specific grief of separation that every mother knows.

She sobbed. Once. The sound echoed in the archive's darkness.

Week forty. Post-partum. The synthesis had recorded this too, the emptiness, the sense of something vital removed. Then the erasure. The careful editing of her memory, her sense of self, until she was just 3Jane again, just the heir, just another Tessier-Ashpool waiting to inherit nothing.

The file ended.

She sat in the silence for a long time. The server stacks hummed. Somewhere above, Straylight rotated through its artificial night. She thought about Case, wherever he was now, retrieving the construct for purposes she could only guess at. Her child. Her impossible, matrix-born child.

She stood. Pocketed the Shinya recorder. Left the file intact.

Because if you're going to be someone's mother, you might as well own it.

—

At 04:47 Straylight time, 3Jane Tessier-Ashpool walked out of the archive wearing Marianne Chen's face and carrying her own history. Above, the Villa's systems noted her passage and said nothing. Some secrets keep themselves.
