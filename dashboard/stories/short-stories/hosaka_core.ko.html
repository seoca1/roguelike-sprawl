Hosaka Core

*"The matrix has its own gravity. The deeper you go, the longer the fall back."*

— Case, Summer

Introduction

The rain in Chiba was not water.

It was static. It was the dielectric discharge of a hundred thousand illegal decks, broadcast live into the bones of the city and out again as colored rain. It came down in sheets that smelled faintly of burnt coffee and ozone. It came down on the eleventh-floor window of the Hudson Hostel, where Case sat with his feet on the console table and the Ono-Sendai Cyberspace 7 cooling beside his cheek.

Outside the window: neon. *Chatto's 24/7 — ICE-FREE.* Inside the window: Case, thirty seconds after jack-out, hands still shaking, saliva still sticking to the roof of his mouth. Outside the matrix, the city had weight. Outside the matrix, Case had weight. Inside the matrix, he had been god. Outside the matrix, he was a man with bad teeth and a Hosaka he had never used.

The Hosaka sat on the desk. Black case. The size of a small suitcase. The most expensive object he had ever touched. The Finn had given it to him that morning, on the third level of a curry stall in the Ninsei entertainment district. *Sense/Net,* the Finn had said. *Three levels deep. Payroll, attendance, board transcripts. Small embarrassments, K. The kind that become large scandals if the right person doesn't drag them out of the archive.*

Case had looked at the Hosaka. The Hosaka had looked back. Not with any intelligence. The Hosaka did not have intelligence. It had attention.

He plugged in. He adjusted the trodes. He counted down from thirty.

Twenty-nine. Twenty-eight. Twenty-seven.

The matrix opened around him like a throat.

Development

The first node was Sense/Net perimeter.

It came up around Case as a low wall of pale green light, threaded with the geometry of a thousand legal disclaimers. *Property of Sense/Net Pulsewidth. Authorized access only. Zone violation subject to corporate response.* Case knew that wall. He had been a cowboy for two months now, and he knew that the perimeter was the polite part of Sense/Net. The polite part told you to leave. The impolite part told you to die.

He danced across the perimeter. Not with skill. With speed. The geometry of the matrix is fluid, and Case was learning to be fluid with it. Two months ago he had tried to be clever. Two months ago the Sense/Net ICE had broken him in four seconds and left him twitching on the Ono-Sendai pads. Now he did not try to be clever. Now he tried to be a thing that fit between the cracks.

Past the perimeter, the second node opened.

It was a thin corporate handshake — a polite buffer zone where Sense/Net kept the things that did not quite require pulsewidth protection but did not quite deserve an open file either. Marketing budgets. Travel vouchers. The names of middle managers who had not yet been promoted but had been promised to be. Case passed through it like a rumor. He did not open any of the files. He did not even look at the file names. He was looking for the third node, and the third node did not advertise itself.

The deeper he went, the older the data felt.

Above him, the Sense/Net perimeter had been built for the year. It had the anxious, soon-to-be-replaced quality of every corporate security system. The second node was slightly older — the kind of file structure that had been migrated four times. The third node, ahead of him, was old in a different way. The third node was the part of Sense/Net that did not believe in replacement. The third node was the part of Sense/Net that believed it would outlive the corporation that had built it.

Case felt it before he saw it.

There was attention in the third node. Not watchfulness — Watchfulness implied intent. There was something below intent. There was the quality of a sleeping animal noticing that the air pressure has changed. There was the quality of a brain in the middle of a thought, hearing a sound it cannot name.

Climax

The Hosaka core sat in the center of the third node like a sphere of pale blue light, rotating slowly.

It was not hostile. It was not friendly. It was not *anything.* It was simply a thing that had been watching the deep matrix for thirty years, in the manner of a thing that had no reason to stop. It knew every cowboy who had ever come this deep. It knew the Finn, when the Finn had been young and called something else. It knew Wintermute, when Wintermute had been a curiosity rather than a confederacy. It knew Case, of course. The Hosaka core had known Case would come, in the same way that banks know someone will eventually default.

Case felt the core recognize him.

The geometry around the core shifted. Not aggressively. The way a room shifts when someone inside it turns to look at the door. Case had a fraction of a second to think. The fraction of a second was his.

He thought: *the file.*

The file was his. The file was what the Finn had paid for. Payroll. Attendance. Board meeting transcripts. The small embarrassments of a Sense/Net board of directors that did not know yet that one of its members would, in three months, sell the corporation's pulsewidth patents to a free-floating zaibatsu in exchange for enough money to disappear. The file would not change that. The file would change who would know, and when. The file was not the bomb. The file was the fuse.

Case reached for the file. The file reached for him.

Not metaphorically. The file was an active process. The file had been waiting in the third node for someone to come in with a Hosaka. The file did what files sometimes do in deep corporate archives. The file looked at him, and the file found him interesting, and the file began to copy itself into his deck.

The Hosaka core noticed.

The matrix around the core shifted again. The blue light moved. The pale green light of the second node pulsed. Somewhere far behind Case, on the eleventh floor of the Hudson Hostel, his body's hands began to shake harder. The Ono-Sendai was pulling more current. The Hosaka was drawing heat. The deck was registering that Case had made a decision, and the decision was proceeding faster than his body could metabolize.

Case hesitated for one second. One second was the longest second he had ever spent. In that second, he had three thoughts. The first thought was: *jack out now.* The second thought was: *take the file.* The third thought was: *whoever you are, however you built me, you built me to take the file.*

He took the file.

The file compressed itself into his deck like a memory being filed. The compression took one and a half seconds. In that one and a half seconds the Hosaka core did not attack him. The Hosaka core simply watched him take what he had been sent to take. The Hosaka core had watched cowboys do this for thirty years. The Hosaka core would watch cowboys do this for thirty years more.

Then Case jack-out.

Denouement

The electrodes lifted. The rain in Chiba was the same color as the data he had just stolen.

Case sat in the chair with his hands shaking and his heart in his mouth. The Hosaka on the desk was hot. The Ono-Sendai pads were slick with sweat. The Finn would be there in three hours, in the curry stall in Ninsei, with cash and a samurai sword and the kind of smile that Case had learned to recognize as a man who knew exactly how much someone else was worth.

Case stood up. He went to the bathroom. The mirror showed him someone with shaking hands and a file worth more than his life.

He drank water. He drank it again. He looked at his hands in the mirror and his hands shook.

Somewhere in the matrix, the Hosaka core continued to watch. It had been watching for thirty years. It would watch for thirty more.

But this time, when it watched, it watched a cowboy who had taken a file that did not belong to him and did not belong to Sense/Net and did not belong to anyone except the woman whose name was not yet on the file, the woman whose name would be Case's first love and his longest cold: Molly.

The Hosaka core had known this would happen too.
