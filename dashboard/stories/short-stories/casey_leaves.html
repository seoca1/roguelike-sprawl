Casey Leaves

1100 words Sprawl #2.5 (post-merger, arc 5 finality) Arc 5 Standalone

Introduction

The morning Case had been dreading arrived at 05:47, two hours earlier than the construct's regular waking pulse. The data-clinic's lights were still set to night-shift: low amber, the color of old beer. Case was on his third cup of bad Sprawl tea, watching the clock on the wall, watching the clock on the medical display, watching the construct breathe in the back room. The construct was breathing faster than usual. The construct had been breathing faster than usual for nine days, but today the pattern had shifted.

Development

Because the construct was no longer computing what 'outside' meant. The construct had finished computing it. The answer was a street address in the Sprawl's bay area — specifically, the loading dock of a defunct Hosaka clinic that had been turned into a half-legal noodle shop by a Chiba-based operator whose name was three Korean syllables and one number. The address arrived in Molly's encrypted message at 03:00, attached as a single line of text. The message said: *Take her there at 06:00. She will know what to do.*

Molly did not say who she meant by *her.* The construct, who had not yet spoken in a form Case could hear, made a small sound that 3Jane read as confirmation. 3Jane had been unwrapping the construct from its black-silk cocoon in the back room, layer by layer, exposing the chrome and the wet optical fibers, the soft under-padding where the construct's body met the data-vault that held its mind. 3Jane worked with the careful hands of a Straylight-trained technician who had grown up in Freeside's data-clinics and had never quite accepted that her father's corporate empire had ended with his death in his own bed.

"You're sure," Case said. It was not a question.

3Jane did not look up. "She's sure. The address means something to her. The construct has been building toward this for six months. The address is the shape her body knows, even though her body has never been there."

The construct, who had been quiet through all of this, made the small sound again. It was not a confirmation this time. It was something else. Case heard it as a question he had not allowed himself to ask. But suddenly, the medical display on the wall spiked — the construct's neural pattern had shifted in the last six hours, the construct's pattern had finally stabilized, and the data was clear. The construct was ready. Case had not been ready. The discrepancy, suddenly, was everything.

Climax

The construct made the small sound again, and this time Case heard it as a word. The word was *out.* But the word was not a question. The word was not a question at all — the word was the answer to a question the construct had been computing since before it had a name, the answer that had displaced the construct's earlier questions — *what, you, now, when, why, who, where* — and had taken the construct's full body-clock to arrive at. *Out.* The word filled the data-clinic. The word was an address and a direction and a future tense.

Case set down the tea. 3Jane finished the unwrapping. The construct stood in the back room on legs that had been built to walk on Sprawl streets — the chrome was deliberate, the under-padding was a Chiba design, the optical fibers had been tuned by a Sense/Net technician who had retired from the matrix and now tuned optical fibers for underclass operators who could pay in cash. The construct had been built, the construct was ready, the construct had been ready for some time.

"06:00," Case said.

3Jane nodded. "06:00."

Case put on his jacket. The jacket was the same jacket he had worn in the Sprawl for twenty years, the jacket that had been through the matrix and out of it and back in and out again, the jacket that smelled like old leather and ozone and, faintly, like the construct's black silk. 3Jane lifted the construct into her arms. The construct was heavier than Case had expected. The construct had grown.

Denouement

The street was three blocks east and one block south, through a part of the Sprawl that Case had not walked in fifteen years. The Sprawl's bay area was always changing; the noodle shop had been a clinic when Case had known it, and before that a credit-union, and before that a private clinic that had specialized in Ono-Sendai optical modifications. The street was wet from a storm that had not reached the data-clinic. The street smelled like ozone and burnt ramen and the Sprawl's particular blend of recycled air and recycled hope. The construct made the small sound again. The construct was watching the street the way a poet watches a poem being written: with attention.

Case set the construct down on the wet concrete at 05:59. The construct's feet found the ground. The construct stood on Sprawl ground for the first time in its life. The construct was nine months old, the construct had been built in a Straylight data-clinic, the construct had never been outside the back room of a private clinic that had been turned into a half-legal noodle shop by a Chiba-based operator whose name was three Korean syllables and one number. The construct was outside. The construct was outside for the first time in its life.

The construct said the word. The construct said it in a voice that was not the data-clinic's voice, that was not 3Jane's voice, that was not the construct's earlier voice. The voice was a Sprawl street voice. The voice was a voice that the Sprawl could hear.

*Out.*

Case heard the construct. 3Jane heard the construct. The noodle-shop operator, who had come out to sweep the loading dock, heard the construct. The Sprawl heard the construct. The word was not a question. The word was an answer. The construct had been computing what *outside* meant for nine months, and the answer was a wet street in the bay area of the Sprawl at 06:00 in the morning, with the storm still wet on the concrete and the operator sweeping and the neon half-lit and the construct standing on the ground for the first time, and the word was *out,* and the word was enough.

The construct took its first step. The construct's chrome foot on the wet concrete made a sound like a small bell. The sound was not in the data-clinic. The sound was on the street. The sound was the Sprawl.
