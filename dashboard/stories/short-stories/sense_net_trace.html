Sense/Net Trace

The Lead

Finn's message arrived at 0400, like all bad news.

Three days I had been tracing T-A's internal routes—the kind of work that paid in paranoia and nightmares, three days of following M's ghost through corporate architecture, through places she had passed that she didn't want anyone to know she had touched. M had been dead for a week. Official story was thermal breach at Sense/Net's Boston node, her brain fried jacking out, something hardware malfunction left her for the corps to dispose of. But M didn't die of hardware malfunction. M died finding something, something buried so deep in Tessier-Ashpool's architecture that even their own cleanup crews couldn't find it, and someone had decided that M's silence was worth more than M's life.

Finn's message was three lines: frequency, time, name. The frequency was a mid-level dead drop, the kind of anonymous channel runners used when they didn't want to be found. The time was 0400, and 0400 meant Finn was being careful. And the name was Koichi Yamamoto—the Sense/Net system administrator who had been selling T-A corporate memos to the highest bidder for the past six months.

"Good data," Finn said when I jacked in. His voice was clean, professional, the voice of a fixer who had built a reputation on secrets. "T-A is bleeding. Someone's making a move. I need you to find out who."

I traced the system administrator's signature through seven proxy nodes, each one a different flavor of corporate gray. The first three were dead—burned addresses, the kind of ghost traces that wise runners avoided. The fourth was hot, a recent active connection, something that didn't want to be seen. The fifth was colder. The sixth was ice. The seventh was so deep in Sense/Net's Boston architecture that I almost lost it—a node that shouldn't exist, connecting to nothing, broadcasting on frequencies reserved for dead runners.

That's where I found M's ghost mark.

Development

She was still in there. Still looking for something. Still hunting.

Seven Proxy Nodes

The system administrator wasn't hard to find if you knew the shape of his search patterns.

Koichi Yamamoto sold data through a series of dead drops and ghost proxies, the kind of job security that only worked because no one looked hard enough. But I was looking. I had been looking for three days since Finn had put me on this job, and I had learned to read the shape of corporate data flows the way a surgeon read X-rays—the way tumors bent around tissue, the way blood vessels avoided masses.

The first proxy node was a dead address in the mid-levels, a forgotten corner of the Sprawl's commercial architecture where data went to be commodified and forgotten. It was cold, abandoned, the kind of node runners used for storage and then abandoned when it wasn't profitable anymore. I tagged it and moved on.

The second proxy was hot—recent access, something touched within the last forty-eight hours. The signature was fresh, professional, the kind of trace pattern that suggested someone who knew what they were doing on a regular basis. I followed it deeper.

The third proxy was ice. Corporate-grade security, the kind of architecture that didn't alarm unless you were looking for it. I was looking. I always was. The ice was thick, layered, the kind of security that suggested something worth protecting. I rode borrowed credentials through it and kept moving.

The fourth proxy was ghost—a phantom node, something that only existed in the negative space between corporate data storage. The kind of architecture only old-school cowboys could read, the kind of pattern most runners wouldn't recognize as anything at all. But I recognized it. M used ghost nodes when she wanted to disappear, and this ghost node was covered in M's fingerprints.

She had been there. Recently. About a week before she died.

Climax

The fifth proxy was a dead drop—a storage node, the kind of architecture runners used to cache data before moving it to the buyer's location. When I found it, it was empty, recently deleted, the kind of thorough that suggested someone had been careful. But I had learned to read residuals, and I had learned to read the ghosts of data that even the best cleaners couldn't completely erase, and this ghost was talking to me. Someone had moved a lot of data through this node in the past month. Corporate memos. Financial projections. Something that might have been worth dying for.

The sixth proxy was a trap.

I felt it close before I understood what it was—a honey-pot, a fake node designed to catch runners who asked too many questions. The corporate architecture around me shifted, crystallized, became something with teeth. ICE. Not the polite perimeter security of the upper levels, but something older, harder, the kind of program that didn't negotiate and didn't surrender.

I pulled out before it bit.

The seventh proxy was so deep in Sense/Net's Boston architecture that I almost lost it—a node that didn't exist in any corporate index, a ghost of a ghost, broadcasting on frequencies I had only used once before. M's frequency. The one she used for her most sensitive work, the kind of job even the corps didn't know existed.

M's ghost was still there. Still waiting. Still looking.

Ghost in the Machine

I found the node three hours after Finn's original message, at 0430.

It was buried in Sense/Net's Boston architecture like something drowned—a phantom address, a ghost frequency, a signal that shouldn't have been broadcasting at all. I approached it the way you approach a body in the cesspool: carefully, respectfully, knowing the corpse might tell you something you didn't want to hear.

Denouement

M had been tracking something through T-A's architecture. Something that belonged to Tessier-Ashpool, something that the family had buried so deep that even their own cleanup crews couldn't find it. Something that M had found, and something that someone had killed her for.

The data was still there. M's ghost had left a message—not in words, but in the shape of the trace, the pattern of her passage, the residual heat of her presence in the frozen code. She had found what she was looking for. She had tried to extract it. And she had died with it still in her hands.

I copied what I could. Financial projections, corporate memos, the kind of data that told a story about something that was coming—something big, something that would change the balance of power in the Sprawl. I didn't understand all of it. I didn't need to. I just needed to know that someone had killed M for this, and that someone would kill again if they knew what I now had.

I pulled out. I tagged the frequency. I encrypted the data and hid it in places even Finn didn't know about.

And I started running.

Because I knew what M had died for now. And I knew who would be next.
