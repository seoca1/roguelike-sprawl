Straylight Approach

*"Tessier-Ashpool had been waiting for a very long time."*

— Gibson, Neuromancer, Chapter 8

도입: The Door

Sil had not wanted the Straylight approach.

He had wanted the Sense/Net data, the market research files, the kind of corporate intelligence that didn't make the family notice. He had wanted a quiet job, a quiet payout, a quiet six months in the Sprawl while the Tessier-Ashpool people forgot his name.

He should have known better. Quiet jobs didn't come to runners like Sil. Quiet jobs went to runners with families, runners with roots, runners who hadn't spent the last decade burning through every favor the Sprawl had to give them. Sil had no family left. Sil had no roots. Sil had nothing but his deck and his reputation and the small, terrible skill of being able to open doors that nobody else could open.

But the Straylight door was different.

The Finn had insisted: Straylight was the door, and Sil was the only jockey in the Sprawl who could open it without breaking. Tessier-Ashpool didn't negotiate with amateurs. Tessier-Ashpool didn't negotiate at all. They sat in their orbital arcology and watched the Sprawl like spiders watching flies — patient, geometric, certain that the flies would eventually come to them.

Sil was the fly. He didn't have a choice.

M had left him a contact, and the contact had left him a job, and the job had left him here: three nodes deep into the Tessier-Ashpool subsidiary, where the architecture stopped being Sense/Net and started being something older. Something that had been there before Sense/Net was a name. Something that smelled of salt water and old money and the particular patience of families that had never had to explain themselves to anyone.

전개: The Geometry

The ICE here was different too.

It didn't move like Sense/Net ICE. Sense/Net ICE moved like a corporate security guard — predictable, procedural, content to scan you and let you through if your credentials were clean. This ICE moved like weather. Slow. Certain. Inevitable. It had been waiting for someone like Sil for a very long time. It had been waiting for someone like him since before he was born.

Sil's hands were trembling. He hadn't eaten in twelve hours. The matrix smelled of ozone and the faint electrical signature of corporate memory, and somewhere in the architecture, a door was opening for him.

He didn't know what was behind the door.

He didn't want to know.

The Tessier-Ashpool subsidiary architecture was different from anything Sil had navigated in fifteen years of running. Sense/Net data was structured like a corporate filing cabinet — logical, hierarchical, designed by people who had taken business classes. T-A data was structured like a spider's web. Not metaphorical. Literally a spider's web — the data packets arranged in radial patterns, the connections stretched between nodes like silk between branches, the whole architecture designed to catch whatever wandered into it and hold it there until the family decided what to do with it.

The family had been here since the early days. Since before the war. Since before the Sprawl had a name. The data carried that age in its weight — every packet heavier than it should be, every connection slower than it should be, every door more patient than any door Sil had ever encountered.

The doors didn't open. The doors waited to be opened.

절정: The Family

Sil had been running for four hours. The door was close now — close enough that he could feel it in the data, the way you can feel a wall before you see it in the dark. The door was older than the rest of the architecture. Older than the subsidiary. Older than the family itself, maybe.

The Tessier-Ashpool family had built Straylight in the 2010s, when space was still expensive and orbital arcologies were still a status symbol. The villa was designed to be a fortress — not against the Sprawl, not against the corps, but against time itself. The family had wanted a place that would outlast them. A place that would remember them, even after the family's biological line had ended.

The door was that place.

Sil paused at the threshold. He could feel the family's presence on the other side of the door — not a single presence, but a chorus. The 3Janes who had run the family for centuries. The Ashpools who had built the first arcology. The Tessiers who had married into the dynasty and brought their own particular brand of corporate patience. All of them, somehow, still there. Watching the door. Watching Sil.

Waiting for him to decide.

He was a fly at a spider's web. He had been a fly his whole life. But the spiders here weren't interested in eating him. They were interested in something else — something the Finn's job brief hadn't mentioned, something the contact hadn't warned him about, something that was older and more patient than any of the families that had come before.

결말: The Memory

The door opened anyway.

It opened the way Tessier-Ashpool doors always opened — slowly, deliberately, with the patient geometry of a family that had never had to ask permission. Behind the door was data: corporate contracts, inter-family correspondence, the kind of small embarrassments that became large scandals. The data was old — older than Sense/Net, older than the Sprawl, older than the family itself. The data was the family, in a way. The data was what the family had decided to keep.

Sil copied what he could. He didn't copy all of it. He didn't have time.

Behind the door, somewhere in the architecture, something was watching him. Not the ICE. Not the Watchdog. Something else. Something that had been waiting at Straylight since before the family built Freeside. Something that remembered M, and the contact, and every runner who had come before Sil, and every runner who would come after.

Sil jack-out. The data burned into his deck like a memory being filed. The Finn would be pleased. The contact would be paid. M's name would be cleared, or not, depending on what the data said.

Sil walked to the Chiba window. The rain was the same color as Straylight's architecture. He didn't know what he had found. He didn't know what he had brought back.

His hand trembled slightly as he lit a cigarette — old habit, muscle memory of a thousand stakeouts — and the smoke curled up through the blue-lit rain. The deck at his hip was warm with the stolen data. Outside, the Chiba neon bled into the wet pavement: pink, blue, the particular orange of a noodle-shop sign four floors down.

Some data, he would sell. Some, he would keep. The data that M had touched — the data that had once been hers, before the family had taken it — Sil would keep. He didn't know why yet. He only knew that the deck's warmth felt different from usual. Heavier. As if the data knew whose it had been, and was waiting to be returned.

Some things are better not named. Some doors are better not opened. But once a door has been opened, it stays open — for the family, for the next runner, for whoever comes next with a quiet job and a quiet payout and no idea what waits on the other side.

The Sprawl's rain kept falling. The data burned in Sil's deck. The family kept watching.

Some things in the Sprawl are patient. Some things are very, very patient.
