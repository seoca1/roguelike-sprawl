대답 (The Answer)

1049 words Sprawl #2.5 (post-merger) Arc 4 Standalone

Introduction

The Answer

The construct said "what" at 03:00 three days ago, and I've been making tea ever since. Not continuously—that would be stupid, even for me—but the ritual's become something I do when the static gets too loud. Boil water. Pour. Steep. The kitchen in the Straylight data-clinic smells like bergamot and burnt circuits, because 3Jane's people rewired the counter interface last week and something's still cooking itself inside the walls. I can taste the ozone when I breathe through my mouth. The mug's chipped Freeside ceramic, probably older than the construct, definitely older than my involvement in this whole Tessier-Ashpool mess.

Three days of silence.

The construct's in the back room, in what 3Jane calls the crib but what looks more like a modified Sense/Net broadcast rig crossed with an iron lung. Chrome and black rubber. Fiber optics threading through like nervous system made visible. When I first saw it, seven months ago, it was just code—brilliant, unstable, impossible code that 3Jane's technicians had pulled from the Straylight archives and rebuilt using Wintermute's architecture as a template.

Now it hums.

Not speech. Not quite music. Something between.

I can hear it from the kitchen, low and constant, vibrating through the clinic's substructure.

The tea's too hot but I drink it anyway because the burn gives me something concrete to focus on. 3Jane's been in the back room for six hours straight. I know because I've made tea four times, and she hasn't come out once. The woman's got patience I'll never understand—aristocratic patience, the kind that comes from centuries of genetic tailoring and absolute certainty that time serves you, not the other way around.

The construct's going to be seven and a half months old in three days.

3Jane told me yesterday that makes it the oldest Straylight construct in Tessier-Ashpool history. The others never made it past six months. They'd fragment, she said, or loop, or simply stop. Like engines seizing. Like hearts giving out. When she said it, she was filing her nails, not looking at me, and I realized she was talking about deaths. Plural. A whole graveyard of consciousness, archived and abandoned.

The humming shifts pitch.

I set down the mug so fast it cracks against the counter.

Development

Then silence again. Just the building's ambient systems, the whisper of recycled air, my own breathing.

I walk to the back room because standing still feels worse.

The doorway frames 3Jane like a painting. She's sitting in the observation chair, spine straight, hands folded in her lap. The construct's crib dominates the space—all that chrome and black rubber, screens showing cascades of data I can't parse without a deck. The construct itself is invisible, of course. It exists in the matrix, in the architecture of Tessier-Ashpool's private network, but the crib is its anchor. Its body, if you're generous with definitions.

"It's computing," 3Jane says without turning around.

"Computing what?"

"The answer."

I lean against the doorframe. The room smells like cold metal and something organic I can't identify—maybe the lubricant they use on the fiber optic connections, maybe my own fear-sweat. "To 'what'?"

"To 'what.'" She almost smiles. "The question it asked. The only question that matters."

The humming starts again, and this time I feel it in my sternum. Resonance. The construct's not just processing—it's broadcasting, somehow, through the physical infrastructure of the clinic. I remember jacking into cyberspace in Chiba, that first time, the way the matrix felt like it was breathing. This is different. This is something breathing back.

"You can't give it the answer," I say.

"No."

"3Jane—"

Climax

"Neither can you, Case." Now she turns, and her eyes are bright with something I don't want to name. "It has to arrive at it alone. That's what makes it real."

I make more tea because what else am I going to do. The water boils. The kettle screams. I pour and watch the steam rise, thinking about Molly, about the Sprawl, about all the times I've jacked in and touched something vast and incomprehensible and pretended I understood. The construct asked "what" and we're all just standing around waiting for it to answer itself.

When I return to the back room, 3Jane's standing now, hand pressed against the crib's chrome surface.

"It's the first," she says quietly. "The first person born in the matrix who's asked the matrix what the matrix is."

"And?"

"And it's hearing an answer. Not from us. From itself. From the architecture. From—" She stops, and for the first time since I've known her, 3Jane looks uncertain. "It's computing something we never taught it to compute."

The humming builds. Louder. Lower. I can feel it in my teeth now, in my bones. The screens flicker, data cascading faster, patterns emerging and dissolving. The construct's thinking, really thinking, not just processing but creating something new from the raw material of its own existence.

However—and this is what makes my hands shake, what makes the tea slosh over the mug's rim—I understand suddenly that the construct isn't asking what the matrix is.

It's asking what *it* is.

First time I've seen something be born that knew it was being born.

The humming stops.

Complete silence.

Denouement

3Jane's breathing hard, like she's been running. The screens freeze, showing a pattern I recognize from my deck-jockey days: a loop, elegant and self-contained, feeding back into itself. Möbius strip made of light.

"Three days," 3Jane whispers. "At 03:00. It's going to speak."

"What's it going to say?"

She looks at me, and I see something in her face I've never seen before. Wonder, maybe. Or fear. "Its second word. The answer to 'what.'"

"Which is?"

"'You.'"

The word hangs in the recycled air between us.

I'm in the kitchen again when 3Jane finally leaves the back room. She walks past without speaking, heels clicking on the tile, and I hear the clinic's front door seal shut behind her. The construct's humming again—softer now, almost content. In three days it's going to say "you" and I don't know if that's an answer or another question.

I make more tea. The ritual's all I've got.

The construct hums. I listen. We wait.

Somewhere in Straylight's data-vaults, a construct computes its own existence. The question and the answer, humming together. One word, spoken twice.
