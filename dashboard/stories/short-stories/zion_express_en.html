Zion Express

1995 wordsSprawl Trilogy

*"Zion was the rock. Zion was the tribe. Zion was home."*

— Gibson, Neuromancer, Chapter 7 (Maelcum's line)

도입: The Package

The Finn called at 04:47 in the morning, which meant the job was either good or bad. There was no middle ground for Finn's 04:47 calls.

"Courier. Got a rush job. Good money. You interested?"

I was sitting in my coffin hotel in the 11th level, watching the ceiling. The ceiling was the same as every other ceiling in the 11th level — black-painted concrete, the kind of ceiling that doesn't care if you look at it or not. The tea I had been drinking was cold. The Sprawl was, as always, the Sprawl.

"I'm listening."

"Zion station. Someone there needs a data package delivered. The corps are intercepting everything that moves through normal channels, but they've got no presence at Zion. Not yet." A pause. The Finn's pauses were calculated, like everything the Finn did. "The catch is you've got to get there in person. No remote jack, no proxy. You've got to physically board a shuttle and ride up."

Zion. I'd never been to the orbital stations — the costs of transit alone were enough to keep me grounded. Most of the runners I knew had been to Freeside once, maybe, to do a job for a corp suit who wanted to look at the Earth from the inside of a casino. None of them had been to Zion. Zion was the rock, the tribe, the home that the Sprawl had tried to be and wasn't.

"How much?"

"Fifteen. Up front. Another five on delivery."

Fifteen thousand. That was the kind of money that could change a life. Not just Sprawl-life, the kind of life where you spent your credits on stim tabs and second-rate sushi and a coffin with a window. Real life. The kind where you got out. The kind where you took a shuttle up and never came back.

"I'll do it."

"Good. Package is waiting at the usual drop point. Shuttle leaves in six hours. Don't be late."

The line went dead. I sat in the silence for another minute, watching the ceiling. The Sprawl didn't care about my decision. The Sprawl never cared about decisions. The Sprawl was just a place where decisions happened.

I got up, put on my jacket, and went to collect the package.

전개: The Drop Point

The package was a standard data core — six centimeters long, three wide, contained in a shock-resistant casing. I'd carried a hundred of them. They all felt the same. They all weighed the same. They all sat in the same pocket of the same jacket.

This one, I ran a quick diagnostic. Just out of habit. Just to make sure.

The encryption was deep — Tier 3 commercial, the kind that took a Tier 5 corp ICE hours to crack. The data inside was dense, but unreadable without the matching key. The standard courier diagnostic couldn't tell me what was in the package. The standard courier diagnostic wasn't supposed to.

Clean data, clean run. On paper, at least.

I pocketed the core and went to find breakfast. The Finn was right — the package was waiting. The Sprawl was clean. The shuttle was scheduled. Everything was as it should be.

Everything except for the weight.

I'd carried a hundred packages. They all weighed the same. This one weighed a little more. Not enough to notice if you weren't paying attention. Enough to notice if you were.

I was paying attention.

절정: The Void Between

The shuttle to Zion was a converted cargo hauler, the kind they use for cheap transport when the corps don't want to spend money on comfort. The seats were metal. The air tasted like recycled everything. The other passengers were a mix of data couriers, traders, and Rastafarians returning home.

The Rastafarians had drums. They played them during the launch, a rhythmic pulse that matched the vibration of the engines, and the sound was the sound of the void — a sound the Sprawl couldn't make, a sound that came from the space between things, the space the data didn't reach, the space where the corps had no presence.

I sat by the viewport. The Earth fell away — a sprawl of neon and data streams, millions of lives lived in the shadow of the corps, all of it shrinking as we climbed, all of it becoming a map, becoming a thing you could hold in your hand, becoming nothing.

The void between the stars. That's what the old man had called it, the contact I was going to meet. The void between the stars. The void the data didn't reach. The void the corps had spent two decades trying to fill with their own infrastructure, and the void that had remained the void.

Zion grew in the viewport as we approached. A mass of habitation modules and solar panels, connected by tubes and walkways, looking like a city that had decided to forget gravity. The closest thing to freedom the Sprawl had to offer. No corp presence. No ICE. Just people who'd decided to build something outside the megacorp's reach.

The customs check was a joke. They scanned the package, asked a few questions about its contents, and waved me through. At Zion, they didn't care what you were carrying — only that you didn't bring trouble with you.

The contact was waiting in a bar that served the worst synthetic beer I'd ever tasted. The bar was called "Dub's" and it looked like every other bar in the Sprawl except for the viewport, which looked out on the void instead of the neon. The void was darker than I had imagined. The void was darker than the Sprawl had prepared me for.

He was old. Patient. With the kind of eyes that had seen too much and forgotten most of it. He had the bearing of someone who had lived through the era of the first matrix jacks, the era of the first ICE, the era when the corps had first started building the Sprawl and the Sprawl had first started forgetting itself.

"You're the courier," he said.

"That's what they tell me."

He took the package. He turned it over in his hands — the way I'd turned it over, the way every courier turned every package, the way you held a thing that could be a paycheck or a death sentence and the only way to know was to deliver it. "This is everything. The corps have been trying to intercept it for weeks. Names, accounts, the whole network. A map of what they've been doing, and a map of what they haven't been."

He looked at me. "You're either very good or very lucky."

"Lucky," I said. "Definitely lucky."

"The corps are getting closer. The package has been moving for three weeks through every back channel the Sprawl has. Three weeks. Every courier who touched it before you touched it is either dead or in a corp data-bath. The corps don't leave witnesses."

He set the package on the table. The package sat there between us — six centimeters long, three wide, the weight of every name the corps had ever tried to bury, the weight of every account they had ever tried to close, the weight of a thing that had been moving for three weeks through the back channels of a world that wanted it stopped.

"You don't have to take it back," he said. "Not if you don't want to. The package has done what it was supposed to do. It's been delivered. The data is here. We have the map. You could stay. We have a place for runners who need to get off-world."

I looked at the package. I looked at the old man. I looked out the viewport at the void.

결말: The Way Out

"Why me?" I asked.

"Because the corps have run out of couriers they've already tried to kill. You're not on their lists yet. You're a clean runner with a clean jacket and a fifteen-thousand-credit job that you took because you needed the money. You're exactly the kind of person they don't look at twice. You're exactly the kind of person who can walk through their checkpoints and not get stopped."

He set a credstick on the table. "Fifteen thousand. As promised. And here's something else." He set a second stick beside the first. "A contact. For when you need to get off-world fast. Zion's not forever. The corps are getting closer. But the contact is real. The contact is a shuttle. The contact is a way out."

I took the credsticks. Thirty thousand credits. The kind of money that could change a life.

"What's the contact for?"

"For when the corps finally catch up to you. Everyone needs a way out. The station may not be forever, but it's a start." He stood, leaving the credsticks on the table. "Take care of yourself, courier. The matrix is a dangerous place. But the void between the stars — that's where real freedom lives."

He walked out. The bar was quiet. The void was quieter.

I looked at the credsticks. I looked at the package. I looked out the viewport at the Earth below — a sprawl of neon and data streams, millions of lives lived in the shadow of the corps, all of it shrinking as I watched, all of it becoming a map, becoming a thing I could hold in my hand, becoming nothing.

In a few hours, I'd be back down there. Running through the matrix. Looking for the next job. The next package. The next way out.

But now I had a way out. A contact. A chance.

The shuttle back to the Sprawl felt faster. Maybe it was the weight of the credsticks in my pocket. Maybe it was the hope that things could be different.

The Finn was waiting when I got back. The Sprawl hadn't changed. The Finn never changed.

"Good run," he said. "Same time next month?"

I didn't answer. I was already thinking about the stars.

에필로그: The Way Out

The contact was real. The contact was a shuttle. The contact was a way out.

I didn't take it — not that month, not the month after, not for two more years. I ran the Sprawl. I ran packages. I ran the matrix. I ran until the day the corps started watching me, the day the Finn stopped calling, the day the only thing left was the contact and the stars.

When I finally took the shuttle, the Sprawl was still down there. The neon was still the neon. The data streams were still the data streams. The void was still the void.

I had the credsticks. I had the contact. I had thirty thousand credits of work I'd done and thirty years of a life I'd lived.

I had the stars.

I had the way out.
