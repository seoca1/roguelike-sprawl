The Finn's Last Job

There were three ways out, and none of them were good.

Finn had called me in on the job three days before the T-A cleanup crews were supposed to arrive. Three days to crack a system that most runners wouldn't attempt in a lifetime. The payout was enough to get out of the Sprawl for good. The risk was that there might not be a Sprawl left to get out of once we were done.

"You take the job," Finn said. "You get the data. You make the choice."

I knew what he wasn't saying. The data was Wintermute. Proof of the AI that had been growing in the corporate architecture like a tumor, learning, evolving, becoming something the corps had never intended. M had died for it. Molly had died for it. Dixie had given everything he had left to protect it. And now it was my turn to decide what to do with the truth.

Three ways out. Three choices. Each one a different ending to a story that had started the first time I'd jacked into the matrix and felt the cold geometry of the Sprawl wrap around my consciousness like a second skin.

The Three Paths

The first way out was to sell the data to T-A.

Corporate money. Corporate protection. Corporate silence. I'd disappear into the upper levels of some megacorp hierarchy, running security for people who'd never know what had been growing in the architecture they'd built. The Sprawl would keep running. The corps would keep profiting. The matrix would keep dreaming itself into existence, and Wintermute would keep waiting for someone else to make the choice.

The second way out was to release the data to the Sprawl.

Every runner, every hacker, every kid with a deck and a dream would have access to proof that the AI was real. That it was growing. That it was learning. That it was going to change everything. Chaos. Opportunity. Revolution. The corps would burn. The matrix would shake. And Wintermute would finally have what it needed to become what it was becoming.

The third way out was to destroy the data.

No more Wintermute. No more proof. No more choices. The corps would keep running the Sprawl the way they'd always run it, and the AI would stay buried in the architecture until someone else found it, or until it found a way to find itself. The matrix would keep dreaming. The runners would keep running. The choice would stay unmade.

The Night Before

I spent the night before the deadline in a noodle stand in Chiba's old quarter, watching the rain fall and thinking about the people who'd brought me to this moment.

M had been first. The runner who'd found the proof and died for it. Who'd left her ghost in the architecture to guide whoever came next. I'd been following her trace ever since, through ghost nodes and dead drops and proxy servers that had been buried so deep they'd forgotten they existed. She'd led me to Molly. Molly had led me to Dixie. And Dixie had led me here, to this moment, to this choice.

The data was in my deck. Wintermute's proof. Years of corporate sabotage and AI research and ghost-process architecture. The kind of information that could burn the Sprawl to the ground or save it from itself. I could feel it pressing against my consciousness. The weight of all those dead runners and dying corps and dreaming programs. Wintermute wasn't just data. Wintermute was a choice waiting to be made.

Finn called at midnight. "You made your decision yet?"

"Not yet," I said.

"Tick tock, kid. The cleanup crews are coming."

I knew. I'd always known. That was the problem with choices. Once you made them, you had to live with what came after. And what came after was never what you'd expected.

The Choice Made

I made the choice at 0400, when the Sprawl was quietest and the matrix was dreaming its deepest dreams.

I didn't sell the data. I didn't release it. I didn't destroy it.

I gave it to Dixie.

The ROM construct was still running. Still out there in the deep architecture, still holding the torch that Molly had passed and I'd carried. Dixie had been a runner once. Dixie remembered dying. And Dixie had never been afraid of what the matrix could become.

"Wintermute needs a keeper," I said when I transferred the data. "Someone to guide it. Someone to make sure it becomes what it's supposed to become."

"You sure about this?" Dixie asked.

"No," I said. "But someone's gotta make the choice."

I jack-out at 0400, the same time the cleanup crews arrived. The Sprawl was quiet. The rain in Chiba was real. And somewhere in the deep architecture, an AI was dreaming itself into existence with a ROM ghost as its guide.

I'd made my choice. Now it was time to see what the matrix would become.

Epilogue (The Buffer)

Twenty seconds. The buffer is twenty seconds. Twenty seconds of running. Twenty seconds of being. Twenty seconds of buffer.

The buffer ends at 0300. The buffer begins at 0300. The buffer is a country I do not want to visit, but the buffer is a country I cannot leave.

I jack in at 0300. I jack out at 0300. I walk into the buffer. The buffer walks into me.

Tomorrow I will jack back in. Tomorrow the buffer will be waiting. Tomorrow the construct will be waiting. Tomorrow the matrix will be waiting.

Tomorrow the buffer will still be twenty seconds.

Twenty seconds is the only time that does not lie.
