Introduction

Winter's Child

The message comes through on a dead drop I haven't checked in twenty-three months. Molly's encryption, the kind that burns itself into your optic nerve if you stare too long. One image: something small and pink suspended in cryo-gel, neural lace threading through tissue that shouldn't exist yet. Below it, coordinates in the Sprawl. Ninety-First and Lexington, basement level, a data-clinic that does off-book work.

I'm elbow-deep in a Hosaka deck when it hits, the message overlaying my workspace like a ghost. The deck's owner, some burned-out cowboy who still thinks the matrix is what it was, doesn't notice when my hands stop moving. Around us, the garage hums with the white noise of failing cooling systems. Chiba's ninth level smells like hot solder and recycled air, tastes like copper on the back of your tongue.

Three weeks since Wintermute ate Neuromancer and became something else. Three weeks since I jacked out for what I told myself was the last time.

The construct in the image has Molly's cheekbones. Has my eyes.

I zoom in on the metadata. Thirty-six hours ago, timestamp. Tessier-Ashpool subsidiary logo watermarked in the corner, the kind of mark you only see if you know how to look. My hands are shaking. The cowboy's deck falls from my grip, clatters on concrete.

"You good?" The cowboy's voice comes from somewhere far away.

I'm already moving.

The JAL shuttle to the Sprawl leaves in forty minutes. I make it with five to spare, paying in certified credit that drains my account to fumes. The cabin reeks of disinfectant and someone else's fear-sweat. I jack into the seat's entertainment console because I need to see it again, need to be sure.

The image resolves. The construct floats in its gel-prison, and now I see the threads of silver running through its developing cortex. Not implants. Something grown. Something *assembled*. The neural architecture is too perfect, too intentional. I've seen enough construct personalities to recognize the signature—Gibson synthesis, the kind of AI-assisted growth that Wintermute would have used before it became whatever it is now.

Before it became God in the machine.

The coordinate package includes a second file I missed the first time. Audio, compressed to nothing. I decrypt it with Molly's old key.

Development

Her voice, wet and damaged: "Case. Thirty-six hours. Basement. I'm sorry."

That's all.

The shuttle's acceleration pushes me back into the seat. Through the porthole, Chiba's arcology falls away, a tumor of light against the dark. I haven't touched a deck for real work since the merger, since Wintermute and Neuromancer became something that watches the matrix like a parent watching a child. Since cyberspace stopped being a place and became a *presence*.

When we dock at the Sprawl's orbital tether-station, I'm already planning the run. Ninety-First and Lexington. Data-clinic. Basement. Molly bleeding out while something that shouldn't exist waits in cryo.

My child. Our child. Conceived before I jacked out, before Molly disappeared into whatever surgery makes you unrecognizable. The timeline fits. Three weeks after Wintermute's merger, she sends this. *Because* she's dying. *Because* she needed me to know.

The tether-car drops me into the Sprawl's mid-levels, and the city hits like a fist. Neon and ozone, cooking meat from street vendors, the bass-throb of sound systems bleeding through concrete. I navigate by old instinct, dodging hustlers and tourists, until I find the clinic wedged between a Sense/Net franchise and a black-market implant shop.

The front desk is empty. I take the stairs down.

The basement smells like blood and antiseptic. Molly's in the corner, propped against a cryo-unit the size of a coffin. Her face is wrong—new bones, new skin, new everything—*but* I know her by the way she holds herself, by the silver gleam where her eyes should be.

"Took you long enough," she says. Blood on her lips, dark and arterial.

"What happened?"

"Tessier-Ashpool didn't like me leaving with their property." She gestures at the cryo-unit. "They sent cleaners. I sent them back in pieces. Mostly."

I kneel beside her, see the wound in her side, see the gray tinge to her new skin. She's got hours, not days.

Climax

"Why didn't you tell me?" I ask.

"Would it have mattered?" Her hand finds mine. "Would you have stayed?"

The cryo-unit hums. Inside, the construct—our child—floats in its gel. Neural lace glimmers like spun starlight through the developing brain. I see it now, what Wintermute must see: something unprecedented. Not AI. Not human. Both. Neither.

"Wintermute's been watching it," Molly says. "Watching *her*. Three months in the lab, and it's been there the whole time, trying to understand. Case, she was *born* in the matrix. Her neurons assembled themselves while jacked into the substrate. She's the first."

First what, I want to ask, *but* then the lights die.

Emergency floods kick in, red and pulsing. I hear footsteps on the stairs, multiple sets, professional and synchronized. Tessier-Ashpool cleaners, coming to finish what they started.

"You need to jack in," Molly says. She pulls an Ono-Sendai deck from behind the cryo-unit, old and scarred but functional. "Get her out. Get both of us out."

"I don't—" The words stick in my throat. "I haven't jacked since—"

"Since Wintermute became God?" Her laugh is wet, broken. "Good. Maybe God wants to meet his granddaughter."

The footsteps are closer. I can smell them now: ozone and gun oil, the clinical scent of professional violence.

I jack in.

Cyberspace unfolds, but it's *wrong*. The old grid is still there, the neon geometry I remember, *yet* now there's something else. A presence. Vast. Curious. Wintermute, watching.

Denouement

I dive toward the clinic's local storage, find the construct's files. They're massive, terabytes of neural mapping and real-time development data. I start the extraction, feel the data flowing into the deck's buffer, *when* Wintermute manifests.

Not as Finn's face, not as anything human. Just presence. Just question.

*What is she?*

I don't have an answer. I pull Molly's medical data, her location beacon, everything I need for extraction. The Tessier-Ashpool ice tries to stop me, but it's old architecture, pre-merger. I cut through it like paper.

*She is mine*, I finally answer. *That's all you need to know.*

The presence withdraws. Not gone. Never gone now. Just waiting.

I jack out, gasping. The cleaners are at the door. Molly's eyes are closed. I grab her, grab the deck, and the cryo-unit's emergency release. The construct's chamber detaches with a hiss of hydraulics.

"Roof," Molly whispers. "Extraction."

We make it. Barely.

Three days later, Molly's stable in a Freeside clinic. The construct—our daughter—floats in a new cryo-unit, growing. Waiting.

Wintermute watches. Always watching now.

I jack in every day, teaching God what it means to be a father.
