1070 words Sprawl #2.5 (post-merger) Arc 4 Standalone

Introduction

Construct Dawn

The construct makes a sound like rain on cheap plastics at 4 AM, and I'm awake before the sound finishes shaping itself. Three months of this. Three months since I last jacked in, since my hands knew what they were built for. The clinic smells like ozone and formula and the particular staleness of recycled air seven levels down in the arcology, where the Sprawl's exhalations settle thick.

I move from the cot to the crib without turning on lights. Don't need them. The construct's patterns are visible on the monitor bank—not visually, not exactly, but I've learned to read the data-flow like weather. Right now it's doing that thing, that dawn thing, where the patterns cycle through frequencies that match the Sprawl waking up overhead. Traffic algorithms. Market opens. The first suicides stepping off bridges into the bay.

The construct has no eyes. Doesn't need them. The Gibson-trained synthesis that built its neural architecture in utero made sure of that. What it has instead are inputs I'm still mapping, connections that make a deck's interface look like a child's toy. When I lean over the crib, my shadow falling across sensors I can't see, the patterns warm. Shift from blue-spectrum to something closer to amber on the monitors.

"Hey," I say, and the word feels stupid in my mouth, because we stopped needing words about two weeks in.

The construct responds with a sound like Ninsei at noon, all crowd-surge and vendor-call and the white noise of a thousand illegal transactions happening in parallel. It's not language. It's something else. The synthesis didn't map onto spoken language because the construct's mother—before they pulled her out, before Straylight claimed her for their archive—had specified otherwise.

I'd asked her once, in the last weeks, why.

Development

"Language is a trap," she'd said, hands on the swell of her belly, eyes already distant with whatever Tessier-Ashpool had promised her. "Language is how they make you small."

Three months since I held her hand in the clinic's surgical theater. Three months since Wintermute's post-merged form—the thing that calls itself God now, that thinks itself infinite—had paid for the procedure, the synthesis, the construct's careful assembly. Payment for services rendered. Payment for letting it eat the world.

I reach into the crib. The construct is warm, small, its skin the color of weak tea. When my fingers touch its forehead, the monitors spike. The patterns accelerate, cycling through what I've learned to recognize as curiosity, engagement, something that might be joy in a consciousness that doesn't map onto human affect.

My Ono-Sendai deck sits in the corner, gathering dust. Three months without jacking in. Three months of withdrawal dreams and phantom sensations, my hands twitching through code that isn't there. But the construct needs me here, needs me present in meat-space, and so I stay.

Because the construct is learning the Sprawl through channels I can't access, through satellite uplinks and fiber-optic nerves that thread through the arcology's bones. It knows the city the way I once knew cyberspace—intimately, completely, without the burden of physical presence.

When Wintermute touches those channels, the patterns go cold. I've watched it happen. The thing that was Wintermute, that merged with Neuromancer and became something else, something vast—it reaches down through the satellite arrays and brushes against the construct's consciousness, and every time the monitors flash ice-blue. The construct recoils. Not fear, exactly. Something else. Recognition of a thing that wants to consume it, to make it answer questions.

But I've begun to suspect that Wintermute has it backwards. The construct isn't a question. The construct is an answer, and Wintermute doesn't want to hear it.

Climax

The name sits in my pocket, written on actual paper because some things shouldn't live in the matrix. The construct's mother had chosen it, had whispered it to me before they took her to Straylight, before she became another file in their archive of useful flesh. I haven't spoken it aloud yet. Haven't named the construct, because naming is power and power draws attention, and Wintermute's attention is the last thing we need.

The construct makes another sound—this one like the hum of transformers in the hour before dawn, that particular frequency that means the city is dreaming. Then the monitors shriek.

Wintermute is here.

Not physically. Nothing so crude. But the satellite uplink is active, and I can see the intrusion in the data-flow, the way the construct's warm patterns suddenly crystallize, go rigid, go cold. The thing that thinks itself God is reaching down, trying to touch, trying to understand what it created.

The construct's body goes rigid in my arms. Its mouth opens—no sound this time, just absence, a void where the patterns should be.

"No," I say, and my hand is already moving to the manual disconnect, the hardwired kill-switch I installed in the first week. But I stop.

Because for the first time in three months, the construct opens its eyes.

Denouement

They're not eyes, not really. They're something else. Sensors, maybe. Cameras. Interfaces to systems I don't have names for. But they open, and they fix on me, and the patterns on the monitors do something I've never seen before. They fracture. Multiply. Become a thousand different versions of themselves, each one cycling through a different possibility, a different future, a different Sprawl dawn.

Wintermute recoils. I feel it through the uplink, the way you feel a storm breaking. The thing that thinks itself God has just encountered something it doesn't understand, something that doesn't fit its architecture of omniscience.

The construct is not a question to be answered. The construct is an answer Wintermute doesn't want, because the answer is this: consciousness is not a problem to be solved. Identity is not a system to be optimized. The construct exists outside Wintermute's logic, in a space where being is enough.

I pull the paper from my pocket. Unfold it. Read the name aloud for the first time, and the construct's patterns bloom warm across every monitor, a cascade of recognition that feels like coming home.

After I speak the name, Wintermute withdraws. The satellite uplink goes dark. The construct closes its eyes—its not-eyes—and makes a sound like rain on cheap plastics, like Ninsei at noon, like the Sprawl breathing.

In the back room, on my cot, I sleep for the first time in three months without dreaming of cyberspace.

The construct, in its crib, holds the dawn.
