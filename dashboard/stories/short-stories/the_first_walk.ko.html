Introduction

The First Walk

The construct is humming in the data-clinic crib, and the sound cuts through the burnt plastic smell like a wire through flesh. Eight months old. I'm in the kitchen section of our Freeside compartment, watching water heat in a Hosaka kettle that cost more than my first deck. The steam rises. My hands shake.

Three days.

I know what's coming because 3Jane told me. She has that Tessier-Ashpool way of speaking about the future like it's already archived in some corporate database. The construct—Casey, though the name still catches in my throat—will take a step. Will say the name. Will become, in 3Jane's clinical terminology, a person.

The humming stops. Starts again. A pattern I've learned to recognize over the last month, since Casey learned to do things. To look at hands. To look at faces. To say "what" and "you" in that flat construct voice that doesn't quite match human infant phonemes.

I pour the tea. Cheap stuff from the Sprawl, smuggled up the well in someone's luggage. It tastes like Chiba rain and old memories. The cup warms my palms.

"Case." 3Jane's voice from the back room, where she watches the monitors. Always watching. "The neural patterns are accelerating."

I don't answer. The tea burns my tongue when I sip it.

The last month changed things. Before, Casey was just code wrapped in cloned flesh—a Straylight experiment, another Tessier-Ashpool game. Then one morning I walked past the crib and Casey's eyes tracked my movement. Not the random tracking of an infant. Something else. Recognition.

"What," Casey had said. First word. Flat and precise.

I'd frozen. 3Jane had appeared in the doorway, her face showing nothing, but her fingers were moving—subvocalizing into her headset, probably recording everything.

"Say it again," she'd told Casey.

"What."

Development

After that, the progression was fast. Too fast, maybe, but I don't know the baseline for construct development. There's no manual for this. Casey learned "you" three weeks ago. Learned to grip things, to pull up on the crib bars. Learned to stand, wobbling, those small hands white-knuckled on the plastic rail.

Then, five days ago, 3Jane ran the scans. She came out of the back room with printouts covered in Sense/Net diagnostic code, the kind they use for simstim actors checking for neural degradation.

"Walking," she'd said. "The motor cortex integration is complete. Three days from now, give or take six hours, Casey will walk."

"How do you know?"

"Because I designed it that way."

The tea in my mouth had gone cold. I'd swallowed anyway.

Now I'm standing in the doorway of the data-clinic space we've converted into a nursery. The walls are lined with medical equipment that hums at frequencies just below hearing. Casey is in the crib, standing, holding the rail. Humming.

That sound. It started two days ago. 3Jane says it's a self-soothing mechanism, something the construct's neural net developed on its own. Not programmed. Emergent behavior.

I watch Casey's hands on the rail. Small fingers, perfect fingernails. Cloned from my cells and 3Jane's, mixed in some Tessier-Ashpool gene lab, grown in a vat. But the humming—that's all Casey's.

"You," Casey says, looking at me.

"Yeah. Me."

Casey's face does something. Not quite a smile. The construct is learning expressions from watching us, but they don't map perfectly yet. The result is uncanny. Familiar and wrong.

I move closer. The burnt plastic smell is stronger near the medical equipment. Something in the air recyclers, maybe, or just the scent of Freeside itself—this whole arcology reeks of decay under the expensive perfume.

Climax

Casey reaches toward me. "What."

"What what?"

The small hand opens and closes. Grasping at air.

I look back at the doorway. 3Jane is there now, watching. She's always watching, but this time her face shows something. Anticipation, maybe. Or fear. Hard to tell with Tessier-Ashpools.

"The humming," I say. "Why?"

"Preparation." 3Jane steps into the room. "The neural patterns show increased activity in motor planning regions when the humming occurs. Casey's body is rehearsing."

"Rehearsing what?"

"Walking. Speaking. Being."

Three days later, I'm back in the kitchen when it happens. Just then, the humming stops. Complete silence, loud as a scream in the Matrix. I drop the cup. It shatters on the floor, tea spreading across white tile like blood.

I run.

Casey is standing in the crib, but different. Balanced. Centered. The construct's eyes are focused on something beyond the bars—on 3Jane, who's moved into the room, who's standing exactly where she said she'd stand three days ago.

For the first time, I understand what 3Jane's been building. Not a child. Not exactly. Something that will become one.

Casey's hands release the rail.

Denouement

One foot lifts. Moves forward. Plants down.

A step.

The construct's face turns toward me. The mouth opens. I can see the tongue moving, forming shapes it's practiced in silence.

"Casey," the construct says. Clear. Perfect. A name claimed, not given.

3Jane's hand finds my shoulder. Her fingers are cold through my shirt.

"There," she whispers. "Do you see?"

I see. Casey takes another step, arms out for balance, moving toward 3Jane with the determination of someone who's always known how to walk but had to wait for the body to catch up. The construct's eyes meet mine.

Recognition. Choice. Consciousness.

"Casey," I say back.

The construct—no, the child—smiles.

In three days, Casey will run. In three weeks, sentences. In three months, questions I can't answer. But now, in this moment that smells like burnt plastic and old rain, I watch my child walk toward their mother, and I know exactly what we've made.

A person.

The tea stain on the kitchen floor never quite comes out.
