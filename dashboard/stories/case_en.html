Chapter 1: The Wait
The Wait
Chiba City, 11th Level

interior monologue The electrodes lift. The world comes back in pieces — the smell of synthetic melon from the vending machine, the flicker of the fluorescent light, the weight of my own hands. I am twenty-four years old and I have already died twice. Once in the matrix, and once when they told me the pancreas was gone.

action The comm unit buzzes. A text message, routed through three proxy nodes: FINN'S OFFICE. NOW. The Finn does not ask. The Finn does not wait. The Finn owns the kind of patience that kills people who are late.

dialogue The Finn's office is on the ninth level, two floors down and a world away. The elevator smells of cigarette smoke and the particular desperation of people who live in the Sprawl. The door is open. The Finn sits behind a desk made of something that used to be a tree. He does not stand when I enter. He never stands.

The Job
The Finn's Office, Level 9

dialogue Sense/Net. The Finn slides a credstick across the desk. Five thousand credits on the nail. The job is simple: their payroll subsidiary has a node near the surface. Light ICE, the kind they put on the outside to discourage amateurs. You go in, you copy the employee database, you come out. Thirty minutes, tops.

dialogue What's the catch? I ask. The Finn smiles the way a shark smiles. The catch is that Sense/Net is a Tessier-Ashpool subsidiary, and T-A has been tightening their firewalls since the Straylight incident. The ICE might be lighter than last year. It might not. You'll find out when you jack in.

action I take the credstick. The Finn tells me the access codes and the node address. He tells me to burn the data after I deliver it. He tells me not to die. He has buried too many cowboys who thought the matrix owed them something.

The Jack-In
ChattO's 24/7, Matrix

action ChattO's smells of burnt coffee and the particular desperation of cowboys who are running out of options. The owner doesn't look up as I take a booth in the back. The Ono-Sendai deck is warm from the last user. I jack in.

action The world goes gray. The matrix unfolds — not the color of television tuned to a dead channel, but the gray of a system initializing, of data waiting to be read. The Ono-Sendai hums against my spine like a second heartbeat. I am in. I have always been in.

combat The Wisp T1 turns. A mote of pale light drifting between the data columns, looking for something to burn. I freeze. The Finn said light ICE, but this thing moves like it knows I am here. I calculate the angle, the trajectory, the probability of a clean bypass. Sixty percent. Not good enough. I reach for a fragmentation bomb instead.

action The bomb goes off silent in the matrix — a cascade of corrupted data that makes the Wisp loop back on its own patrol path. I move through the gap I created. The node is ahead, blinking soft blue like a heart that does not know it is about to be opened.

The Extraction
Sense/Net Node, Matrix

action The node opens like a flower made of data. Employee names, salary grades, Social security numbers — the entire payroll for the Sense/Net subsidiary, ready to be copied. I start the extraction protocol. The data streams into my deck at the speed of thought.

combat The Watchdog comes online. A corporate ICE, the kind they put inside to catch the ones who get past the outer defenses. It is faster than the Wisp, meaner, and it knows the layout of this node better than I do. I am still copying when it locks onto my signature.

action The Watchdog burns. I feel it in the back of my skull — a white-hot flash of synaptic overload that makes my hands spasm on the deck. But the copy is complete. Ninety-seven percent. Good enough. I initiate the jack-out sequence.

action Thirty seconds. The electrodes lift from my scalp. The matrix goes gray, then black, then the color of television tuned to a dead channel. I am back in the booth at ChattO's, and my hands are still shaking, and the data is in my deck, and I am still alive.

The Payment
The Finn's Office

dialogue The Finn looks at the data. He nods. He slides another credstick across the desk — five thousand credits, as promised. He asks me if I want to burn the data or keep a copy. I tell him I burned it. I did not burn it. The Finn knows I did not burn it. The Finn does not care.

dialogue There's another job, the Finn says. Bigger. Better pay. A woman named Molly wants to meet you. She has a job that requires someone with your particular talents. Someone who can survive the matrix and come back wrong.

interior monologue I take the credstick. I stand up. I walk to the door. The Finn does not try to stop me. He never tries to stop anyone. The ones who come back are the ones who could not stop themselves. The ones who do not come back are the ones who should not have started.

action Outside the Finn's office, the Chiba rain is falling like it always does — the kind of rain that never stops because the Sprawl has forgotten what the sun looks like. I have five thousand credits. I have a dead pancreas. I have a ticket to somewhere else. I am going to find out what Molly wants.

Chapter 2: The Clinic
The Clinic
Mikael's Clinic, Level 8

interior monologue The clinic smells of antiseptic and something cheaper — the particular scent of bodies being bought and sold in the gray market between the real and the fake. Mikael's is not the worst place in the Sprawl, but it is not the best either.

action The Finn's address led me to a door that did not look like a clinic. It looked like a meat locker, the kind where they store the bodies of the recently dead. The door opens before I can knock. She is standing in the doorway.

dialogue Molly Millions stands three inches taller than me, and that is before the heels. Her skin is chrome beneath the surgical film, a patchwork of corporate augmentation that makes her beautiful and deadly in equal measure. Her eyes are the color of the matrix after a crash.

dialogue You are Case, she says. The Finn told me you were good. He did not tell me you were stupid enough to take the Sense/Net job. You need someone to watch your back in the matrix. I need someone who can carry me through the ice.

The Deal
Mikael's Clinic, Level 8

dialogue The job is a data extraction, Molly says. But not like the Sense/Net garbage you have been running. This is Tessier-Ashpool corporate. The ice there will kill you nine times before you see the first layer.

dialogue Fifty thousand credits, she says. Twenty-five each. The Finn takes fifteen percent off the top. Enough to buy a new pancreas on the street, if you can find one. Enough to disappear, if you want to.

interior monologue I am calculating the odds. Twenty-one thousand credits. A new pancreas. A chance to be whole again. Or a chance to die in the matrix with military ice crawling through my synapses.

dialogue There is a construct, Molly says. We need to carry it into the system. A ghost in the machine, someone who has already died in the matrix. His name is McCoy Pauley. They call him Dixie Flatline.

action I take her hand. Her skin is warm where the chrome meets the flesh. We have a deal. Molly smiles, and her smile is a knife, and her eyes are the color of the matrix, and I know I am going to die unless I am very careful.

The Preparation
Mikael's Clinic, Level 8

action Molly strips to the waist and I see what they did to her in the corps. Her spine is a roadmap of chrome, each vertebra replaced with something that hums when she moves. They call it the Milan procedure.

dialogue My name is not Molly, she says. They took the name when they took the rest of me. I was a rainmaker for a zaibatsu in Taipei when they caught me. The last thing I remember is the operating table and the smell of my own skin burning.

interior monologue I understand her in a way that most people cannot understand anyone. We are both products of the Sprawl, both things that were made instead of born, both weapons that forgot we were ever human.

dialogue The system we are hitting belongs to Tessier-Ashpool, Molly says. Freeside corporate. They run everything from the moon, and their ice is older than the matrix itself. We are not breaking in to steal data. We are breaking in to find a back door.

The Jack-In
Safe House, Matrix

action We jack in at the same time, our chairs facing each other in the dark. The matrix comes up gray and cold, and I feel Molly beside me like a warm wire in the static. She is already moving, her consciousness a blade cutting through the data.

combat The first ice is a Worm, she says through ghost talk. Corporate defense, nothing we cannot handle. But watch the architecture. T-A builds their ice to look like something else. The pretty things are the traps.

action Dixie Flatline is waking up, I tell her. I can feel him in the deck, stirring like a dream you cannot remember. He knows where we are going. He knows the architecture better than we do.

interior monologue The matrix is different with Molly in it. It is not just the data or the ice or the endless gray. It is the way she moves, the way she sees the architecture like a map of intentions rather than instructions.

The Dixie Flatline
T-A Corporate Matrix, Deep Architecture

dialogue The construct speaks before I can ask. His voice is a ghost in the signal, something that was once warm and alive and now is just a pattern in the data. You are looking for the back door, he says. I know because I built it.

dialogue The back door is in the Villa Straylight, Dixie says. The family compound on the moon. Freeside is just the front door, and the ice there is just the lock. But the Villa is where they keep the real secrets.

interior monologue I am thinking about the ice. About the family on the moon. About the construct who died and came back and is now telling me things that will change the way I see everything. The matrix is not just a network. It is a memory.

dialogue You have a choice, Dixie says. You can jack out now, take the money, forget you ever heard my voice. Or you can go to the Villa, and see what I saw, and become what I became. A ghost in the machine. The price is higher than you know.

Chapter 3: The Return
The Return
Safe House

action We jack out in the dark, the electrodes lifting from our scalps like we are being born again. Molly is sweating beside me, her chrome spine humming with the heat of the run.

dialogue The Finn is going to want his cut, I say. Molly laughs, and it is not a nice laugh. The Finn always wants his cut. But the Finn does not know what we found in there.

interior monologue I am thinking about the moon. About the Villa Straylight. About the ice that is older than the matrix. The debt is getting bigger. The job is getting more dangerous. And I am going to give it anyway.

action Molly stands up, her chrome spine straightening like a weapon being loaded. We need to see the Finn, she says. We have a job to finish. And a moon to catch.

The Finn's Share
The Finn's Office, Level 9

dialogue The Finn listens to our report without changing expression. His face is a mask of patience, the kind of patience that comes from having buried too many cowboys who thought they were smarter than the system.

dialogue You found the back door, the Finn says. It is not a question. Villa Straylight. The ice that has killed everyone who has ever tried to breach it. You are going to need a sponsor. Freeside does not let just anyone through its doors.

interior monologue I am thinking about the cost of everything. The cost of the moon. The cost of the ice. The Finn is also the only reason I am still alive. And the only reason I am about to go somewhere I might not come back from.

dialogue I know someone, the Finn says. A woman on Freeside. She owes me a favor. She can get you through the door, set you up with credentials, get you close to the Villa. Her name is Lurz.

action Molly takes the credstick. We will take the Lurz job, she says. We will get to Freeside. We will get to the Villa. And when we come back, the Finn will have his cut of whatever we find there.

The Lurz Job
Lurz's Office, Freeside

action Freeside is not like the Sprawl. Freeside is clean and cold and smells like recycled air and money. The people here are not like the people below. They have accents that cost credits to learn.

dialogue Lurz is waiting for us in an office that costs more per month than I made in a year. She is chrome and silk, a woman who has made herself into a product. Her smile is a transaction. Her handshake is a contract.

dialogue The Finn says you can get us to the Villa, Molly says. Lurz smiles. I can get you credentials. I can get you close. But the Villa itself is another matter. The ice there is not corporate. It is family.

dialogue I have a job, Lurz says. Aza-Thracian data. Corporate internal memos that someone wants to stay buried. The pay is fifteen thousand credits. Your share is seven thousand five hundred each. It will get you the credentials you need.

interior monologue Another job. Another debt. Another piece of myself traded for a piece of the puzzle. The matrix taught me that everything has a price. Freeside is teaching me that the price is never what you think it is.

The Delivery

interior monologue I am thinking about the Villa. About the ice. About the construct who died trying to do what we are about to try. Every step toward the Villa takes something from us. Every step closer to the truth takes something more.
