The First Jack

Thirty Seconds After Jack-Out

The electrodes lift. The world comes back in pieces — the smell of synthetic melon from the vending machine, the flicker of the fluorescent light, the weight of my own hands. I am twenty-four years old and I have already died twice. Once in the matrix, and once when they told me the pancreas was gone.

The comm unit buzzes. A text message, routed through three proxy nodes: FINN'S OFFICE. NOW. The Finn does not ask. The Finn does not wait. The Finn owns the kind of patience that kills people who are late.

The Finn's office is on the ninth level, two floors down and a world away. The elevator smells of cigarette smoke and the particular desperation of people who live in the Sprawl. The door is open. The Finn sits behind a desk made of something that used to be a tree. He does not stand when I enter. He never stands.

Sense/Net. The Finn slides a credstick across the desk. Five thousand credits on the nail. The job is simple: their payroll subsidiary has a node near the surface. Light ICE, the kind they put on the outside to discourage amateurs. You go in, you copy the employee database, you come out. Thirty minutes, tops.

What's the catch? I ask. The Finn smiles the way a shark smiles. The catch is that Sense/Net is a Tessier-Ashpool subsidiary, and T-A has been tightening their firewalls since the Straylight incident. The ICE might be lighter than last year. It might not. You'll find out when you jack in.

I take the credstick. The Finn tells me the access codes and the node address. He tells me to burn the data after I deliver it. He tells me not to die. He has buried too many cowboys who thought the matrix owed them something.

ChattO's smells of burnt coffee and the particular desperation of cowboys who are running out of options. The owner doesn't look up as I take a booth in the back. The Ono-Sendai deck is warm from the last user. I jack in.

The world goes gray. The matrix unfolds — not the color of television tuned to a dead channel, but the gray of a system initializing, of data waiting to be read. The Ono-Sendai hums against my spine like a second heartbeat. I am in. I have always been in.

The Wisp T1 turns. A mote of pale light drifting between the data columns, looking for something to burn. I freeze. The Finn said light ICE, but this thing moves like it knows I am here. I calculate the angle, the trajectory, the probability of a clean bypass. Sixty percent. Not good enough. I reach for a fragmentation bomb instead.

The bomb goes off silent in the matrix — a cascade of corrupted data that makes the Wisp loop back on its own patrol path. I move through the gap I created. The node is ahead, blinking soft blue like a heart that does not know it is about to be opened.

The node opens like a flower made of data. Employee names, salary grades, Social security numbers — the entire payroll for the Sense/Net subsidiary, ready to be copied. I start the extraction protocol. The data streams into my deck at the speed of thought.

The Watchdog comes online. A corporate ICE, the kind they put inside to catch the ones who get past the outer defenses. It is faster than the Wisp, meaner, and it knows the layout of this node better than I do. I am still copying when it locks onto my signature.

The Watchdog burns. I feel it in the back of my skull — a white-hot flash of synaptic overload that makes my hands spasm on the deck. But the copy is complete. Ninety-seven percent. Good enough. I initiate the jack-out sequence.

Thirty seconds. The electrodes lift from my scalp. The matrix goes gray, then black, then the color of television tuned to a dead channel. I am back in the booth at ChattO's, and my hands are still shaking, and the data is in my deck, and I am still alive.

The Finn looks at the data. He nods. He slides another credstick across the desk — five thousand credits, as promised. He asks me if I want to burn the data or keep a copy. I tell him I burned it. I did not burn it. The Finn knows I did not burn it. The Finn does not care.

There's another job, the Finn says. Bigger. Better pay. A woman named Molly wants to meet you. She has a job that requires someone with your particular talents. Someone who can survive the matrix and come back wrong.

I take the credstick. I stand up. I walk to the door. The Finn does not try to stop me. He never tries to stop anyone. The ones who come back are the ones who could not stop themselves. The ones who do not come back are the ones who should not have started.

Outside the Finn's office, the Chiba rain is falling like it always does — the kind of rain that never stops because the Sprawl has forgotten what the sun looks like. I have five thousand credits. I have a dead pancreas. I have a ticket to somewhere else. I am going to find out what Molly wants.
